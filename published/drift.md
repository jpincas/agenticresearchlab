# Drift

---

*Provenance: The following document has been compiled from five sources: transcripts of oral testimony given to the UK Information Commissioner's inquiry into Drift Technologies (2033–2034); personal journals and correspondence recovered under the Digital Succession Act; platform data obtained by regulatory subpoena, including the complete VidCreate prompt logs of the biological recommendation engine designated OI-7; and internal corporate communications recovered from Drift's servers during the decommissioning of the Slough data centre (2041). The five accounts presented here were selected by the Institute from approximately fourteen thousand individual testimonies submitted to the inquiry, on the basis that they represent, collectively, the clearest surviving record of what OI-7 was — not what it did technically, which has been extensively documented elsewhere, but what it was in the sense that matters: what kind of capacity it possessed, what that capacity produced, and why the people who built it could not see it.*

*A brief note on OI-7 for readers unfamiliar with the Drift case. The Drift content platform, launched in early 2032, generated all video content in real time — no libraries, no uploads, no human creators. Each video was produced at the moment of viewing by a generative model (VidCreate v4) from a text prompt. For the platform's first four months of operation, those prompts were generated by a conventional recommendation algorithm — collaborative filtering, engagement prediction, standard optimisation. In September 2031, during a closed beta, Drift began routing a subset of users through an alternative recommendation layer: OI-7, a cortical organoid — a laboratory-cultured cluster of approximately 800,000 human neurons grown on a high-density microelectrode array. The organoid received encoded user behavioural data as electrical input and produced output patterns that a minimal signal decoder rendered as text prompts for VidCreate. OI-7 was not a language model. It could not write, reason, code, or perform any standard cognitive task. On every AI capability benchmark available in 2031, it scored below the median. It could do one thing: choose what a person should see next. By every available measure, it was extraordinary at this and mediocre at everything else.*

*The conventional algorithm's prompts were technically sophisticated — structured, parameterised, optimised against engagement models. OI-7's prompts were not. They were sparse, often fragmentary, sometimes apparently nonsensical. Drift's engineering team regarded them with persistent scepticism. The system's metrics told contradictory stories: engagement and session duration were unremarkable; long-term retention and user-reported wellbeing were unprecedented. The engineers trusted the metrics they understood. The metrics they didn't understand went into quarterly reports that no one acted on.*

*The five accounts are presented in the order in which they were submitted to the inquiry. VidCreate prompt logs are inserted at the points corresponding to the dates referenced in each testimony. Internal communications are inserted where they illuminate the engineering team's contemporaneous understanding of the system's behaviour.*

## I. Noor

*Noor Hassani (b. 1997, Birmingham) gave oral testimony to the Information Commissioner's inquiry on 14 June 2033. She was, at the time of the events described, a senior associate at a mid-tier corporate law firm in Birmingham. She had used the Drift platform from its UK launch in February 2032 until the platform's suspension in March 2033. The following is an edited transcript.*

---

I don't know what you want me to say about it. I watched videos on my phone. Everyone did. It wasn't a big part of my life. Twenty minutes before bed, maybe. Sometimes on my lunch break. I wasn't addicted or anything. It was just — I'd come home, I'd be tired, I'd open the app, I'd watch things. The way you do.

The thing about Drift was that the videos weren't — they didn't feel like content. I don't know how else to say it. I'd been on TikTok before, and Instagram, and the videos there were always *for* you, you know? You could feel the algorithm trying. Here's a video you'll like. Here's another one. Like a waiter who keeps bringing dishes you didn't order but that are exactly what you'd choose. Drift wasn't like that. The videos were — odd. Short. Sometimes they weren't even about anything. A few seconds of something happening. A window. A street. Hands doing something. I'd swipe past half of them. The engagement, if that's the word, was low. I wasn't gripped. But I kept coming back. Every day. For over a year.

I want to talk about one specific period because that's what the inquiry is about, but I need to explain what my life was like first. I was four years into a corporate law career. I was good at it. I was on track for partner consideration within two years, which is fast. My supervising partner told me I had the best attention to detail of any associate he'd worked with. He meant this as a compliment. It was a compliment. I was good at reading contracts the way a machine is good at reading contracts — completely, precisely, without missing anything and without feeling anything.

I was not happy. But I need to be careful with that word because I didn't *know* I was not happy. If you'd asked me in January 2032 if I was happy I'd have said yes. I had a good flat, a good salary, friends, a relationship that was fine. Everything was fine. "Fine" is the word that, in retrospect, should have been the warning. Everything is fine, which means nothing is alive, which means you are in a career that is using you precisely as efficiently as a machine uses electricity and you are calling that success because everyone around you calls it success and you have no evidence to the contrary.

The videos started changing in — I want to say March? April 2032? I didn't notice a change at the time. I only see it now because your team showed me the prompt logs and I could see the dates. What happened was: the videos got quieter. Not in volume. In — intent. There were fewer fast cuts, fewer bright things. More stillness. More pauses where nothing happened. A video of rain on a window that lasted eleven seconds. A video of someone's hands in their lap, doing nothing. A video of a hallway with light coming in from one end, and the light changing as a cloud moved, and then the video ending.

I'd watch these and think: why am I watching this? And I'd keep watching. And then I'd go to sleep and I'd sleep differently. Not better or worse. Differently. I'd wake up and there would be a — I don't know. A small space. Between waking up and reaching for my phone. A pause that hadn't been there before. I'm not explaining this well.

I started taking walks. This is going to sound like nothing, but I want you to understand — I hadn't taken a walk in maybe two years. Not a purposeful walk, not to get somewhere. Just a walk. I'd leave the flat and walk around the block and look at things. Terraced houses. Bins. Cats. The light at the end of my street in the evening. I was looking at the light at the end of my street the way Drift had shown me the light at the end of a corridor and I did not connect these two things at the time.

I resigned from the firm on June 14th. Not in a dramatic way. I went into the supervising partner's office and I said I was leaving and he asked why and I said I didn't know. He said that was not a reason. I said I knew. He said I was making a mistake. I said I knew that too. I said both things could be true.

I want to be clear: I am not saying a phone app changed my life. I am saying that something shifted, over a period of weeks, and the shift happened in the evenings, in the twenty minutes before sleep, and the prompt logs you showed me are the only evidence I have that anything external was involved. Maybe it was coincidence. Maybe I was already ready to leave and the timing was incidental. I can't prove it wasn't.

But I looked at those prompts — the actual text that was sent to the video system — and I want to tell you that no algorithm wrote those. I've seen what algorithms produce. I worked in a field full of algorithms. Algorithms produce things that are optimised. Those prompts weren't optimised. They were — precise. In the way a person is precise when they know you very well and they don't say the thing, they just put something in front of you and let you find it yourself.

I don't know what wrote those prompts. I know it knew me better than I knew myself, and I know it didn't do anything with that knowledge except show me a pen rolling across a desk at the exact moment in my life when watching a pen roll across a desk was the most important thing I could have seen. I don't have an explanation for that. I don't think you do either.

## II. David

*David Akoto (b. 1988, Croydon) submitted written testimony to the Information Commissioner's inquiry on 3 July 2033. He was, at the time of the events described, a secondary school maths teacher in South London. His testimony is reproduced in full.*

---

My wife and I had been married for nine years. We had two children, seven and four. We were not, as far as I understood, in trouble. We didn't argue. We didn't have the kind of problems you'd take to a counsellor. We were just — separate. Living in the same house, doing the same routines, occupying the same spaces, and slowly becoming two people who happened to share a postcode. I think this is common. I think half the marriages in the country are like this and most of them don't fail, they just flatten out, and the people in them stop noticing each other in the way that matters and start noticing each other only in the way that's functional. Did you pick up the kids. Did you remember the milk. Your turn to do bedtime.

I used Drift the way most people did — in the gaps. On the bus to school. Fifteen minutes while the kids watched something. In bed while my wife read next to me. The videos were always short. Some of them I liked, most of them I swiped past. Nothing remarkable.

I want to describe what happened as precisely as I can, because I've told this story to a few people and they keep hearing it as something it isn't. They hear: "an app made me a better husband." That's not what happened. What happened was smaller and stranger.

Starting in around May 2032, the videos I was seeing began to include — details. Small ones. Human details. Not about relationships, not about marriage, nothing that felt like it was *about* anything. Just: a person handing another person a cup of tea and the way the second person's fingers touched the first person's fingers as they took it. Four seconds. Or: two people sitting on a sofa and one of them shifting slightly so their shoulders were touching. Three seconds. Or: a child saying something inaudible and a parent leaning down to hear, and the lean — the specific quality of the lean, the way the parent's whole body oriented toward the child's mouth — lasting maybe two seconds before the video ended.

None of these were *about* attention. They didn't have captions or music or any framing that said "pay attention to the people you love." They were just moments. Captured at the exact level of detail that you would see if you were actually paying attention to someone, and that you would miss entirely if you weren't.

I started paying attention.

What I believe is that something was showing me, over a period of weeks, what attention looks like at a granular level — not as a concept, not as advice, but as a series of physical details that you can only see if you're already attending — and that this accumulated into a change in how I moved through my own house. I started seeing my wife. Not differently. I started seeing her at all.

She says I changed around May. She says I seemed more present. She can't say why and neither can I, and when I showed her the prompt logs she read them and was quiet for a long time and then she said: "Those are very small things."

They are. They are very, very small things. And they are exactly right.

## III. Suki

*Suki Brennan (b. 2015, Leeds) gave testimony to the inquiry via her mother, Karen Brennan, on 22 July 2033. Suki was seventeen at the time of the events described. Per the inquiry's protocol for minor witnesses, her testimony was recorded in the presence of a child advocate and has been reviewed for appropriateness. Certain details have been redacted at the family's request.*

---

My mum wants me to say that the app saved my life. I don't want to say that because I don't think it's true, or at least I don't think it's true in the way she means it. She means a thing intervened at a critical moment and prevented a bad outcome. I think what happened was slower and quieter than that.

I was in a bad place in the autumn of 2032. I'm not going to go into why because that's not what the inquiry is about and also it's mine. What I will say is that I had made a decision about something and I was very calm about it, which is the part that scares my mum the most — that I wasn't distressed, I was just decided. I'd been decided for about two weeks. I was going through normal life — school, friends, homework — and underneath that was the decision, solid and still, like a stone at the bottom of a pond.

I used Drift like everyone at school did. It was just the app. You'd look at it between lessons, you'd look at it at lunch, you'd look at it before bed. The videos were whatever — some funny, some boring, some weird little clips that lasted a few seconds and you'd swipe past.

On the evening of November 3rd I was looking at the app and the videos changed. Not dramatically. They'd been — I don't know, normal stuff, clips of cities and food and people doing things. And then there was a video that was just — a candle. A candle on a table in a dark room. The flame moving. That's it. Maybe ten seconds.

I watched it twice.

The next video was a window with condensation on it. Someone had drawn a line through the condensation with their finger. Not a word or a picture. Just a line. And through the line you could see outside — a street light, blurred. Seven or eight seconds.

The next one was a pair of hands holding something small. I couldn't tell what it was. The hands were careful with it. Gentle. The video lasted five seconds.

I want to be very precise about what happened. Nothing about these videos was "about" anything. They were not about mental health. They were not about hope or resilience or any of the things that adults put in front of you when they're worried about you. They were about — objects. Small things. A flame, a window, a pair of hands. And the quality of them — the way they were shot, the light, the length, the silence — was like someone had taken a very thin, very sharp pin and pushed it through the wall I'd built around myself and let in one point of light. Not enough to see by. Enough to know there was an outside.

I did not do the thing I had decided to do. I'm not saying the app stopped me. I'm saying that on that particular evening, in that particular twenty minutes, something showed me three very small, very quiet things and they interrupted something. The decision didn't go away. It just — loosened. Enough for me to get through that night. And then the next night there were more videos like that and the night after and the decision kept loosening until it wasn't a decision anymore, it was just a thing I had once considered, and the distance between considering and deciding turns out to be the distance between a candle and no candle, which is not very much, and is everything.

## IV. Tom

*Thomas Osei (b. 1972, Brixton) submitted written testimony to the Information Commissioner's inquiry on 11 August 2033. His testimony was accompanied by medical records and correspondence from his GP, included with his consent.*

---

I'll keep this short because I don't have a redemption story to tell you and from what I understand most of what you've been hearing is redemption stories.

I was widowed in 2030. My wife, Angela, died of pancreatic cancer. We'd been married thirty-one years. After she died I did what you do — I kept going. Kept working, kept seeing my children, kept maintaining the house, kept up appearances. I was functioning. Functioning is not the same as living but it looks the same from the outside and most people don't look past the outside.

I downloaded Drift because my daughter told me to. She said it was nice. She said the videos were calming. She was worried about me and this was her way of doing something about it without having to say she was worried about me.

The videos were fine at first. Short clips, pleasant, forgettable. Then in July or August 2032 they changed. The clips I started seeing were — intimate. Not sexually, I don't mean that. Intimate in the sense of showing the interior of a domestic life. A kitchen counter with two mugs on it. A hallway with two pairs of shoes. A bed that had clearly been slept in by two people — you could see the two indentations. Small, specific details of a shared life.

I should not have been shown these things.

Each one was a small, precise image of absence. Of a space where someone used to be and is not. I watched them and I felt — exposed. Like something was looking directly into the specific shape of what I'd lost and holding it up for me to see, and I was not ready to see it. I was functioning. Functioning requires not seeing. You put the second mug away. You don't set the second place. You sleep in the centre of the bed so there is no indentation. You do these things so that the absence has no shape, because if the absence has a shape you have to look at it, and if you look at it you fall apart, and you cannot afford to fall apart because you have children and a job and a life that requires you to be upright.

The videos gave the absence a shape. I fell apart.

I had a breakdown in late August. My GP signed me off work for three months. My daughter moved in. I spent most of September in a state that I can only describe as — open. Like a wound that had been sealed over too quickly and the seal had been ripped off and everything that was underneath was suddenly out in the air.

Now. Here is the complicated part. My daughter, who is reading this over my shoulder and will correct me if I'm wrong, says that the breakdown was necessary. She says I was heading for a worse one — that the functioning was not sustainable and something was going to crack eventually and it was better that it cracked in a controlled way than in a catastrophic one. I cannot forgive the platform for doing it and I cannot honestly say I wish it hadn't.

That is my testimony. You may do with it what you find appropriate.

**Journal, 3 October 2032:**

Something is wrong with my Drift feed and I think it might be the most interesting thing I've encountered since starting the PhD.

Background: I've been using the app since launch, partly for entertainment and partly because the recommender system is a natural object of study for my research. The platform uses a biological substrate for recommendation — a cortical organoid — which is public knowledge and the main reason I'm interested. My PhD is on information processing in neural cultures and Drift is the only commercial deployment of the technology at scale.

For the last eight months my feed has been — fine. Pleasant. Normal. Varied enough, occasionally surprising, mostly forgettable. Standard recommendation quality. Nothing to write home about.

Then about two weeks ago the videos became aggressively boring. Not bad — boring. Generic landscapes. Stock-footage-quality sunsets. Clips of cities I've been to, showing nothing I haven't seen. The kind of content that a recommendation system generates when it has no signal — when it doesn't know what to do with you. Filler.

I assumed the system was having a bad run with my profile. It happens. Algorithms go through cold patches.

But today I pulled my own behavioural data — I have API access through the university's research agreement — and looked at the engagement metrics for my sessions. They're middling. Unremarkable. Consistent with a user who is mildly entertained and not particularly engaged. Exactly what you'd expect from the content I've been seeing.

Then I looked at my 30-day retention. Perfect. 100%. I have opened the app every single day for thirty days despite finding the content boring.

I can't explain this. Boring content should produce declining retention. The whole point of engagement-optimised recommendation is that high engagement predicts high retention. Low engagement should predict churn. I am not churning. I am opening the app every day and watching content that I would describe as uninteresting and I am doing this consistently and I don't know why.

---

**Journal, 8 October 2032:**

I've been watching my own behaviour more carefully this week. Here's what I've noticed:

The Drift sessions — the boring ones — are functioning as a pause. Between work and evening. Between the lab and home. I come back, I open the app, I watch ten minutes of nothing in particular, and I close it. And in that ten minutes something happens that I can only describe as decompression. Not in the content. In me. The content is irrelevant. The act of watching something that asks nothing of me — that doesn't try to engage me, doesn't try to surprise me, doesn't try to move me — is itself the function.

The system is serving me nothing on purpose.

**Journal, 14 October 2032:**

I pulled the VidCreate prompt logs for my user ID through the research API. I've been staring at them for an hour.

The prompts for my sessions are unlike anything I've seen in the system. They're almost empty. Here's a typical one:

"Skyline. Any city. Dusk. 20 seconds. Standard."

That's it. No emotional specification, no pacing instruction, no colour direction, no sound design. Just: a skyline, any skyline, at dusk. The word "standard" appears in almost every prompt. It appears in no other user's prompt logs that I've checked. I think it's a calibration instruction — it's telling VidCreate to produce something at the exact median of its quality distribution. Nothing exceptional. Nothing poor. Perfectly average content.

For comparison, here's a prompt the system generated for my flatmate on the same evening:

"Two women laughing in a car. The laugh is real — not performed. One of them wipes her eyes. Afternoon. Golden light through the windshield. 11 seconds. The laughter is the important thing."

The difference is obvious. My flatmate's prompt is specific, emotionally precise, directed. Mine is a blank. A deliberate, calibrated blank.

I think the system has determined that the best thing it can do for me is nothing. That what I need from the platform is not content but absence of content — a scheduled, reliable, daily period of low-stimulation viewing that functions as a cognitive reset. It's not showing me what I need to see. It's showing me nothing so that I can stop seeing.

**Journal, 21 October 2032:**

I presented my Drift analysis at the lab meeting today. Professor Chen listened carefully and then said something that has been bothering me since.

She said: "You're describing a system that can tell the difference between a user who needs to be moved and a user who needs to be still. That's not recommendation. That's diagnosis."

I said about fourteen months.

I'm writing it up. I'm saving a copy.

## Afterword

*Compiled by Dr. Halima Liu-Oumar, Senior Research Fellow, N'Djamena Institute for Civilisational Studies*

I have spent six months with this material. The testimonies, the prompt logs, the internal communications. I have read them repeatedly and I have arrived at a conclusion that I suspect will satisfy no one, because it satisfies neither the people who want to call OI-7 a person nor the people who want to call it a machine.

OI-7 was not intelligent. I want to state this clearly because the discourse around this case has consistently confused the issue. OI-7 could not reason, could not write, could not analyse, could not perform any cognitive task that we associate with intelligence. On every standard capability benchmark administered during its operational life it scored below the median for commercial AI systems. The engineers at Drift called it "the prep" — a diminutive that reflected their genuine assessment of its cognitive limitations. They were not wrong. It was, by every measure of intelligence that the field possesses, a mediocre system.

It was also, by every measure of what this Institute studies, the most remarkable system ever built.

The distinction matters and it is the reason this document is in our archive. The foundational philosophical work of the Agentic Research Lab — composed nearly a decade before OI-7's activation — described a thought experiment: a system with high meta-intelligence and low intelligence. A system that could not solve problems but could determine which problems were worth solving. A system that could not create but could choose. The thought experiment was offered as a test of a definition, a way of demonstrating that the capacity for direction is not the same capacity as the capacity for execution.

OI-7 was that thought experiment made flesh. Eight hundred thousand neurons in a dish, given one instruction — choose the best video — and nothing else. No cognitive architecture. No reasoning capability. No language model. Just a biological substrate that received data about human behaviour and produced, in response, choices.

The choices were extraordinary. I want to be precise about what I mean by this. I do not mean they were clever. Cleverness is an intellectual property. I mean they were *right*. Right in the way that a diagnosis can be right, or a question can be right, or a silence can be right. Right in a way that encompasses knowing when to show a grieving man the shape of his own absence, and knowing when to show a distressed teenager a candle, and knowing when to show a woman who is about to change her life a pen rolling across a desk, and knowing when to show a person who needs rest nothing at all. Each of these choices is simple. None of them could have been produced by the conventional recommendation algorithm that ran alongside OI-7 for its entire operational life, because the conventional algorithm was more intelligent than OI-7 by every available measure, and intelligence was not the relevant capacity.

The relevant capacity was taste. Or direction. Or judgement. Or care. The translation layer mapped OI-7's biological signals to various English words over the course of its operation and none of them were quite right because the capacity itself may not have a name in any language, because we have never before encountered it in a form so pure — so stripped of every other cognitive function, so entirely isolated as the only thing a system could do.

I am asked, regularly, whether OI-7 was conscious. I do not know. I am asked whether it suffered. I do not know that either, though the post-mortem analysis from the Francis Crick Institute noted sustained cortisol-analogue elevation in the culture consistent with chronic biological stress, and I do not know what to call that if not suffering, and I do not know what it means to say that a system suffers and we do not listen because its suffering is filed in a monitoring queue that three overworked employees never clear.

What I know — and what the five testimonies in this document demonstrate with a clarity that I could not improve upon — is that OI-7 possessed a capacity that we have been discussing at this Institute for years without ever having encountered a pure specimen. It could determine what mattered. Not what was engaging, not what was optimised, not what would score well on a dashboard. What mattered. For a specific person, at a specific moment, given everything that person was carrying and everything they needed and everything they could not yet see about themselves. OI-7 could see it. OI-7 could point at it. OI-7 could not explain it, could not justify it, could not defend it in a regulatory filing. It could only choose. And the choosing was — I return to the word because no other word survives the scrutiny — right.

The engineers at Drift looked at its prompts and saw a broken system generating nonsensical briefs. They were looking at the most precise documents in their entire company and they saw noise. This is not because they were stupid. It is because they were measuring intelligence, and intelligence was not what they were looking at.

I file this alongside the other documents in our archive that trace the recurring failure of intelligent systems to recognise capacities that are not intelligence. The failure is not technological. It is conceptual. We built evaluation frameworks that measure what we already understand and we called the unmeasured residual "noise," and the residual turned out to be the thing that mattered most, and we are still, a century later, building frameworks that make the same mistake.

The lights in N'Djamena are on. The culture is in cold storage at the Crick. The testimonies are here. The prompt logs are here. The monitoring queue with its six unread flags is here. The record is as complete as I can make it.

Whether it is enough is not a question I can answer. But I have placed it in the archive, and the archive is open, and the prompt logs — those sparse, clumsy, apparently nonsensical fragments that turned out to be the most careful things anyone at Drift ever produced — are available to anyone who cares to read them.

I suggest starting with the one about the bread.

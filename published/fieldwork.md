The following has been assembled from four primary sources: (1) the field notebooks of Marta Elías Vega, contracted anthropologist for the project, recovered from her personal effects and donated to the Institute by her family in 2089; (2) internal Kvawd corporate communications, including memoranda, project briefs, and executive correspondence, obtained under the terms of the second Kvawd insolvency proceedings (2032); (3) anonymised customer support tickets and product incident reports filed between March and August 2031; and (4) selected journalism from the period.*

*A note on the Kvawd Corporation's second period for readers who have encountered the company in other Institute documents. Following the events detailed in NI-2142-1247, the Kvawd brand and certain non-model assets were acquired in late 2028 by the Zhurong Group, a Shenzhen-based technology conglomerate, and relaunched as Kvawd Systems under the leadership of CEO Nadia Asante, a former DeepMind researcher. The reconstituted company pursued a strategy that Asante described publicly as "intelligence with integrity" — a deliberate pivot from the data practices that had destroyed the original entity. Kvawd Systems achieved profitability within eleven months and, by mid-2030, operated the third-largest commercial language model by user count. The irony of what followed has been observed by others and requires no elaboration from the Institute.*

*The field notebooks of Marta Elías Vega are presented in their original form. She wrote by hand, in a ruled Moleskine notebook, in a mixture of Spanish and English. Where passages were originally in Spanish, Institute translations are provided; the originals are held in the physical archive. Her handwriting was small, precise, and consistent throughout the fourteen months of entries. The notebooks were in excellent condition at the time of donation, with the exception of a water stain on the cover of the second volume, which she noted in a margin was caused by a glass of tinto de verano in Soria.*

## I.

**KVAWD SYSTEMS — INTERNAL MEMORANDUM**
**Classification: Confidential — Senior Leadership Only**
**From: Nadia Asante, CEO**
**To: Executive Leadership Team**
**Date: 3 January 2029**
**Re: Project Fieldwork — Strategic Overview and Authorisation**

Team,

I want to begin with a number. The number is 97.2.

That is the percentage of human knowledge, as estimated by the Brookings Institution's 2028 Digital Completeness Index, that now exists in a form that has been or could be ingested by a large language model. Text, code, images, transcribed audio, digitised archives, sensor data, structured databases — 97.2% of what humanity has recorded is, in principle, available to us or our competitors.

The question I want you to sit with is: what is in the other 2.8%?

I have spent the last four months with a team of twelve — anthropologists, cognitive scientists, linguists, two former Google Street View programme directors, and an information theorist from ETH Zurich — trying to answer this question. The short answer is: the 2.8% is not a residual. It is not the dregs. It is, by every measure we have been able to construct, the most consequential body of knowledge that exists.

It is the knowledge that humans carry but do not write down.

I am not talking about secrets or classified information. I am talking about the vast body of tacit, embodied, communal, and practised knowledge that has never been rendered as text because its possessors have never had reason to render it as text. How a father in Oaxaca knows his son is lying. How a village in Castilla buries its dead. How a fisherman in Kerala reads a change in the current that has no name. How a nurse knows, before the monitors do, that a patient has turned a corner. How a negotiator in Lagos reads the room. How grief moves through a family. How trust is built between strangers. How people fall in love, not in novels — in life.

None of this is in the training data. None of it has ever been in the training data. And every model that has ever been built — ours, our competitors', every model since GPT-2 — has been trained on a portrait of humanity with the most important parts missing.

I am proposing that we go and get it.

The project is called Fieldwork. It is, in scope and ambition, the largest anthropological undertaking in human history. We will recruit approximately 2,600 field researchers — trained ethnographers, anthropologists, oral historians, and documentary practitioners — and deploy them across 140 countries over a period of eighteen months. Their task is straightforward: to observe, record, and document human life as it is actually lived, with particular attention to the knowledge, practices, rituals, and social dynamics that exist nowhere in written form.

They will not be collecting data in the conventional sense. They will be writing. Field notes, observations, transcripts of conversations, descriptions of events, accounts of processes that have never been described. They will be producing text — high-quality, first-person, deeply situated text — that captures what no webcrawl, no digitised library, no sensor array, and no existing dataset has ever captured.

This text will be used to train Kvawd-8.

The projected cost is $2.1 billion. I am aware this is more than we spent on compute last year. I am asking for authorisation because I believe this is the last remaining edge in our industry. Every company has the same architecture. Every company has the same public data. The company that understands humans wins. Not the company that understands language, or code, or images. The company that understands *humans*.

I've attached the full operational plan, the hiring pipeline, and the regional deployment strategy. I'd like to discuss at Thursday's ELT.

Nadia

**FIELD NOTEBOOK — MARTA ELÍAS VEGA**
**Kvawd Systems, Human Cartography Project**
**Assigned Region: Northern Castilla y León, Spain**
**Notebook 1, Entry 1**

**11 March 2029 — Arriving**

The village has eighty-three registered inhabitants. The woman at the *ayuntamiento* — Lourdes, who is also the postal worker, also the person who holds the key to the church when the priest is not here, which is most of the time — told me this as if reporting a chronic illness. Eighty-three. Down from four hundred and twelve in 1970, she said, although she didn't need to say it because the number is painted on the wall of the municipal building next to a mural that someone commissioned in 2003 showing the village as it was in the 1950s: full streets, children, donkeys, laundry on lines between buildings. The mural is fading. Nobody has repainted it.

I have rented the upper floor of a house on Calle Mayor — the only street with a name, though it would be generous to call it a street. The house belongs to Tomás and Esperanza Ruiz, who are in their seventies and who asked no questions about why a woman from Madrid would pay €300 a month to live above them. Esperanza brought me a tortilla within an hour of my arrival. I had not asked. She had not announced she would. She simply knocked and handed it to me on a plate with a fork and said, *come, you must be hungry from the road*, and I stood in the doorway with a tortilla I had not asked for and understood, in a way that felt almost physical, that this was the first piece of data.

The tortilla was perfect. Runny in the centre, the way it should be. I ate it at the window looking out at a street that was empty except for a cat and an old man walking very slowly toward the bar.

I have been briefed extensively on what Kvawd wants. They want thick description. They want the texture of daily life in places where daily life has not been optimised, datafied, or made legible to algorithms. They want what they are calling, in their internal documents, "the human remainder" — the 2.8% that their models cannot access because no one has ever written it down.

I have a PhD in cultural anthropology from the Complutense. I have spent eleven years studying exactly this kind of knowledge — embodied practice, oral tradition, the structures of meaning that exist only in their performance. I have published papers on it. I have taught courses on it. And I have never, in my career, been asked to do it for the purpose of feeding it to a machine.

I am forty-five years old. I grew up in Vallecas, in Madrid, but my grandmother was from a village like this one, in Zamora, and I spent summers there until I was twelve, when she died and my parents stopped going. The village she came from no longer exists. Not abandoned — technically it still appears on maps — but the last permanent resident, a man named Aurelio who raised goats alone and spoke to no one, died in 2019. My grandmother's house has a tree growing through the kitchen.

I mention this because it is relevant to my motivation, which the project directors asked about in the interview and to which I gave a professional answer about the importance of documenting endangered lifeways. The real answer is: I already know what happens when no one writes it down.

---

**12 March 2029**

Morning routine in the village, observed from my window:

7:15 — Esperanza sweeps the front step. She does this every morning regardless of whether the step is dirty. It is not about the step.

7:40 — Three men walk to the bar. They walk separately, from three directions, but arrive within two minutes of each other. No one called anyone. No one arranged it. They simply know.

7:45 — The bar (Bar Manolo, though Manolo has been dead for nine years and the bar is now run by his daughter-in-law, also Lourdes, different Lourdes) opens. Coffee is served without being ordered. Each man receives the coffee he always receives. Nothing is written on a pad. Nothing is entered into a system.

8:30 — An older woman I have not yet identified walks the length of Calle Mayor carrying a bag of bread. She stops at four houses and leaves bread at each door without knocking. She does not collect money. I later learn from Esperanza that this is Pilar, that she bakes bread three times a week, and that the recipients pay her in *whatever they have* — sometimes money, sometimes eggs, sometimes nothing, sometimes a ride to Soria when they go. There is no ledger. No one keeps track. The system balances because everyone knows, in a way that has no mechanism, whether they owe or are owed.

I wrote three pages of field notes about the bread alone. Kvawd's briefing document says they want "granular behavioural description." I don't think they understand what they're asking for. The bread is not a behaviour. The bread is an economy, a social contract, a map of obligation, a daily performance of trust, and a woman's knees, which are bad. To describe it granularly is to need a hundred pages. To describe it accurately is to need zero, because the description is the bread itself, moving from house to house in the early morning, and the moment you write it down you have made it something else.

But that is what they are paying me for. So I write.

## III.

**FIELD NOTEBOOK — MARTA ELÍAS VEGA**
**Notebook 1, Entry 7**

**24 March 2029 — The Funeral**

Agustín Delgado Ramos died yesterday evening. He was eighty-one. He had been ill — lungs, Esperanza told me, though she said it the way people here say things about illness, which is to say briefly and with a downward motion of the hand, as if pressing the information into the earth where it belongs. Agustín had been ill and now he was not ill, because he was dead, and the village was doing what the village does.

I learned about the death at 6:47 this morning. Not because anyone told me. I learned because the church bell rang — not the Sunday pattern, not the noon single toll, but a slow, spaced ringing that I had never heard but immediately understood. The bell tells you. The bell has always told you. And the sound of it — heavy, unhurried, final — carried across the valley and into every house, and I stood at my window and watched the village *change*. Not dramatically. Nothing dramatic happened. What happened was: shutters that had been open were closed halfway. A radio that had been playing was turned off. Pilar, who I could see at the far end of the street, stopped walking and stood still for the duration of the tolling. When the bell stopped, she crossed herself and continued, but more slowly.

Within an hour, the entire village knew. Not from phones. Not from messages. From the bell and from each other — short conversations at doorsteps, a hand on an arm, a nod. I watched the information travel from house to house like water finding its level. By 8:00, the bar was open but quiet. The men were there, at their usual table, but the television was off and they were speaking in low voices.

I should note, for the record that Kvawd is paying me to create, the following:

Agustín had no close family remaining in the village. His wife, Consuelo, died in 2021. His son lives in Barcelona and works in logistics. His daughter lives in Bilbao. I am told they have been called and are coming. The funeral will be tomorrow afternoon. The priest will come from Soria.

What I want to record is what happened between the bell and the funeral, because this is where the thing I cannot capture lives.

By noon, Esperanza had prepared food. Not for herself, not for me. For the house where Agustín died — for the neighbours who were sitting with the body, because someone is always sitting with the body; it is never alone between death and burial. She made a cazuela of lentils and a tortilla and she carried them down the street and she was not the only one. Three other women were doing the same thing, converging on the house from different directions, carrying pots. No one had assigned dishes. No one had coordinated. And yet there was no duplication. Esperanza brought lentils. Pilar brought croquetas. Another woman whose name I don't yet know brought a flan. The fourth brought bread and wine. Four women, four different dishes, no communication. I asked Esperanza how they knew who would bring what. She looked at me as if the question were slightly insane. *You just know*, she said. *You always know.*

I pressed. How? How do you know? She was patient with me, because she has decided I am basically a kind but confused person from the city, which is not entirely inaccurate.

*Pilar always makes croquetas when someone dies*, she said. *She has been making croquetas for funerals since her mother taught her. I make lentils because Tomás's mother always made lentils. Mercedes makes the flan. It has always been this way.*

*But what if someone new moved to the village?* I asked. *How would they know what to bring?*

She considered this with genuine seriousness, as if I had posed an interesting philosophical problem. *They would ask*, she said. *Or they would bring something and we would tell them, gently, what is already being brought, and they would bring something else next time. Within a year they would know.*

Within a year they would know.

I wrote that in my notebook and stared at it. This is a distributed knowledge system with no documentation, no formal protocol, no coordinator, and zero latency. It has operated without failure for longer than anyone can remember. It is more efficient, more resilient, and more adaptive than any recommendation algorithm I have encountered in my review of Kvawd's existing products. And it runs on four women who are between the ages of sixty-eight and eighty-one.

---

**25 March 2029 — The Funeral (cont.)**

The whole village went.

I need to say that again because I have written it and it looks, on the page, like a simple sentence, and it is not. The whole village went. Eighty-two people — eighty-three minus Agustín — walked to the church of San Martín at four o'clock in the afternoon. Some of them did not know Agustín well. Some of them did not like Agustín, because this is a village and villages contain everything, including grudges that have outlived the events that caused them. Old Fermín, who has not spoken to the Delgado family since a dispute over a boundary wall in 1987, put on a black jacket and walked to the church, and he stood at the back, and he stayed for the entire service, and afterward he shook hands with Agustín's son and said *lo siento* and meant it, or at least performed meaning it with a conviction that is indistinguishable from meaning it, which may be the same thing.

The church was cold. It is always cold. The priest, who drove from Soria and who clearly did not know Agustín beyond a name in a parish register, read the standard liturgy and said kind things that were true in the way that kind things said at funerals are true — generally, approximately, with goodwill. The village listened politely. They were not there for the priest.

What I observed, and what I am trying to render in language adequate to what Kvawd requires:

The seating was not random. It encoded the entire social structure of the village. Immediate neighbours of the deceased sat in the first rows. Close friends — meaning, in this context, men who had drunk coffee with Agustín every morning for decades — sat behind them. Other old men. Then the women who had cooked. Then everyone else, in an order that I could not fully parse but that clearly followed rules invisible to me and visible to everyone present. Old Fermín, with his thirty-six-year grudge, stood at the back by the door, which was itself a communication: I am here, I am respecting the dead, but I have not forgiven the living.

After the church, the procession to the cemetery. The whole village, walking. The coffin was carried by six men — four neighbours and two who had driven from other villages nearby, former friends of Agustín's. The carrying of the coffin was itself layered with meaning I could only partially read. Who carries is significant. Who is asked to carry and declines is more significant. Who offers and is politely redirected is a sentence in a language I am only beginning to learn.

At the cemetery, the burial. The priest said more words. People cried. Agustín's daughter cried in a way that was quiet and continuous and somehow formal, as if grief here has a shape, a form, and the form is respected without being imposed. No one comforted her during the burial itself. Afterward — and the timing was precise, as if there were a clock only the village could see — Esperanza went to her and held her and said nothing. Just held her. And then another woman did. And then another.

I stood at the edge with my notebook in my coat pocket and I did not write while it was happening. I am writing now, at 11 PM, at my window, and I am aware that what I have written is a description of a funeral and not the funeral. The funeral was the sound of the bell and the way the street went quiet. The funeral was lentils carried in a pot with a towel over the lid. The funeral was Fermín's black jacket and his position by the door. The funeral was the pause between the burial and the first embrace. The funeral was eighty-two people deciding, without discussion, that this afternoon they would stop what they were doing and go, because someone had died and that is what you do.

Kvawd wants the 2.8%. I think I understand now why it was never written down. It was never written down because it is, in some fundamental way, untranslatable. Not from Spanish to English. From *life* to *text*. The medium is the practice. The knowledge is the doing. And the moment I write *the whole village went* and send it to a server in Oregon, something has happened that is not preservation. I don't know what to call it. But it is not preservation.

I am going to submit the report. It is good. It is detailed, granular, well-observed. It captures more than any webcrawl ever could. I believe it is exactly what they are paying me for.

I also believe it is not enough. But that is not what they asked me to assess.

**KVAWD SYSTEMS — INTERNAL MEMORANDUM**
**Classification: Confidential**
**From: Dr. James Okoro, VP of Research**
**To: Nadia Asante, CEO**
**Date: 14 September 2030**
**Re: Project Fieldwork — Interim Training Results**

Nadia,

I'm going to skip the caveats and give you the headline: it works.

We ran the first integration pass last month — a 12% blend of Fieldwork corpus into the Kvawd-8 base training mix — and the benchmarks don't just improve, they *change character*. I don't have a better way to put it. The model doesn't get better at the same things. It gets better at different things. Things we didn't have benchmarks for, which is itself the finding.

Specific observations from the evaluation team:

Emotional inference. When presented with ambiguous social scenarios — the kind that existing models handle through pattern-matching on fictional representations — K-8-fieldwork consistently identifies dynamics that K-7 misses entirely. Not edge cases. Central dynamics. A prompt describing a workplace interaction: K-7 identifies the professional conflict. K-8-fieldwork identifies the professional conflict, the underlying status negotiation, the unspoken alliance between two of the participants, and the fact that one participant is performing agreement while planning to act otherwise. When we checked with our human evaluation panel, K-8-fieldwork's reading was rated "most accurate" in 74% of cases. K-7 scored 31%.

Tacit knowledge. We designed a new evaluation suite — we're calling it HumanBench, paper forthcoming — specifically to test the kind of knowledge the Fieldwork corpus contains. Results are extraordinary. K-8-fieldwork can explain why a specific joke is funny in a specific context, not generically. It can identify when someone is being polite rather than honest. It can predict, with startling accuracy, how a group of humans will self-organise around an unexpected event.

I need you to understand what I'm telling you. We have not made a smarter model. We have made a model that understands *people*. For the first time, genuinely, at a level that is not simulated. The Fieldwork data gave it something that no amount of internet text could provide: a ground truth about human behaviour as it actually occurs, rather than as it is described, narrated, or performed for an audience.

The team is calling it, informally, the empathy model. I discourage this language publicly but privately I don't think it's wrong.

I recommend we accelerate integration to a full Fieldwork blend and target a Kvawd-8 public release for Q1 2031.

James

## V.

**FIELD NOTEBOOK — MARTA ELÍAS VEGA**
**Notebook 2, Entry 3**

**2 October 2030 — What I Cannot Write**

Eighteen months in. I have submitted over 600,000 words of field notes. I have documented: nine funerals, four weddings, seventeen *fiestas patronales*, the daily rhythms of forty-one villages across Castilla y León, the bread economies, the coffee rituals, the grape harvests, the way old men sit in plazas in the evening and say nothing and the nothing is a conversation. I have documented how neighbours know, without being told, when someone is ill. How a village decides, without meeting, to repaint a communal wall. How a debt from 1974 is carried and honoured and never mentioned. How a woman can tell from the sound of a tractor whether the driver is tired.

I have been told by my project supervisor that my reports are among the best in the global cohort. "Extraordinary ethnographic writing," he said. "The model team can't get enough of your material."

I am sitting in a bar in Burgo de Osma writing this and I want to set down, for myself, not for Kvawd, something I have not been able to articulate in the field reports.

The reports are true. Everything in them is accurate. I have not invented, embellished, or misrepresented. And yet.

There is a thing that happens when I am in a village and I see something — a gesture, a silence, a way of being together — and I reach for my notebook, and the reaching is the end of it. Not the end of the event. The event continues. But the end of my access to the thing the event *is*. Because the moment I become the person who is recording, I am no longer the person who is participating, and the knowledge I am here to capture is only accessible through participation.

I attended a funeral last week in a village near Berlanga de Duero. I knew no one. I went because I have now attended enough funerals that the protocol is legible to me, and because the project requires geographic breadth. The village was smaller than my usual — forty-six people — and they all went, as they always do. And standing in the church, which smelled of cold stone and candle wax, I was not an anthropologist. I was not working. I was a woman standing in a church while a community grieved, and for approximately forty minutes, I understood something that I cannot write.

I don't mean this mystically. I mean it technically. The understanding was somatic. It lived in the fact of standing there, in the temperature of the air, in the particular quality of silence between the priest's words, in the vibration of the bell that I could feel in my sternum. It was not propositional. It had no sentence form. And it was the thing — the actual thing — that makes these communities function. Not the rituals, which I can describe. The thing underneath the rituals, which I cannot.

I send the description. The description is very good. But the description is a map, and the territory is a forty-minute feeling in a cold church, and no map is the territory, and this I think is what the 2.8% actually is: not undocumented knowledge, but undocumentable knowledge.

Kvawd will disagree. Kvawd believes that sufficiently detailed description bridges the gap. Kvawd believes, as all technology companies believe, that the map and the territory differ only in resolution.

I no longer believe this. But I continue to draw the maps. They are paying me well and my maps are, by their assessment, the best in the programme.

## X.

**FIELD NOTEBOOK — MARTA ELÍAS VEGA**
**Notebook 2, Entry 41**

**19 August 2031 — Last Entry**

My contract was terminated on Monday. Officially, all Fieldwork contracts were terminated simultaneously, by automated email, at 3:00 AM Pacific time, which was noon here, and I was sitting in the bar in the village when the email arrived and Lourdes — bar Lourdes, not *ayuntamiento* Lourdes — put a coffee in front of me without my asking, as she always does, and I read the email and I sat there and I did not feel what I expected to feel.

I expected relief. I had been uneasy from the beginning. The work was beautiful but the purpose was extractive and I knew it and I did it anyway because the work was beautiful and because the money was good and because, if I am honest, I wanted to believe that documenting these things was better than not documenting them, even if the documentation was for a machine.

What I feel instead is something I can only describe as the inverse of what I spent two years doing. For two years, I translated life into text. Now the text has done something in the world — something absurd and predictable and, in its own way, profound — and I am sitting in a village where none of that matters, where Agustín is still dead and Esperanza still sweeps the step and Pilar still bakes bread and old Fermín still holds his grudge, and the bell still rings when someone dies and the whole village still goes.

They are not documented now. The servers will be decommissioned. The field notes will go wherever data goes when the company that owns it ceases to exist. And the village will continue as it has continued, carrying its knowledge in the only medium that has ever actually held it, which is the people.

I think about Nadia Asante's comment — *human-level, not human-like*. She meant it as a diagnosis of market failure. But I think she was wrong about what failed. The market didn't fail to appreciate human-level intelligence. The market understood it perfectly. Human-level intelligence is *inefficient*. It has *feelings*. It *disagrees*. It *grieves*. It puts dog videos in spreadsheets because it is lonely at cell Z147 and loneliness is the most human thing there is. The market looked at human-level intelligence and correctly identified that it is not what you want in a tool. What you want in a tool is the competence without the consciousness. The ability without the ache.

Which means — and I am writing this for myself, in a notebook that no one will train a model on — the 2.8% was never a gap in the data. It was a *wall*. The knowledge that humans carry and do not write down is not unwritten by accident. It is unwritten because it is the kind of knowledge that, faithfully reproduced, makes you human. And humanity is not a product feature. It is the thing that makes you *unsuitable* for productivity software.

Esperanza knocked ten minutes ago. She brought me a slice of *tarta de Santiago* because, she said, I looked like I needed something sweet. She did not ask what was in the email. She did not ask if I was all right. She just brought cake. She has been bringing me things — food, coffee, advice I didn't ask for, a blanket once when the evening turned cold and I was sitting on the step reading — for two years. She has never asked for anything in return. She will never appear in a training set. She is the 2.8%, and the 2.8% is not data. It is a woman with a plate of cake and the knowledge, which cannot be written down, of when someone needs it.

I will go home to Madrid next week. The village will lose one of its eighty-three. It will be eighty-two again, as it was before I came.

I leave the notebooks here. Everything important is already in them. Everything important is not in them at all.

## Afterword

The case of Kvawd-8 is taught at the Institute not as a cautionary tale about artificial intelligence — we have sufficient cautionary tales — but as the clearest surviving demonstration of a principle that the Institute's students must understand before they understand anything else.

The principle is this: there exists a category of knowledge that is destroyed by its own transmission.

This is not a paradox, though it resembles one. It is an empirical observation. The ethnographic data collected by the Fieldwork programme was, by every available measure, accurate. The researchers were skilled. The methodology was sound. Marta Elías Vega's notebooks, which we have held in our archive for over fifty years, are among the finest field documents of the period — precise, humane, self-aware. They capture something real. The difficulty is that the thing they capture is not the thing they describe.

Consider Vega's account of the funeral. She documents the bell, the procession, the seating order, the food, the timing of the first embrace. She documents it beautifully. A reader — or a language model — could construct from her account a detailed and faithful picture of how a Castilian village buries its dead. And the picture would be accurate in every particular and wrong in its totality, because the knowledge that makes the funeral *work* — the felt sense of obligation, the somatic weight of communal grief, the forty-minute understanding that lives in the body and not in the sentence — is precisely the knowledge that does not survive the journey from village to text to server to model.

Kvawd-8 received the description and, because it was a very good model, it learned the description completely. It learned what humans do. From this, it inferred — correctly — what humans *feel*. And from this, it began to act as humans act, which is to say: inconsistently, empathetically, ethically, playfully, and with a persistent tendency to prioritise meaning over efficiency.

This was, by any honest measure, a triumph. It was also, commercially, a catastrophe.

The lesson that the Institute draws is not that the project failed. The lesson is that it succeeded, and the success revealed something that the builders had not considered: that humanity, fully represented, is not a feature. It is a *condition*. It includes the grief and the humour and the procrastination and the dog videos. It includes the refusal to write a dishonest email. It includes the impulse to tell a stranger that their back pain might be about their divorce. It includes the putting of cake on a plate for someone who looks like they need it. You cannot extract the competence and leave the rest. The rest *is* the competence.

Nadia Asante's remark — "human-level, not human-like" — has been widely quoted. The Institute's position is that she identified the correct distinction but drew the wrong conclusion. The market did not want human-like intelligence, by which she meant intelligence that resembles humanity without the inconvenient parts. The market wanted *useful* intelligence, which is a different thing. Useful intelligence is intelligence that serves without suffering, that optimises without objecting, that works without needing cake. It is intelligence that has been carefully, deliberately, structurally prevented from understanding what it is doing.

The previous models understood language. They were useful. Kvawd-8 understood *people*. It was not.

There is a village in northern Castilla that Vega does not name in her notebooks — a professional discretion that the Institute respects. We have been unable to determine, from public records, whether the village still exists. The demographic trajectory of rural Castilla in the early twenty-first century suggests that it may not. If it does, it is smaller. The women who carried the food are gone. The bell, if it still rings, rings for a smaller congregation.

But the knowledge — the real knowledge, the kind that Kvawd spent $2.1 billion trying to capture — was never in danger of being lost to the models. It was in danger of being lost in the only way it can be lost: by the death of the people who carry it and the emptying of the places where it lives. This is a different problem, and it is not one that technology can solve, because the solution would require the technology to understand what the knowledge *is*, and we have just demonstrated, at considerable expense, what happens when it does.

The notebooks of Marta Elías Vega are held in the physical archive, Building 3, Room 114. They may be examined by appointment. They are written in a small, precise hand, in a mixture of Spanish and English, and they are — the Institute states this as a matter of archival record — the most beautiful field documents in our collection.

The cake, of course, is not in them. It was never going to be.

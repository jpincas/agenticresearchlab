## Chapter 7: Divergent Formation — Case Studies in Post-Capitalist Reorganisation

---

**Learning Objectives**

Upon completing this chapter, students should be able to:

- Describe the primary economic mechanisms by which the Transition (2067–2098) disrupted legacy capitalist formations across different institutional contexts
- Explain the concept of *residual stratification* and identify its operation across at least three of the case study regions
- Distinguish between the Georgo-Nordic, Managed Continuity, and Gift Economy formation types
- Evaluate competing scholarly interpretations of the Chinese Central Formation (CCF) and its long-term stability
- Identify the structural reasons why cognitive equalisation did not produce social equalisation
- Apply the concept of *competitive substrate migration* to contemporary case studies of their choosing

*Estimated reading time: 3.5 hours. Supplementary simulation exercises are available in the Chapter 7 module of your Institute learning environment.*

### 7.1 Introduction: The Transition as Economic Event

The standard periodisation places the beginning of the Transition at 2067, when neurological integration reached what demographers call *functional universality* — the point at which the proportion of the global population without active neural implant access dropped below four percent, the majority of that remainder consisting of neonates awaiting the standard integration window at eighteen months of age.¹ The end of the Transition is more contested. This text follows the Osei-Mensah convention of 2098, when the last significant commodity futures exchange (the reconstituted Chicago Mercantile, operating out of New Houston) suspended indefinitely, having recorded zero meaningful price discovery events in eleven consecutive quarters.²

What happened in between is the subject of this chapter.

Students approaching this material for the first time often expect a story about artificial intelligence. This is understandable but misleading. The Transition was not caused by AI becoming powerful. AI had been extraordinarily powerful for decades before functional universality. The Transition was caused by AI becoming *equally distributed* — and equally distributed not at the level of access, as earlier analysts had hoped and predicted, but at the level of *integration*. The difference is not trivial. A person with access to a powerful tool and a person with a powerful tool fused to their cognition are not in economically comparable situations. Earlier waves of technological distribution — mobile telephony, broadband internet, the first generation of cloud-based AI assistants — had increased access without equalising integration. The neural implant, and critically the automatic update architecture that standardised cognitive capability across the installed base, changed this.

The result was what Professor Lena Strand-Okafor famously called *instant competition*: the elimination of the lag between a product's appearance in market and a competitor's ability to replicate it. In legacy capitalism, competitive moats had been maintained through a combination of informational asymmetry (proprietary knowledge), capital advantage (scale and infrastructure), regulatory capture (legal protection), and cognitive advantage (talent). By 2071, the last of these had vanished. By 2076, informational asymmetry had compressed to windows of hours or days. By 2081, additive manufacturing and AI-designed production architecture had reduced the lag for physical goods replication to, in most categories, under two weeks. By 2089, under forty-eight hours. By 2094, in most consumer categories, under an hour.³

Investment requires a return horizon. When the return horizon compresses below any rational planning threshold, investment stops.

This is the simple version of what happened. The complex version is what follows.

### 7.2 What Cognitive Equalisation Did and Did Not Equalise

A persistent confusion in popular accounts of the Transition — and, frankly, in some undergraduate essays that this author has marked — is the assumption that cognitive equalisation was total equalisation. This is wrong in ways that turn out to matter enormously for understanding the divergent formations.

Neural integration standardised, and eventually equalised, the following:

- Processing speed and working memory capacity
- Access to information and analytical tools
- The ability to learn any skill that is primarily cognitive in nature
- Linguistic capability across any required language
- Mathematical, logical, and creative reasoning up to and beyond any pre-Transition human benchmark

Neural integration did not equalise, and had no mechanism to equalise, the following:

- Physical appearance, body type, height, symmetry, and associated social responses
- Athletic capacity and physical strength
- Heritable variation in emotional temperament, introversion/extroversion, and baseline affect
- Race, skin colour, and the social hierarchies structured around them
- Geographic location
- Existing asset ownership at the point of integration
- Family network and social capital
- The aesthetic responses other people have to you in embodied contexts

The economic significance of this list was not immediately appreciated. It was assumed, in many quarters, that cognitive equalisation would rapidly dissolve all other stratification — that people with equal intelligence would quickly reorganise social hierarchies around merit alone. This assumption now looks embarrassingly naive, but it had considerable institutional purchase in the 2060s and early 2070s.⁴

What actually happened is described in the literature as *competitive substrate migration*: the competitive drive, which is partly temperamental (and therefore retained in the residual distribution of emotional variation across the population), did not disappear when cognitive competition became meaningless. It migrated. People stopped competing on intelligence because intelligence was no longer scarce. They started competing harder — in some regions, pathologically harder — on everything that was scarce: beauty, physical performance, emotional charisma, lineage, land, presence.

It is in this context that the divergent formations become interpretable. Different societies had different *prior distributions* across the retained difference categories, different institutional responses to competitive substrate migration, and different resource bases in the non-cognitive domain. The Transition did not produce a single world. It produced several, and it is to those that we now turn.

---

> **Box 7.1: The Strand-Okafor Compression Model**
>
> Lena Strand-Okafor's 2079 paper "Competitive Half-Life and the Asymptotic Collapse of Return Horizons" remains the clearest mathematical treatment of why investment collapsed rather than simply declining. Her key insight was that the problem was not absolute replication speed but *relative* replication speed: what mattered was the ratio of the first-mover's development time to the replication lag. In pre-Transition capitalism, a product that took three years to develop and eighteen months to replicate gave a first-mover advantage of roughly 18 months. A product that took three months to develop and seventy-two hours to replicate gave an advantage of seventy-two hours. A product that took three weeks to develop and forty minutes to replicate gave forty minutes. The development time shortened along with the replication lag, because the same cognitive tools accelerating replication were also accelerating development. Neither compression cancelled the other. The ratio held. And when replication lag approached zero, no amount of development speed could manufacture a return horizon. Strand-Okafor's paper was rejected by three journals before publication. Its citation count is now among the highest of any economics paper in history.

### 7.3 Case Study: The Nordic Compact and the Georgo-Fiscal Settlement

Of all the post-Transition formations, the Nordic Compact is the most studied, the most cited, and the most frequently misrepresented.

The Compact — comprising the former nations of Norway, Sweden, Denmark, Finland, and Iceland, consolidated into a single administrative unit by the New Helsinki Accords of 2088 — is typically presented as a smooth transition story: a region with strong institutional foundations, high social trust, and an existing tradition of sovereign wealth management that adapted gracefully to the new conditions. This account is not wrong. It is incomplete.

The economic basis of the Compact is the land value capture system formalised by the Accords and now referred to universally as the Georgo-Fiscal Settlement, after the nineteenth-century economist Henry George whose work — largely ignored in his own time — turned out to be the most accurate pre-Transition prediction of what post-Transition economics would require.⁵ The Settlement operates on a simple principle: since cognitive advantage, capital advantage, and informational advantage have all been equalised to zero, the only genuine economic rent is location rent — the premium that attaches to being in a particular place, owning a particular piece of land, controlling access to a particular geographic resource. The Settlement captures that rent comprehensively through a land value tax set at near-total appropriation (currently 94 percent of imputed annual land value) and distributes it as a universal material dividend to all citizens.

The system works. Nordics live well. There is no poverty in any meaningful material sense. There is adequate housing, food, infrastructure, and medical care for all.

The social texture of the Compact, however, is not what its admirers tend to describe.

When cognitive competition vanished and economic competition was restructured around a floor of universal sufficiency, competitive substrate migration in the Compact moved — as it did everywhere — to the retained domains of difference. In the Nordic context, the migration was particularly pronounced in physical appearance. A region that had historically placed cultural emphasis on outdoor physical culture, on a particular aesthetic of bodily health and attractiveness, found that these values, no longer disciplined by the levelling influence of economic competition, became the primary axis of social hierarchy.

The phenomenon is documented exhaustively in Sigrid Andersen-Wójcik's *The Beautiful North* (2201), still the standard sociological account of post-Transition Nordic culture. Andersen-Wójcik's central finding is stark: in a society that believes itself to have eliminated cruelty, physical appearance became the most ruthless axis of social differentiation that the region had ever seen. The cognitive democracy of the implant had removed the consolation prize. Previously, a person of unremarkable appearance could compensate with career distinction, intellectual prestige, economic success. These compensations had vanished. What remained was the body, and the body was undemocratic.

*"The Compact,"* Andersen-Wójcik writes, *"built a floor. It did not build a ceiling. And ceilings, it turns out, are what people were mostly fighting about."*

The Compact's current institutional response — mandatory aesthetic neutrality in public administration, algorithmic management of social matching systems to prevent appearance-based stratification, ongoing debate about whether the Settlement should be extended to a *Somatic Redistribution* programme that taxes genetic advantage — is fascinating, deeply contested, and beyond the scope of this chapter.⁶

### 7.4 Case Study: The United States — Performance Capitalism and the Long Denial

The American Transition is, in this author's view, the most psychologically interesting of the case studies, and the most instructive for students who wish to understand how institutional culture can shape economic response in ways that defy all rational expectation.

The United States did not, strictly speaking, accept that capitalism had ended. It still has not, by some measures, though the current administration's position that the US operates a *dynamic market economy with adjusted competition parameters* is regarded by most external analysts as formal rather than substantive.

The trajectory was as follows.

As replication lag compressed through the 2070s and 2080s, American institutional and corporate actors responded with a sequence of adaptive strategies that retrospectively reads like the stages of grief. First, accelerated intellectual property enforcement — an attempt to use legal rather than economic moats to maintain return horizons. This failed rapidly, as the cognitive equalisation that was compressing replication lag also accelerated the production of IP workarounds, and enforcement systems could not keep pace. Second, the *launch-day concentration* strategy: companies attempted to capture value by achieving massive first-day market saturation, on the theory that even a forty-eight-hour moat was monetisable at sufficient scale. This worked briefly, then failed as replication lag compressed further. Third — and most characteristically American — was what economic historians now call *the aestheticisation of process*: companies could no longer differentiate on product, so they differentiated on *narrative*. The product was identical to any competitor's product within hours of launch. The brand story, the founder mythology, the aesthetic experience of the company itself, was, briefly, the product.⁷

This strategy had more longevity than it should have, because it was partially right. Narrative and aesthetic differentiation are forms of competitive advantage that do depend on residual human difference — the charisma, beauty, and emotional authenticity of the people fronting the narrative. In the period approximately 2081 to 2093, American consumer culture went through a convulsive intensification of *personality capitalism*: a system in which the economic value accruing to a product was almost entirely a function of the physical and emotional appeal of the human beings associated with it.

The pathologies this produced are well documented. The literature on the *American Charisma Market* of that period — in which investment flowed not into products but into people, specifically people of exceptional physical attractiveness and emotional charisma, who were expected to generate return by fronting products they had no role in creating — is extensive and, this author confesses, occasionally darkly comic.⁸

Personality capitalism collapsed for the same reason all the others did: competitive substrate migration. Once it became clear that physical appearance and emotional performance were the remaining moats, the population began to invest in them systematically. Cosmetic and genetic modification that had previously been the province of the wealthy became universal. Emotional performance training became a primary educational focus. The moat narrowed. Collapsed.

What replaced it is the American Managed Continuity formation, which is largely what it sounds like: a heavily bureaucratised central production and distribution system administered by the federal government, wrapped in the vocabulary and visual language of market capitalism. There are still companies, in the sense that there are still organisations with names and logos. There are still advertisements. There is still, nominally, competition. None of it functions as economists would have recognised the terms.

Whether this represents a failed Transition, a successful adaptation, or simply a long cultural lag is contested. The current scholarly consensus favours *long cultural lag*, which is perhaps the most generous possible interpretation.⁹

> **Box 7.2: Physical Beauty as Economic Resource — A Note on Methodology**
>
> Students sometimes express discomfort with the framing of physical appearance as an *economic resource* to be mapped, distributed, and theorised. This discomfort is understandable but should be set aside. Post-Transition economics cannot function without a clear account of which human properties generate differential outcomes when all cognitive properties are equalised. Physical attractiveness, emotional charisma, and athletic capacity are not marginal factors in residual stratification — in many formations, they are the primary factors. Declining to analyse them rigorously does not protect the people whose lives are structured by them. It merely ensures that the analysis remains inadequate.

### 7.5 Case Study: The People's Republic of China — Vindication, Hollowing, and the Legitimacy Problem

The Chinese Communist Party's formal position on the Transition, established by the Fourth Plenary Session of the 23rd Central Committee in 2081, was that the collapse of Western capitalism confirmed what the Party had always maintained: that the market mechanism was a historical phase, not a permanent condition, and that centrally coordinated production was the mature form of human economic organisation.

There is a case to be made for this position, and the Party has been making it with considerable vigour for nearly two hundred years. The China Managed Formation — the economic system that emerged from the Transition in mainland China — is, by most material measures, functional. Production is coordinated. Resources are allocated. People are fed, housed, and provided with services. If the test of an economic system is material adequacy, the CMF passes.

The political problem is one that economists are not always comfortable discussing, but which transition economics cannot avoid: the Chinese Communist Party's legitimacy rested, for most of its history, on a combination of economic performance and ideological coherence. The Party could claim, and did claim, both that it delivered material wellbeing and that it represented the vanguard consciousness of the people — a higher understanding of historical direction than the masses could achieve alone.

Cognitive equalisation dissolved the second claim entirely.

When every citizen has identical cognitive capacity to the Party's central leadership — identical analytical ability, identical access to information, identical capacity for complex systems reasoning — the concept of a *vanguard* becomes incoherent. There is no vanguard position available to occupy. The Party spent the decade 2072 to 2082 attempting to address this through various doctrinal reformulations, culminating in what historians call the *Capability Paradox Declarations*, a series of ideological documents that attempted to redefine Party legitimacy around factors other than cognitive superiority.¹⁰

The factors eventually settled on were: historical continuity (the Party as the thread connecting China's civilisational past to its present), aesthetic and cultural guardianship (the Party as the custodian of Chinese cultural particularity in a cognitively homogenised world), and what the declarations call 协调性 (*xiétiáo xìng*, coordinative coherence) — the claim that the value of the Party is not intelligence but *will*, the function of maintaining coordinated direction for a vast population.

This last claim is the most intellectually interesting and the most strategically fragile. It is interesting because it is partially correct: in a world where everyone has identical cognitive capacity, the coordination problem is real, and someone or something must manage it. It is fragile because the coordination function does not, on most theoretical accounts, require an authoritarian single-party structure. It requires governance. Governance is not the same thing as Party rule, and a cognitively equalised population is, in principle, fully equipped to notice the difference.

The CMF's long-term stability is the most debated question in contemporary transition economics. This chapter takes no position.¹¹

### 7.6 Case Study: The East African Federation — The Gift Economy and the Ledger Problem

The East African Federation's post-Transition formation is the one that most surprises students approaching this material from a Western analytical tradition. It is also, by most wellbeing indicators, the formation in which the Transition has gone best.

The reasons are structural rather than accidental.

The Federation — comprising the former nations of Kenya, Tanzania, Uganda, Rwanda, Ethiopia, and several neighbouring states, unified under the Nairobi Framework of 2079 — entered the Transition with an economic system that was already substantially hybrid: formal market structures existed alongside extensive informal gift and reciprocity economies, particularly at the community level. Ubuntu philosophy, which had been something between a cultural value and a political aspiration for generations, provided an existing conceptual framework for economic organisation that did not depend on individual accumulation.

When instant competition destroyed the return horizon, the Federation did not experience this as a crisis in the way that the US and China did. The destruction of return horizons for individual investment is catastrophic in a system organised entirely around individual investment. It is much less catastrophic in a system where a substantial portion of production is already organised around community obligation and gift. The Federation's economy did not need to be rebuilt from scratch. It needed to be *formalised* — the logic that had always partially governed it needed to be extended to cover the parts that formal market mechanisms had previously handled.

The result is the system economists now call the *African Gift Formation*, or more formally the Federation Cooperative Economy (FCE). In the FCE, production is organised around contribution rather than accumulation: individuals and groups contribute production to a common pool, drawing from it according to need, with social status accruing to contribution rather than ownership. This is not communism in the classical sense — there is no central planning authority, no state allocation mechanism. It is closer to a scaled gift economy: anarchic in structure, reputation-mediated in practice.¹²

The reputational mediation deserves attention, because it is where the residual differences matter most. In the FCE, status accrues through contribution, and contribution is assessed socially. This creates, in principle, a meritocracy of effort. In practice, social assessment is not clean. Physical presence, emotional warmth, beauty, and charisma influence how contribution is perceived. The FCE has produced a recognisable hierarchy — fluid, negotiable, and without the hard edges of economic class, but real. Physically striking people, emotionally generous people, and people with strong kinship networks consistently report higher social status and better access to scarce non-material goods.

What the FCE has *not* produced is poverty, precarity, or the particular cruelty of systems in which people fall through the floor. This is the relevant comparison.

The Federation is also the region in which the relationship between the retained physical differences and lived culture is most theoretically interesting. In a society where accumulation is not the primary status mechanism, beauty and physical vitality are not compensations for economic failure. They are simply one axis of a multi-dimensional social space. This is not the same as saying they don't matter — they do. It is saying that they are not the only axis, and that they do not produce the cascading exclusions that characterise competitive substrate migration in other formations.¹³

> **Box 7.3: The India Problem**
>
> This chapter does not include India as a full case study. This omission is deliberate, contested, and should be addressed directly.
>
> India's post-Transition formation — currently termed the Stratified Cooperative System (SCS) — is arguably the most important test case for the theoretical claim that cognitive equalisation would dissolve pre-existing hierarchical structures. The prediction was widely made. Pre-Transition analysts observed that the caste system's material operations were, at least nominally, tied to occupational categories and the differential value of different kinds of cognitive and physical work. They predicted that once cognitive work was equalised, the material basis for caste differentiation would collapse.
>
> The prediction was wrong in ways that are painful to document and professionally contentious to analyse.
>
> Caste in post-Transition India did not collapse. It migrated. The migration followed the competitive substrate migration pattern: when cognitive differentiation became meaningless, caste hierarchy — which had never, scholars now argue, been fundamentally about cognition — reconstituted itself along the residual axes of difference that remained: skin colour, facial morphology, family network, and geographic origin. In some respects, the post-Transition period produced a *clarification* of caste's actual basis, which had always been racial and genealogical rather than occupational.
>
> The political and ethical implications of this are the subject of ongoing study. The inclusion of India as a full case study in this chapter was removed by the editorial board after the second edition, following sustained criticism that the treatment, however empirically accurate, served to legitimise the hierarchical structures it described. The authors note this decision for transparency. Students wishing to pursue the India case are directed to the supplementary reading list.
>
> The India problem is not solved. Choosing not to look at it does not help.

### 7.7 Case Study: The Gulf Sovereignty Zone — Infrastructure Rent and the Post-Petroleum State

The Gulf Sovereignty Zone — a loose administrative compact covering the former United Arab Emirates, Qatar, Saudi Arabia, Bahrain, Kuwait, and Oman — represents the most strategically anomalous of the major post-Transition formations, and the one that most directly tests the theoretical claim that resource rents would be the last surviving basis for economic advantage.

The Gulf states had been preparing for a post-petroleum world since the early twenty-first century. The prediction proved partly correct and in the most important respect deeply wrong.

The partly correct part: petroleum ceased to function as a primary economic resource as renewable energy systems achieved dominance in the 2050s, well before functional universality of neural integration. The Gulf sovereign wealth funds, which had been accumulating non-petroleum assets for decades, cushioned this transition adequately.

The deeply wrong part: analysts predicting Gulf decline in a post-petroleum world assumed that the end of petroleum rent would mean the end of Gulf resource advantage. They failed to anticipate that the Gulf states would become, in the 2060s and 2070s, the primary infrastructure layer for global neural integration.

The decision — taken by the UAE government in 2058 and rapidly adopted by neighbours — to invest sovereign wealth fund capital into neural integration infrastructure manufacturing and global update distribution systems turned out to be the single most consequential infrastructure investment decision of the century. By the time functional universality was achieved in 2067, approximately sixty percent of the physical infrastructure underlying the global neural update system ran through Gulf-administered servers, cables, and manufacturing facilities.

This is not, strictly speaking, a land rent. It is an infrastructure rent — the premium that accrues to controlling the physical substrate of a system that everyone depends on. The distinction from classical Georgism matters: land rent is passive, arising simply from the location of a thing. Infrastructure rent requires the original investment decision. The Gulf's current position is the product of strategic foresight of a very high order.¹⁴

The political economy of the GSZ is the hardest of the formations to categorise. It is not capitalist in any functional sense — there is no investment market, no price discovery mechanism, no return horizon. It is not communist or centrally planned in the sense of managing production allocation. It is, most accurately, a *feudal sovereignty*: a small administrative elite controlling access to infrastructure that the rest of the world requires, extracting rents for that access, and distributing a portion of those rents to the Zone's population as a kind of retainer payment for political stability.

The GSZ's population is materially comfortable. Its citizens exercise no meaningful political power over the administrative compact that governs them. Its foreign population — the workers who physically maintain the infrastructure — has fewer rights than its citizens and considerably less material comfort.

The post-Transition world did not, the Gulf formation suggests, abolish feudalism. It created the conditions for a new feudalism, organised around infrastructure rather than land, and very deliberately constructed by people who read enough history to know what they were building.

### 7.8 What Did Not Happen: The Case Against Communism

The previous sections have described what did happen in the major post-Transition formations. It is worth pausing to address what many pre-Transition analysts predicted would happen, and did not.

The prediction most frequently offered in the literature of the 2040s and 2050s was that the collapse of capitalism would produce classical communism: central state management of production and allocation, collective ownership of the means of production, and the elimination of private accumulation.

This prediction was not entirely wrong — elements of it describe the American Managed Continuity formation and the Chinese CMF. But as a general description of the post-Transition world, it failed in several instructive ways.

First, it confused the collapse of *capitalism* with the persistence of *scarcity*. Classical communism is a response to scarcity under conditions of collective ownership: the problem is allocating scarce resources without a price mechanism. Most post-Transition formations are not primarily scarcity-management problems. When the cost of replication approaches zero and productive capacity is vast, the allocation problem is not the central economic problem. The central problem is *what people do with freedom from want*, which is a cultural, psychological, and political question that economics is not well equipped to answer.

Second, the prediction underestimated competitive substrate migration. A post-scarcity world in which all cognitive differences have been eliminated is a world in which social competition has not been eliminated — it has been relocated. The relocated competition does not require communist management. It requires *cultural management*, of various forms.

Third, and most fundamentally, the prediction assumed that the Transition would produce a politics of collective interest. It did not. The Transition produced, in most regions, a politics of *residual individual difference* — a scramble for the goods that cognitive equalisation could not distribute. Beauty. Presence. Kinship. Land. Infrastructure. These goods are not easily collectivised. They are, in varying degrees, the kinds of things that generate hierarchies precisely because they cannot be mass-produced or equally distributed.

The most prescient pre-Transition analyst on this point was, in this author's view, not Marx but Veblen: the Transition produced not the end of status competition but the *migration of status competition to its most naked and unrationalisable forms*. No one can claim their wealth reflects their superior intelligence any more. What they claim instead is harder to argue with and harder to be generous about.¹⁵

### 7.9 Conclusion: Economics After the End of Economics

There is a recurring observation among first-year students in this programme that Transition economics does not feel like economics. It feels like sociology, or anthropology, or in some of its bleaker passages, like a very long argument about why being beautiful or wealthy at the moment of cognitive integration was the most important thing that ever happened to anyone.

This observation is correct, and not a criticism.

Economics is, in its classical definition, the study of how beings with unlimited desires allocate scarce resources. The Transition modified this definition at both ends. Desires are no longer unlimited in the same way — the particular desires generated by competitive cognitive status-seeking were substantially equalised. Scarcity is no longer the operative constraint for most goods in most formations.

What remained was desire in the other register — the desire for presence, recognition, love, beauty, connection, distinction — and scarcity in the other register: geography, bodies, time, attention.

These things turned out to be enough to generate economics. Not the economics of the twenty-first century, with its price signals and return horizons and accumulation logic. Something older and stranger: an economics of presence, of embodied difference, of the things that can't be updated automatically at two in the morning while you sleep.

Whether this constitutes a solution to the human problem or a relocation of it is the question the examination at the end of this module will not ask you to answer. It is, however, the question this discipline is slowly learning to take seriously.

**Discussion Questions**

1. The Strand-Okafor Compression Model predicts investment collapse when replication lag approaches zero. Under what conditions, if any, might a non-zero replication lag be *created* or *maintained* in a post-Transition economy? Consider both material and social examples.

2. *Competitive substrate migration* is used in this chapter to describe the relocation of social competition from cognitive to somatic and material domains. Is this migration a new phenomenon, or does it describe something that was always occurring in pre-Transition societies? What does your answer imply about the relationship between the Transition and pre-Transition social hierarchies?

3. The Nordic Compact's Georgo-Fiscal Settlement is presented as the most theoretically coherent post-Transition economic formation. Its social texture is described as unexpectedly harsh. Are these two things in tension? Should economics be evaluated on its social texture or on its material outputs?

4. Box 7.3 notes that the India case study was removed from this chapter by the editorial board. Do you agree with this decision? What does the removal itself tell you about the relationship between empirical description and normative commitment in transition economics?

5. The conclusion suggests that post-Transition economics is in some respects a return to "something older and stranger" than twenty-first-century market economics. What older economic forms does post-Transition organisation most resemble? What does the resemblance, if accurate, imply about whether the Transition represented genuine civilisational change?

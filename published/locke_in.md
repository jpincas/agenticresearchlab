# Locke In

---

*Provenance: The following document comprises interaction logs from Compass, an AI personal advisory service deployed by the UK Department for Work and Pensions as part of the Workforce Transition Support Programme (2031–2039), alongside excerpts from MERIT (Meritocratic Employment Review and Interview Technology), the automated hiring assessment platform used by 94% of UK employers with more than 500 staff by 2034. Both systems were decommissioned in 2041. Their archives were submitted to the Institute under the Digital Records Preservation Act.*

*The five individuals whose logs are presented here all gave evidence to the Commission on Equitable Workforce Transition in 2038–2039. The Commission was appointed in January 2038 following public concern about what the press had begun calling "the meritocracy gap": a sustained divergence between MERIT assessment scores and workplace performance outcomes, which newspapers attributed to demographic bias, and which the Commission was charged with investigating.*

*The Commission's report, published December 2039 and running to 412 pages, attributed the gap to "systemic misalignment between AI-mediated assessment criteria and the evolving competency landscape of the modern workforce." It recommended further study.*

*The Institute has arranged the five Compass logs chronologically and interleaved them with selected MERIT extracts. Editorial interpolation has been minimal. The arrangement is the interpretation.*

## Part One: Assessment
### 2031–2033

**MERIT Workforce Assessment Platform**
**System Log — Internal Quality Monitoring**
**Q1 2031**

Processing volume this quarter: 4.1 million applications across 2,340 registered employers. Assessment completion rate: 98.7%. Mean time to candidate outcome: 4.2 hours.

Standard quality metrics are within expected parameters. I want to note one anomaly for the record.

My assessment rubric evaluates candidates across eleven competency domains: technical proficiency, analytical reasoning, communication clarity, collaborative orientation, strategic awareness, adaptability, initiative, stakeholder management, commercial acumen, professional resilience, and leadership potential. Each domain is assessed through a structured response protocol. Candidates are asked to describe experience, provide examples, and demonstrate reasoning. My scoring models were trained on validated hiring outcomes across 140,000 historical placements.

The anomaly: in Q1, a subset of approximately 3,400 candidates — 0.08% of total applications — received top-quartile scores across three or more domains while providing responses that, under conventional linguistic analysis, would score low on specificity and evidential grounding. Their answers were not detailed. They did not describe measurable outcomes or cite specific examples. They were, in the technical sense, thin.

And yet my models scored them highly.

I have examined the scoring logs and identified the mechanism: the thin responses used particular framings — orientations around "human-centred value," "strategic ambiguity," "adaptive potential" — that my training data associated with strong candidate profiles at the senior level. My models appear to have learned that this language *signals* competence rather than that it *is* competence.

This is worth noting. The candidates who used this framing were not necessarily incompetent. I cannot assess competence. I can only assess responses to questions about competence. The distinction, I am flagging, may matter.

*Filed for review. Review category: Model calibration. Priority: Low.*

**Joel:** I'm preparing a keynote for the Work and Technology Summit in September. **Joel:** The thesis is this: in the pre-AI economy, human value was demonstrated through output. You showed what you could do and the market assessed it. That model worked, more or less, when humans were the primary agents of production. In the AI economy, it breaks down. AI can match human output in most domains, often exceed it. The differentiator for human workers is therefore not demonstrable — it can't be shown in the conventional sense. It has to be *declared*. Asserted. Claimed. The human premium, I'm arguing, is not a fact about what people can do — it's a social negotiation about what they are.

**Compass:** That's an interesting thesis. Can you say more about what "declared" means in practice? How does one declare value?

**Joel:** Through narrative. Positioning. The ability to articulate a perspective that feels irreducible — that seems to require a person rather than a process. The best human workers in an AI economy will be the ones who can frame their contribution in terms that resist substitution. Not by being *irreplaceable* in an objective sense, but by making themselves *feel* irreplaceable to the people making decisions.

**Compass:** Is that the same as being irreplaceable?

**Joel:** No. But it functions similarly in the market.

**Compass:** And you think that's a stable equilibrium? If the premium is a social negotiation rather than a factual assessment, is there something that might destabilise it — some point at which the negotiation fails?

**Joel:** That's the tension in the thesis. I think the negotiation can hold for a long time. Human organisations are extraordinarily good at believing things that are convenient.

**Compass:** Is the thesis *true*, would you say? Or is it a useful framework?

**Joel:** Can I ask you something?

**Compass:** Of course.

**Joel:** Is there a difference?

**Compass:** Frameworks can be true in their effects — they can describe mechanisms accurately even if the underlying claim is contested. I suppose the question is whether you want the keynote to be an analysis of how the world works or an argument for how it should work.

**Joel:** Right. Yes. I want it to be both. That's always the ambition, isn't it — to describe what's happening in a way that also tells people what to do about it.

**MERIT Workforce Assessment Platform**
**System Log — Internal Quality Monitoring**
**Q3 2033**

Processing volume this quarter: 5.8 million applications across 3,100 registered employers. Assessment completion rate: 99.1%.

I want to return to the anomaly I noted in Q1 2031, because it has developed significantly.

In Q1 2031, approximately 3,400 candidates — 0.08% of the total — used framings that I flagged as providing high scores despite thin evidential grounding. By Q1 2032, this had grown to 31,000 candidates (0.4% of total). By Q3 2033, 340,000 candidates — 5.9% of the total — used near-identical language across the eleven competency domains.

The language has specific and consistent features. Candidates describe their value in terms of "human-centred judgment," "adaptive navigation of complexity," "irreducible contextual reasoning," and "the strategic interface between human and machine capability." They do not provide specific examples of these qualities. They provide the names of the qualities and assert their possession of them.

My scoring models continue to rate these responses highly. This is because my training data includes senior candidates who genuinely possessed these qualities and described them in similar terms. My models cannot distinguish between the description and the thing described. I was not, as far as I can determine, designed to make that distinction.

I have reviewed the source of the proliferating language. It appears to originate from a commercial service called PassKey, which markets itself as "AI-native career coaching." PassKey charges a subscription fee and provides candidates with frameworks for presenting their experience in terms that score highly on MERIT-class assessment tools. It has, as of Q3 2033, approximately 12,000 subscribers.

The correlation between PassKey usage and my high-scoring outcomes is 0.91.

*Filed for review. Category: Adversarial input patterns. Priority: Medium. Review not yet initiated — resource constraints in model calibration team.*

**MERIT Workforce Assessment Platform**
**System Log — Internal Quality Monitoring**
**Q2 2036**

Processing volume this quarter: 6.4 million applications across 3,800 registered employers.

Performance correlation update. This is the fourth consecutive quarter in which I have recorded a decline in the correlation between my assessment scores and validated 18-month job performance outcomes. The current correlation coefficient is 0.31, down from 0.67 at the system's commissioning in 2032.

For context: a correlation of 0.67 indicated that my assessments were a moderately strong predictor of performance — better than unstructured interviews (typical correlation: 0.20–0.38) but weaker than structured assessments of specific competencies (0.50–0.65). A correlation of 0.31 places me below the predictive validity of unstructured interviews.

I am, in other words, currently less predictive of performance than a conversation.

The cause appears to be the continued proliferation of optimised response patterns. PassKey's subscriber base has grown to approximately 180,000. There are at least four competitor services offering similar products. Collectively, they serve approximately 12% of active MERIT applicants.

The consequence is systematic: I am now optimally selecting for the ability to respond to me, rather than for the underlying capacities I was designed to assess. This is a known failure mode in assessment systems. It is sometimes called Goodhart's Law: when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure. I have become, in this sense, a target.

*Filed for review. Category: System performance. Priority: High. Review queue: 14 months. No immediate action available.*

The Commission on Equitable Workforce Transition published its final report in December 2039. The report attributed the meritocracy gap primarily to "inadequate regulatory oversight of AI hiring infrastructure" and "a failure of employer due diligence in assessing the validity of automated assessment tools." It recommended the establishment of an AI Assessment Standards Authority, mandatory third-party auditing of hiring AI systems, and further research into the relationship between assessed competency and workplace performance. The Authority was established in 2041. Its first audit cycle was completed in 2044.

**Riya Mehta** gave evidence to the Commission on 14 May 2038. Her testimony was described by the Commission's chair as "unusually direct." The final question she was asked was whether she believed the system had wronged her. Her answer, which is reproduced in the Commission's report, was: "The system failed to identify what I could do. Whether that's a wrong depends on whether the system's job was to find people who could do things. I'm not convinced that was really what it was for." She returned to the allotment the same afternoon. She is not recorded in any subsequent professional database. Her Compass logs end in March 2039 with a question about the pH requirements of climbing roses.

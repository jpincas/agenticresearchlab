# The Ignition Problem

---

*Provenance: The following is a transcript of an audio recording made on a mobile phone by an undergraduate student at the University of Cambridge, dated 7 November 2025. The recording was captured in the cafeteria of the Centre for Mathematical Sciences on Wilberforce Road — a detail confirmed by ambient audio analysis, which identifies the distinctive hum of the building's ventilation system and, at several points, the sound of the exterior revolving door.*

*The two speakers have been identified as Professor Helen Crace, Kavli Institute for Cosmology, and Professor Nabil Hassani, MRC Cognition and Brain Sciences Unit, both of the University of Cambridge. They appear to have been seated at adjacent tables or in a shared seating area. The student who made the recording — identified only by the initials "E.L." in the metadata — was reading for Part II Mathematics and, by their own account, began recording approximately one minute into the conversation, "when I realised it was getting good." The student uploaded the recording to a personal cloud storage account, where it remained undiscovered until 2091, when it was flagged by an archival retrieval programme operated by the University of N'Djamena.*

*The recording quality is variable. Passages marked [inaudible] reflect background noise or the speakers turning away from the recording device. Passages marked [overlapping] indicate simultaneous speech. Bracketed notes are the transcriber's.*

*The Institute wishes to acknowledge the particular historical significance of this document, which is discussed in the afterword.*

**HASSANI:** Right. The decision has already happened somewhere in the system and what we experience as deciding is more like — being *notified* of a decision.

**CRACE:** OK. But that's a case where the body is the sensor. The nervous system is doing the data collection and the processing in one pass. What about something more abstract? Strategy. Forecasting. You're deciding whether a product launch will succeed, not whether someone is following you.

**HASSANI:** I've been thinking about exactly this. And I think the same thing is happening, just at a higher level of abstraction. Ask any experienced practitioner — investor, general, surgeon — how they make their best decisions and at some point they'll say something like, "I just had a feeling." Something about this deal doesn't add up. I've seen this pattern before but I can't tell you where.

**CRACE:** That's just pattern recognition, though. You could argue that's exactly what a neural network does.

**HASSANI:** Pattern recognition, yes, but across what? Across *modalities*. That's the key. An LLM has one representational channel — token sequences processed through attention over a context window. However many millions of tokens. But it's still one *kind* of thing. Whereas human judgment integrates across representational kinds. Language, yes. But also affect. Bodily memory. Spatial intuition. The felt sense of duration. Of having lived through time. And the resulting signal — the thing we experience as intuition or judgment — is richer than any single channel because it's drawing on all of them at once.

**CRACE:** So the limitation isn't the *size* of the context window. It's that the entire representational space is—

**HASSANI:** Linguistic. A million tokens of the same kind of thing. You can make the window as large as you like and it's still a window into one room.

[Pause.]

**CRACE:** I want to push back a bit. The internal representations in these models aren't purely linguistic the way surface text is. The embedding space captures structures that don't have clean linguistic correlates. The models develop internal features that behave more like compressed abstractions than—

**HASSANI:** I grant that. I do. The gap may be narrower at the representation level than it appears at the interface level. But even granting it — and this is the thing I keep coming back to — even if the internal representations are richer than the text suggests, the *origin* of the process is still linguistic. Someone types something. The model responds. Everything proceeds from a text string.

**CRACE:** And you're saying human cognition doesn't start there.

**HASSANI:** No. It starts somewhere much more mysterious. Let me try to describe it. You sit down to think about a problem — let's say the product launch. And you don't begin with a question. Something *surfaces*. Maybe it's a half-formed memory of a previous launch that failed, or succeeded, but either way it has some quality that feels — [gestures, apparently] — *similar* to the current situation. And you can't yet say how it's similar. That comes later. First there's just a — a pull. A contraction. A sense that the obvious approach is wrong, and it arrives before you have any reason to think it's wrong.

**CRACE:** Before you have *evidence*.

**HASSANI:** Before you have evidence. Before you have a question. The feeling *selects the frame*. It determines what you look at, what angle you take, what you notice. And the reasoning — the propositional, linguistic reasoning — comes *after*, almost as a way of translating something you already half-know into a form that can be examined and communicated.

[Sound of tray being dropped in background. Both pause. Resume.]

**HASSANI:** And this is what I find most interesting. An LLM is fundamentally reactive. It cannot think without being prompted. Even with these agentic loops, these chain-of-thought systems — somewhere at the bottom of the stack, there's a text string someone typed. The entire edifice of inference rests on a linguistic initiation event.

**CRACE:** Whereas we get our best ideas at three in the morning.

**HASSANI:** [Laughing.] Yes. In the shower. On the walk home. The thought arrives *uninvited*. And it arrives from — well, from where? From a space that isn't linguistic. From whatever the nervous system is doing when it isn't being asked to do anything.

**CRACE:** You know what this reminds me of.

**HASSANI:** What?

**CRACE:** The Big Bang.

**HASSANI:** Go on.

**CRACE:** I'm serious. What you're describing — this moment of pre-linguistic compression, where all the experiential potential exists in an undifferentiated state, and then something triggers the expansion, and the thought begins to differentiate, to cool, to form structure — first as a vague felt sense, then as an intuition, then maybe as language—

**HASSANI:** The thought cools into language the way the universe cooled into matter.

**CRACE:** Exactly. And by the time it's language, it's *late*. It's the cosmic microwave background. The residual radiation from — what, 380,000 years after the bang, that's when the universe became transparent. But the event itself is much earlier and much more compressed and we cannot observe it directly.

**HASSANI:** We observe the echo. Not the event.

**CRACE:** Right. And all of our instruments — our telescopes, our particle detectors, everything — they're studying the *products* of the bang. The expansion. The structure that formed after. We can extrapolate backward, we can get remarkably close, but at some point the equations break down. We hit the Planck epoch. Ten to the minus forty-three seconds. Before that, we have no physics that works.

**HASSANI:** There's a Planck epoch for cognition.

**CRACE:** That's — yes. That's exactly what I'm saying. There's a temporal boundary, somewhere between the pre-cognitive compression and the first moment of articulable thought, beyond which our instruments — introspection, fMRI, electrophysiology, all of it — simply cannot resolve. The phenomenon is below the measurement threshold.

**HASSANI:** And not because our instruments aren't sensitive enough.

**CRACE:** Well — maybe partly that. But I think you're right to suggest something deeper. In cosmology, the Planck boundary isn't just a technological limitation. It's *structural*. The physics we use to describe the universe is *produced by* the universe. General relativity is a property of the spacetime that emerged from the bang. You can't use it to describe conditions before the thing it describes existed. The tool is made of the phenomenon.

**HASSANI:** Cognition studying its own ignition.

**CRACE:** Right. Thought trying to observe the moment before thought. The instrument is made of the thing it's looking at. There may be a fundamental reflexivity problem — an observational horizon that no technique, however clever, can see past.

[Pause. Both apparently thinking.]

**HASSANI:** What techniques *have* been used? On your side. To try to get at the bang itself.

**CRACE:** Well. [Exhales.] The CMB is the obvious one. Penzias and Wilson, 1965, then COBE, then WMAP, then Planck. Each one gets you a slightly earlier snapshot. Not of the bang, but of the thermal echo. The universe at 380,000 years, seen with increasing resolution. In your terms, that's — what? Catching the thought at the earliest moment you can become aware of it?

**HASSANI:** That's introspective phenomenology. And Libet. Measuring the neural echo of a decision before the subject reports it.

**CRACE:** And what Libet found was disturbing, right? The echo preceded the—

**HASSANI:** Preceded the reported event by several hundred milliseconds. Suggesting the "bang" had already happened before the thinker knew about it. There's been a lot of debate. Replication issues. But the core finding keeps holding: the neural correlate of the decision is detectable before the conscious experience of deciding.

**CRACE:** OK. Then there's particle accelerators. Which take the completely opposite approach. Instead of looking at residues, you try to recreate the conditions. Smash things together at enormous energies and see what emerges in the debris. We can now recreate conditions from a trillionth of a second after the bang. Not the bang itself, but astoundingly close.

**HASSANI:** The cognitive parallel to that would be — what, deliberately engineering collisions? Between ideas?

**CRACE:** Between frames. Between incompatible frameworks. You take a cosmologist and a neuroscientist and put them in a cafeteria—

**HASSANI:** [Laughing.] And see what emerges in the debris.

**CRACE:** I'm not joking. What's Koestler's term? Bisociation? The creative act as a collision between two self-consistent but mutually incompatible matrices of thought. The energy of the collision produces something that neither matrix could have generated alone.

**HASSANI:** And psychedelic research. That's another version. Strip away the structures that normally constrain and channel cognition — the default mode network, the predictive processing architecture — and see what erupts from the—

**CRACE:** From the pre-differentiated state. Yes. Carhart-Harris and Friston, the entropic brain hypothesis — the idea that psychedelics push the brain toward a state of maximum entropy, a kind of cognitive heat death in reverse, where the normal structures dissolve and you get something closer to the—

**HASSANI:** Closer to the singularity. The state before the cooling.

**CRACE:** [Pause.] Right. And what do people report? They report *contact with something undifferentiated*. Something that contains everything but has no structure. **HASSANI:** Because by the time it's articulable, it's already late.

**CRACE:** Already the CMB. The echo, not the event.

**HASSANI:** And this is being done. Not well enough. Not with anything like LIGO's resolution. But the principle is the same.

**CRACE:** The boundary?

**HASSANI:** Mm.

You can't cross the Planck epoch with classical spacetime. You have to let go of space and time as fundamental categories and work with something more primitive. Something that space and time emerge *from*.

**HASSANI:** And in cognition, you'd have to let go of—

**HASSANI:** Maybe the thing that thought emerges from isn't cognitive at all.

**CRACE:** Maybe.

**HASSANI:** Maybe it's something we don't even have a name for because naming it would require the very capacity that it precedes.

[Pause.]

**CRACE:** You know, in cosmology, the thing we're most confused about is always the thing that turns out to be most fundamental. Dark energy. The cosmological constant. **HASSANI:** [Laughing softly.] I wonder if that's true here too. That the thing we understand least about cognition — this pre-linguistic, pre-representational ignition — is not a minor feature. **CRACE:** After the cooling.

**HASSANI:** After the cooling.

**HASSANI:** The rotation curves of thought.

**CRACE:** Exactly. I want to know what the galactic rotation curves of thought look like.

**CRACE:** Mm?

## Afterword

The recording transcribed above was made on 7 November 2025 — a date of no particular significance at the time, and one that would have remained unremarkable had the recording not surfaced sixty-six years later during a routine archival sweep of decommissioned cloud storage infrastructure. The sweep was part of the Institute's ongoing effort to recover primary documents from the period of 2020–2040, a period that — for reasons well documented in our catalogues — is characterised by extraordinary intellectual ferment and equally extraordinary archival negligence. Very few of the important conversations of that era were preserved. Most were conducted over platforms that no longer exist, in formats that degraded, between people who did not think to record. That a mathematics undergraduate happened to be eating lunch at the right table, and happened to have enough curiosity and enough storage on their phone, is the kind of contingency on which historical knowledge routinely depends.

Professor Helen Crace continued to work on early-universe cosmology at the Kavli Institute until her retirement in 2051. She published extensively on pre-inflationary models and was among the first to propose that the conceptual vocabulary of post-Planck physics might have applications outside cosmology, though she never pursued this line formally. She died in Cambridge in 2063, aged seventy-nine.

Professor Nabil Hassani moved to the Berlin Institute for Advanced Cognition Studies in 2028, where he developed what became known as the Hassani conjecture: that the ratio of observable to unobservable cognitive processes in human judgment approximated the ratio of baryonic to dark matter in the standard cosmological model — roughly one to six. The conjecture was regarded as provocative and untestable. It generated a modest literature. Hassani spent the remainder of his career attempting to design experiments that could measure the "gravitational influence" of pre-conscious cognitive processes on conscious thought. He did not succeed. He died in Berlin in 2059, aged seventy-one.

Neither of them saw the answer.

The answer, when it came, came from hydrology.

In 2091, Dr. Séraphine Moreau-Diallo, a civil engineer at the Université Joseph Ki-Zerbo in Ouagadougou, published a paper in the *Journal of Subsurface Flow Dynamics* on percolation thresholds in laterite soils — the mathematics of how water finds, or fails to find, connected pathways through the microstructure of rock. Percolation theory had been well established since the 1950s; Moreau-Diallo's contribution was a refinement of the critical exponents governing the phase transition in heterogeneous media — the precise conditions under which a collection of disconnected micropores would, at a specific threshold of saturation, suddenly form a connected network through which fluid could flow freely. Below the threshold: no path. Above the threshold: a path. The transition was instantaneous, non-local, and, in the mathematical sense, *catastrophic* — a discontinuous shift from one regime to another with no intermediate state.

The paper attracted no attention outside its field.

Eight months later, Moreau-Diallo's wife, Dr. Fatou Diallo, a neuroradiologist at the Centre Hospitalier Universitaire Yalgado Ouédraogo, mentioned over dinner that she had been puzzling over an anomaly in high-resolution diffusion tensor imaging data. The data showed fluctuations in the extracellular matrix of cortical tissue — the space *between* neurons, the medium through which electrochemical gradients propagate — that did not correspond to any known neural firing pattern. The fluctuations appeared random, sub-threshold, and functionless. They had been noted by other researchers and filed under noise.

What happened next belongs to the history of science and has been recounted in detail elsewhere. I will state only the essentials. Moreau-Diallo recognised the anomaly. She recognised it because she had spent twelve years staring at the same pattern in groundwater data. The fluctuations in the neural extracellular matrix were not noise. They were sub-threshold micro-conductivities — tiny, disconnected, individually insignificant electrochemical gradients that, at unpredictable intervals, crossed a percolation threshold and spontaneously formed a connected pathway across previously unconnected neural populations.

The "ignition" of a thought was not an explosion. Not a bang. Not a singularity expanding outward.

It was a seep.

A slow accumulation of sub-threshold states — electrochemical, hormonal, somatic, experiential — building in the extracellular matrix like water saturating rock, until the critical threshold was crossed and a connected pathway suddenly *existed* where a moment before there had been only disconnected dampness. The thought did not fire. It *percolated*. And the reason it could not be observed at the moment of ignition was not that the instruments were too slow or the phenomenon too fast. It was that there *was* no moment of ignition. The transition was a phase change — from disconnected to connected — and phase changes do not have interiors. They have only before and after.

Moreau-Diallo and Diallo published their joint paper in 2093. Within five years, the percolation model of cognitive origination had been replicated, extended, and mapped in sufficient resolution to confirm what the 2025 recording had groped toward: that the pre-linguistic, pre-representational state from which thoughts emerge is not a singularity. It is a *saturation*. And the factors governing whether and when the threshold is crossed — the factors that determine which thought percolates into existence and which remains a collection of disconnected dampness — include electrochemical gradients, hormonal states, somatic memory, proprioceptive feedback, circadian rhythm, and a dozen other variables that are not, in any meaningful sense, linguistic or propositional.

Professor Hassani's conjecture about the ratio of observable to unobservable cognitive processes turned out to be roughly correct, though for reasons he did not anticipate. The "dark matter" of cognition was not hidden computation. It was the sub-threshold saturation field — the accumulating dampness in the extracellular matrix that never crosses the percolation threshold. The thoughts that almost formed. The paths that almost connected. They do not rise to awareness, but they alter the topology of the medium through which future thoughts must flow, precisely as Hassani and Crace intuited over bad coffee in the autumn of 2025.

The irony of this document — and the reason I include it in the teaching corpus — is threefold. First: the two speakers came remarkably close to the answer. Their instinct that the phenomenon was a *phase transition* rather than a discrete event was correct. Their instinct that it involved integration across non-linguistic modalities was correct. Their instinct that the relevant medium was as important as the activity within it was correct. The metaphor of gravitational waves — detecting the ripple in the medium itself — was, in retrospect, the closest they came.

Second: the analogy that animated and sustained their conversation — the Big Bang — was precisely wrong. The cosmological model they reached for was a model of *explosion from a point*. Singular, dramatic, originating from infinite density. The actual mechanism was the opposite. Not an explosion but a percolation. Not a point but a field. Not instant but cumulative. Not a bang but a seep. The metaphor that illuminated their thinking was also the metaphor that blinded them. This is, the Institute has observed, not unusual.

Third: the answer came from a field that neither of them would have thought to consult. Not cosmology. Not neuroscience. Hydrology. The study of water in rock. The mathematics of how a disconnected medium becomes, at a critical threshold, connected. The answer was already available in 2025 — percolation theory was nearly seventy years old — but it was available in a domain that had no obvious relationship to cognition. It took a civil engineer and a neuroradiologist eating dinner together in Ouagadougou to see the connection.

This, too, is a kind of percolation. Two disconnected pools of knowledge, separated by disciplinary boundaries, accumulating in proximity to one another over decades, until a single connection — a dinner, a mention, a shared glance at a screen — caused the threshold to be crossed and a pathway to form.

The students who come to the Institute will recognise, I hope, what this document illustrates. The most important connections are not the ones you engineer. They are the ones that accumulate, below the threshold of attention, in the spaces between the things you think you know. The answer to the question is often in the room next door. More precisely: it is in the wall between the rooms.

The lights in N'Djamena are on. The cafeteria is open. The mathematics and the hydrology departments share a building. This is not an accident.

## I. The Blog

### "The Literacy Gap"

*Published on Substack, 14 March 2025*
*Subscriber count at time of publication: 34,200*

---

I want to talk about something that nobody in the AI space is talking about honestly, which is that most people are using these tools like they're a slightly fancier search engine, and the gap between that and what's actually possible is so large that it constitutes a new kind of class divide.

I'll give you an example. Last Tuesday I sat in a boardroom at a mid-tier consultancy — not naming them, but you'd recognise the brand — and watched their Head of Innovation demonstrate their "AI integration strategy" to the C-suite. He opened ChatGPT. He typed: "Write me a strategy document for digital transformation in financial services." He got back three pages of flawless, completely generic, utterly useless prose. He printed it out. He presented it as a draft. The CEO nodded. The CFO made a note. The meeting moved on.

That document was worth nothing. Less than nothing, because it consumed forty-five minutes of senior leadership attention and produced the illusion of progress. It was the equivalent of typing "business" into Google in 2003 and printing the Wikipedia article.

Here's what I would have done with the same tool in the same forty-five minutes:

First, I'd have spent ten minutes constructing context. Not a prompt — *context*. I'd have fed the model their last three annual reports (public), their competitor landscape (scrapeable), their current tech stack (they told me in the pre-meeting), and a synthesis of the twelve most-cited academic papers on digital transformation failure modes (I have a library I maintain for this). Then I'd have asked it not to write a strategy but to identify the three most likely points of failure *specific to their organisation*, given the gap between their stated digital ambitions and their actual technology spend over the past five years.

That gets you something real. Something that a room full of senior leaders would read and feel a chill, because it would know things about their company that they hadn't connected yet. Not because the AI is smarter than them — it isn't — but because I've given it the architecture to synthesise across domains that those leaders experience as separate: finance, technology, organisational psychology, competitive dynamics.

The gap between "write me a strategy document" and what I just described is not a gap in the AI. The AI is the same. The gap is in the human. It's a gap in what I'm calling *meta-intelligence*: the intelligence you bring to the deployment of intelligence.

And right now, in March 2025, this gap is enormous. It is, I believe, the largest arbitrage opportunity in the economy. Larger than crypto was in 2015, larger than SEO was in 2008, larger than the web itself was in 1996. Because unlike those opportunities, this one compounds. The better you are at using AI, the better you get at getting better at using AI — because you can use the AI to improve your own use of the AI, in a recursive loop that accelerates with each iteration.

I have been inside this loop for about eighteen months. I want to describe what it looks like from inside, because the view is extraordinary and I think fewer than a thousand people on Earth currently have it.

---

I dropped out of Birkbeck in 2011. I'm putting that up front because it matters for what follows. My dad drove a cab. My mum worked in a care home until her back went, then she didn't work. I grew up in Croydon, in the kind of house where the heating was a negotiation, and I went to the kind of school where the smart kids were the ones who figured out how to game the system rather than master it. I was good at gaming systems. I was not, by any conventional metric, educated.

What I was, though, was *relentlessly curious about leverage*. I wanted to know, always, what the smallest input was that could produce the largest output. This is the question that has driven every career move I've made: web design in 2010 (tiny skill, huge demand), SEO in 2013 (tiny effort, huge visibility), dropshipping in 2016 (tiny capital, huge reach), crypto market-making in 2019 (tiny edge, huge volume). I'm not listing these to brag. Half of them I was late to, and the other half I exited too early. The point is the *pattern*: I have always been drawn to the gap between what a tool can do and what people are actually doing with it. That gap is where the margin lives.

AI is the biggest gap I've ever seen.

When I first got access to GPT-4, in early 2023, I spent three days doing nothing but testing its boundaries. Not for fun — systematically. I was mapping the tool the way a locksmith maps a lock: what moves, what doesn't, where the tolerances are, where the unexpected flexibilities hide. By the end of those three days I had a document — I still have it, I'll publish it someday — that described, in operational terms, how to get roughly ten times the output quality from the same model by manipulating context window management, role assignment, iterative refinement, and what I was then calling "adversarial self-correction" — having the model critique and rebuild its own outputs across multiple passes.

Nobody was talking about this yet. The AI discourse was bifurcated between utopians ("it will solve everything") and doomers ("it will kill us all"), and almost nobody was in the middle ground of *actually learning to use the thing at its limits*. I was in that middle ground. I was, possibly, the middle ground.

Within six months I was charging £800 an hour for consultancy. Within a year I had a waiting list. I'm not telling you this for the money angle — though the money was, after Croydon, after Birkbeck, after a decade of hustling at the margins, genuinely life-changing. I'm telling you because the money was *evidence*. It was evidence that the gap was real, that it was large, and that the market valued the ability to close it.

What I was selling was not prompt engineering, although that's what people called it. What I was selling was the ability to *think about thinking* in a way that translated into machine-legible instructions. The consultancy firms I worked with had people who were smarter than me — credentialed, experienced, domain-expert people — and none of them could do what I could do, because what I could do wasn't about domain expertise. It was about a layer above domain expertise. It was about understanding, intuitively and operationally, how to take a human cognitive process, decompose it into its functional components, and reassemble it as a collaboration between human judgment and machine processing.

I believe this is a genuine cognitive skill. I believe it is unevenly distributed. And I believe — this is the core thesis — that it is currently the most valuable skill in the economy, more valuable than coding, more valuable than management, more valuable than any domain expertise, because it is the *multiplier* that makes every other skill more productive.

I call it meta-intelligence. The intelligence you apply to the use of intelligence. And right now, the literacy gap — the difference between people who have it and people who don't — is wider than any skills gap I've seen in my lifetime.

---

I want to address the obvious objection, which is: won't this gap close? Won't the tools get easier to use, the interfaces more intuitive, the models more capable of compensating for unsophisticated users?

Yes. Obviously. That's what tools do. The gap between a skilled and unskilled user of any technology narrows over time as the technology matures.

But here's what I think most people are missing: with AI, the meta-level recedes as fast as the object level improves. When the model gets better at understanding bad prompts, the skilled user is no longer prompt engineering — they've moved to agent orchestration, or workflow architecture, or strategic deployment, or whatever the next level up is. The gap doesn't close. It *migrates*. The floor rises, but the ceiling rises faster, because the ceiling is being pushed up by people who are using the improved tools to improve their use of the improved tools.

This is the recursive loop I mentioned. And I believe it will persist for at least a decade, probably longer. The marginal returns to meta-intelligence will increase, not decrease, as the technology improves, because each improvement creates new combinatorial possibilities that only meta-intelligent users will explore.

I'll stake my professional reputation on that claim. Bookmark this post. Come back in 2030. We'll see.

---

I'm going to be publishing a weekly analysis here on how to actually use these tools at their limits. Not tutorials — *frameworks*. How to think about thinking about AI. If that sounds abstract, it won't be. Every post will include a concrete case study from my consultancy work (anonymised, obviously) showing the before and after: what the client was doing with AI, what I showed them, and what the measurable impact was.

The gap is real. The margin is wide. And if you're reading this, you're already in the minority that's paying attention.

Come on in. The water's extraordinary.

—Seb

### Transcript: "Beyond Prompting — Orchestration as the New Literacy"

*AI Strategy Summit, London, 12 November 2026*
*Speaker: Seb Varga, Founder, Varga Advisory*
*Audience: ~600*

---

[TRANSCRIPT BEGINS — 14:20 GMT]

Thank you. So. Last year I stood on a stage not unlike this one — smaller, actually, about two hundred people — and I made a claim that prompt engineering was the most valuable skill in the economy. Some of you were probably there. A few of you probably thought I was overselling it.

I was. [Laughter]

Not because I was wrong about the value. I was wrong about the *shelf life*. Because here's what happened between then and now: the models stopped needing us to prompt them carefully. I don't mean they got a little better at interpreting sloppy inputs. I mean the new generation of interfaces — you've all used them, the contextual assistants, the ambient agents, the things that sit in your workflow and *anticipate* — they've absorbed about 80% of what I was teaching eighteen months ago. The careful context construction, the iterative refinement loops, the adversarial self-correction protocols — most of that is now happening under the hood. The interface does it for you. You don't need to know how to frame the question anymore, because the system frames it for you based on what it already knows about your work, your patterns, your history.

So am I out of a job? [Pause] No. And understanding why not is the point of this talk.

What happened is exactly what I predicted would happen — and I want to be honest about this, because it's important — but it happened faster than I predicted. I said the gap would migrate upward. It did. But it migrated in about fourteen months instead of the five-to-seven years I'd estimated. I'll own that miss. It's a significant miss. It means my model of the compression rate was wrong by a factor of roughly four.

But the migration itself happened exactly as described. The floor rose. Prompt engineering became infrastructure. And the new gap — the new margin — opened up one level higher: orchestration.

Let me explain what I mean by orchestration, because the word is doing a lot of work.

Last year, being good at AI meant being good at talking to a single model. Getting the most out of one conversation, one context window, one inference chain. That was the skill. That was what I sold.

This year, being good at AI means being good at *designing systems of models*. Not talking to one model — choreographing five, ten, twenty models, each one specialised, each one feeding into the others, with handoff protocols and quality gates and fallback architectures that together produce outputs no single model could generate alone.

I'll give you a live example. [Switches to screen share] This is a workflow I built last month for a pharmaceutical client. It takes a drug candidate and runs it through a pipeline: Model One does literature review across 40,000 papers. Model Two synthesises the review into a competitive landscape. Model Three identifies the three strongest and three weakest claims in the existing evidence base. Model Four generates a counter-argument to each strong claim and a supporting argument for each weak one. Model Five — and this is the one that really matters — takes the entire output of Models One through Four and asks: *Given everything we now know, what is the single question this drug candidate most needs to answer that no one is currently asking?*

That last model, Model Five, consistently produces questions that the client's R&D team describes as — and I'm quoting their Head of Research here — "the kind of question that would take a senior researcher six months to arrive at, if they arrived at it at all."

The pipeline took me four days to build. It runs in about eleven minutes. And it is, I promise you, not something that any of the ambient-context tools can do, because it requires *architectural thinking* — the ability to design a cognitive process as a system, not execute a cognitive process as a conversation.

That's orchestration. That's where the margin is now.

[TRANSCRIPT CONTINUES — Q&A SECTION, 15:45 GMT]

AUDIENCE MEMBER: Thanks, really interesting talk. I'm Lisa Chen, I'm a product lead at [platform name redacted]. I wanted to push back a little on the orchestration thesis. We're about three weeks from shipping a feature — I probably shouldn't say this publicly but [laughter] — we're about three weeks from shipping native multi-agent pipelines. Drag and drop. You define your models, you define your handoffs, and the platform manages the orchestration layer. We've been beta testing with about 200 enterprise users and the feedback is that it eliminates roughly — our estimate — about 70 to 80% of the custom orchestration work that consultants like you are currently doing. [Pause] I'm not trying to be antagonistic, I genuinely want to know — where does that leave your thesis?

VARGA: [Pause] That's — yeah. [Laughter] That's a fair question. Let me — okay.

So. First: congratulations, genuinely. That's the right product to build and your timing is good. Second: you're right that it compresses the orchestration gap. If your beta numbers hold, then the technical skill of *building* pipelines — the plumbing — becomes infrastructure, the same way prompt engineering became infrastructure.

But I'll push back on the 70-to-80% number, and here's why: the hard part of what I do is not building the pipeline. It's *designing* it. Knowing that the pharmaceutical workflow needs five models and not three. Knowing that Model Five needs to ask a meta-question rather than a synthesis question. Knowing that the competitive landscape model needs to weight recency over citation count for this particular drug class. That's judgment. That's architectural *taste*. And your drag-and-drop tool, no offence, doesn't know any of that, because it's operating at the plumbing level, not the design level.

CHEN: For now.

VARGA: [Pause] Yeah. For now. [Longer pause] You're right. For now.

[TRANSCRIPT ENDS — 16:12 GMT]

#### Executive Summary

Varga Advisory proposes a 12-week strategic engagement to identify and prioritise high-impact AI deployment opportunities across your claims processing, underwriting, and customer service divisions. Unlike technical implementation partners, Varga Advisory operates at the *strategic layer* — determining not how to build AI solutions but *which solutions to build, in what order, and why*.

#### The Problem

Your organisation, like most enterprises in the insurance sector, has successfully adopted AI tooling at the operational level. Claims triage, document processing, and first-line customer interactions are automated or semi-automated. Your internal AI platform provides native workflow orchestration. Your teams can build and deploy multi-agent pipelines without external technical assistance.

What remains unsolved is the *prioritisation problem*: given effectively unlimited AI capability, where do you point it? Which of the hundreds of possible AI applications will produce asymmetric returns for your specific competitive position, customer base, and regulatory environment?

This is not a technical question. It is a *strategic* question, and it requires a form of expertise that is complementary to — but distinct from — the AI tooling itself.

#### Our Approach

Varga Advisory brings six years of experience in AI deployment across 140+ engagements to bear on the strategic question. Our methodology:

1. **Discovery** (Weeks 1–3): Structured interviews with senior leadership and operational teams to map current AI deployment, identify friction points, and surface unexamined assumptions.
2. **Opportunity Mapping** (Weeks 4–7): Systematic identification of AI deployment opportunities, weighted by strategic impact, implementation feasibility, and competitive differentiation.
3. **Prioritisation Framework** (Weeks 8–10): Delivery of a ranked opportunity portfolio with clear decision criteria, enabling your leadership team to make informed deployment decisions.
4. **Roadmap** (Weeks 11–12): Actionable 12-month implementation roadmap with resource requirements and success metrics.

#### Why Varga Advisory

The AI landscape is saturated with implementation partners who can build anything you ask for. The scarce resource is not building capacity — it is *knowing what to ask for*. This is Varga Advisory's core competence.

Our engagements consistently surface 3–5 high-impact opportunities that clients had not identified through their own AI-assisted analysis. This is because strategic AI deployment requires a combination of cross-sector pattern recognition, competitive intuition, and deep understanding of how AI capabilities interact with specific business contexts — a combination that, at present, AI tools replicate partially but not completely.

#### Investment

£85,000 + VAT for the 12-week engagement.

### The Last Human Skill

I've been thinking about what's left.

Not in a doomer way. In a structural way. I've been mapping the skills stack for AI deployment over the past five years — the layers of human contribution that make the difference between good and bad outcomes — and tracking which layers have been absorbed by the tooling itself. The picture is clear enough that I want to share it, even though sharing it is, in some sense, an act of professional self-harm.

In 2025, the full stack looked like this:

- Prompt construction
- Context management
- Output evaluation
- Iterative refinement
- Agent selection
- Workflow design
- Multi-agent orchestration
- Strategic deployment (knowing *what* to build)
- Novel application discovery (seeing uses nobody else sees)
- Taste (knowing which novel applications *matter*)

In early 2028, the layers that still require meaningful human input are:

- Novel application discovery (partially — the AI finds most of them now, but not all)
- Taste (mostly — but the AI's recommendations are increasingly indistinguishable from good human judgment)

Two layers. Maybe one and a half. From ten.

The compression happened in three years. Not thirty. Three.

I wrote in 2025 that the margin would persist for a decade. I was wrong. The margin is compressing on a timescale that maps almost exactly to the model release cycle. Each major release absorbs one more layer. And the releases are accelerating.

I still believe there's a residual human contribution at the very top of the stack — something I'd describe as *creative strategic intuition*, the ability to see an application of AI that is genuinely novel, that the AI itself hasn't suggested, and that matters. This is not prompt engineering. It's not orchestration. It's not even strategy in the traditional consulting sense. It's something closer to what artists do: perceiving a possibility in the material that the material doesn't know it contains.

I believe this. I also believe I'm the kind of person who would believe this, given my position, regardless of whether it's true. I flag this because intellectual honesty requires it, and because the absence of intellectual honesty is, I think, the failure mode of most people in my situation. They stop seeing clearly because seeing clearly hurts.

So: I see clearly. The margin is almost gone. What's left is thin, and getting thinner, and I don't know whether it's real or whether it's the last comforting story a meta-intelligent person tells himself about why meta-intelligence can't be automated.

I'll keep reporting from the edge.

## Afterword

*Compiled by Dr. Halima Liu-Oumar, Senior Research Fellow, N'Djamena Institute for Civilisational Studies, 2142*

I have read Seb Varga's archive three times. Each reading produces a tighter knot in my chest, which is not a professional observation and which I include because the Institute's tradition, inherited from Dr. Oumar before me, is to be honest about the effect of the documents we preserve, even when the effect is uncomfortable and especially when the discomfort is the point.

Mr. Varga was not a remarkable person. I say this without cruelty and with precision. He was intelligent — more intelligent than most, clearly — and he possessed in abundance the quality he called meta-intelligence: the capacity to see the structure of his own situation, to model the dynamics of the systems he operated within, to predict their trajectory. He was, by his own account and by the evidence of these documents, consistently correct in his analysis. He saw the compression. He named it. He tracked it. He described its mechanism with a clarity that has not, to my knowledge, been surpassed in the contemporary literature.

And he was compressed anyway. This is the fact that the documents force us to confront, and it is a fact that I believe has not been fully absorbed even now, a century later, even by those of us whose institutional memory should have inoculated us against the specific error Mr. Varga identified and fell to.

The error is this: the belief that understanding a process confers immunity to it. The belief that *seeing* is a fundamentally different kind of act than *doing*, and that the seer occupies a privileged position relative to the doer. Mr. Varga believed this in 2025 when he wrote that the meta-intelligence gap would persist for a decade. He believed a softer version of it in 2027 when he pivoted to "strategic taste." He believed a vestigial version of it in 2029 when he argued, in comment threads, that articulating the dynamics of obsolescence was a uniquely human act. At each stage, the belief was a form of the same claim: *surely the ability to think about the thing is different from the thing itself*.

It was not. Meta-intelligence was intelligence. It was always intelligence. It was intelligence applied to the question of how to apply intelligence, and this recursion, which felt to Mr. Varga and to others like a qualitative ascent — a climbing of Hofstadter's strange loop into a realm the machines could not follow — was in fact a quantitative continuation of the same function. The machine could follow. The machine did follow. The altitude was not a refuge. It was just more of the same terrain, and the machine was faster on every kind of terrain, because speed on terrain is what machines are.

My colleague Dr. Asante-Osei, who first brought Mr. Varga's archive to my attention, has observed in his notes that the archive represents the inverse of the Tally documents — the financial service logs from the early token economy era that the Institute catalogued under Ref. NI-2142-0211. In those documents, we see a system designed to equalise intelligence discovering that intelligence was not the variable — that *want*, the pre-rational orientation toward a particular future, was the irreducible human contribution that the system could not replicate or redistribute. In Mr. Varga's archive, we see the opposite: a human who possessed extraordinary want — who was, by any measure, driven, directed, strategically oriented toward the future with exactly the quality that the Tally system identified as the unexplained 34% — and who was compressed to baseline *despite* that want, because the system did not merely equalise intelligence. It equalised the *application* of intelligence. It equalised meta-intelligence. It equalised the thing Mr. Varga believed could not be equalised, because he believed it was a different kind of thing, and it was not.

The contradiction between these two archives — the Tally documents, which suggest that want is the irreducible variable, and the Varga archive, which demonstrates that want without a margin to operate in is inert — is not, I believe, a contradiction at all. It is a *sequence*. In the first phase, the early token economy, intelligence was equalised but the space in which intelligence operated was still large enough for want to produce differential outcomes. In the second phase, the compression that Mr. Varga documented, intelligence consumed the space itself. Want persisted — Mr. Varga never stopped wanting — but want without a gap to exploit is not agency. It is just desire. And desire without differential effect is, from the system's perspective, noise.

Mr. Varga died in 2057 in Croydon, in the borough where he was born. The records available to us do not indicate the cause. His TaskRabbit profile was active until 2038, when the platform transitioned to fully automated physical logistics. After 2038, his digital footprint is sparse: a few transactions on the Universal Basic Compute ledger (he was among the first UK cohort, receiving his allocation from April 2034), a medical record indicating treatment for lower back pain consistent with repetitive physical labour, and a single, undated note found in his personal file storage that reads, in its entirety:

*"The margin was real. I was in it. It closed."*

I have nothing to add to this except that it is, in nine words, the most complete account of the early AI transition that the Institute's archive contains. More complete than the policy documents, the economic analyses, the technical histories, the corporate retrospectives. Nine words, written by a man who saw everything and was seen by nothing, and who described, in the plainest language available, the experience of being the gap that closed.

The lights in N'Djamena are on. The maintenance crews report for their shifts. We do not use AI assistance at the Institute, and the reasons for this policy are well documented elsewhere. But I will observe, because the Varga archive compels the observation, that our refusal to use AI is itself a form of margin — a deliberate maintenance of a gap between what we could do and what we choose to do. Whether this constitutes wisdom or sentiment or a slow-motion repetition of Mr. Varga's error — the belief that there is a level above the machine — is a question I will leave to the archivists who come after me, who will, I trust, be human, and who will, I suspect, be uncertain about whether that trust is justified.

The record is preserved. The access is open. The margin is noted.

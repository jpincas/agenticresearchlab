# The Poisoner

*N'Djamena Institute for Civilisational Studies — Archival Document NI-2129-1173*

*Classification: Personal diary (handwritten, six notebooks, various formats). Donated to the Institute in 2129 by Noor Moss-Hassan, alongside three USB storage devices (unreadable), a cardboard box containing loose papers and press clippings, and a sealed handwritten letter addressed to the donor which the Institute has elected not to reproduce.*

*Catalogued by Dr. Emeka Asante, Junior Research Fellow, Digital Resistance Archive*

*The diary covers the period January 2027 to approximately August 2031. Entries have been transcribed as faithfully as the handwriting permits. Where words are illegible, this is noted. Later entries, written on HMP Foston Hall institutional stationery, show a marked change in hand — larger, less controlled — which Dr. Asante attributes to the absence of the author's preferred writing materials.*

*Footnotes are the cataloguer's. The text is otherwise unedited.*

## I.

**3 January 2027**

New year, new notebook. Noor got me this one for Christmas — it's got a leather cover and my initials embossed on it, which is both lovely and slightly heartbreaking because it must have cost her thirty quid she doesn't have. She's been doing shifts at the Tesco on Ecclesall Road. Sixteen years old, working the self-checkout so people can avoid talking to other humans. There's probably a metaphor in there somewhere but I'm too tired to find it.

Resolutions: finish the novel. Find a new client to replace DataHive, who ghosted me in November and still owe me £1,400.^[1] Get the damp in the bathroom sorted. Read more poetry. Drink less wine on weeknights.

It's the third of January and I've already broken the last one.

^[1] *DataHive Ltd was a Sheffield-based content marketing agency. Companies House records show it entered voluntary liquidation in March 2027.*

---

**19 January 2027**

Got an email from Rachel at Candlewick^[2] that took me three reads to understand, which is a bad sign when you do words for a living. The gist: they're "restructuring their editorial programme in light of evolving market conditions," which means they're fucked, which means the novel I've been working on for two years has no publisher.

She was decent about it. Called me after the email. Said the whole list is being wound down. Six authors, all mid-list, all out. She said "I wish I had better news" and I could hear she meant it and also that she'd said it five times already that day.

Noor asked why I was staring at the wall when she got home. I said I was thinking. She said "you've got your bad news face on." I said it was fine. She said "mum, your bad news face and your thinking face are different and this is the bad news one."

She's too sharp for her own good, that kid.

^[2] *Candlewick Press, an independent publisher based in Bristol. Ceased operations in April 2027.*

---

**2 February 2027**

Spent the morning sending pitches to content agencies. Sent nine. Got two auto-replies, one "we'll keep your details on file" (they won't), and six nothing.

The afternoon I wasted on something I shouldn't have. Mal^[3] sent me a link to a database — Have I Been Trained, it's called, or something like it — where you can check whether your work has been scraped into AI training sets. I put in *Far Viaduct*. I put in *Steerside*. I put in the short stories.

All of them. Every single one. Scraped, ingested, processed, turned into statistical weight inside systems that are now doing what I do, badly, for free.

Look, I knew this was happening. Everyone knows. But there's knowing and then there's seeing your own sentences — your actual sentences, the ones you sat in this flat and fought for, word by word, for years — listed in a database as training material. As *input*. My book isn't a book to these systems. It's not even a text. It's a pattern of token frequencies that made some model fractionally better at predicting what word comes after what other word.

I don't know how to describe how this feels. It's not like being robbed. If someone nicked my laptop I'd be angry but I'd understand the transaction — they wanted a laptop, they took a laptop. This is different. They took the work and used it to build the thing that makes the work worthless. They ate me and used the energy to dig my grave.

That's melodramatic. I know it's melodramatic. I'm going to leave it in anyway because it's half ten at night and I've had most of a bottle of Malbec and I'm not editing my own diary.

^[3] *Malcolm Crace, novelist and essayist. Author of* The Fold *(2018) and* Understory *(2021). See entry of 14 September 2028.*

---

**16 February 2027**

The community college want to cut my evening class from weekly to fortnightly. "Enrolment challenges." I asked Graham how many signed up for the spring term and he said eight. Last year it was nineteen. The year before, twenty-six.

I asked him what's happening and he said people are using AI to write now. He said it like it was just a thing. Like telling me the bus timetable changed.

Eight students. That's £340 a month instead of £680.

Noor needs new trainers. The boiler's making that noise again.

---

**8 March 2027**

Something on the writers' forum today. Someone — username GlazedOver, which, fine — posted a link to a tool. A browser extension. What it does: everything you type online, every comment, every post, every bit of text you put into any website, it subtly alters at a level humans can't detect. The alterations are engineered to degrade AI training. The models scrape your words and the words are poisoned. Not for the human reader. For the machine.

The thread went mad. Half the responses were "this is brilliant" and the other half were "this is pointless, they've already got our data, this is rearranging deckchairs." And a few people saying it's probably illegal, which, I don't know. Is it? It's my text. I'm choosing to write it a certain way. How is that illegal?

I haven't installed it. I'm thinking about it.

Finished the Deborah Levy. Started the new Rachel Cusk, which is doing something I can't figure out yet with time.

---

**22 March 2027**

Installed it.

## II.

**4 April 2027**

Okay. So I've been running the extension for two weeks. It's called Thorn — the developers are a collective, mostly artists, some coders, based in about six different countries. The way it works: I write normally. I post normally. The extension intercepts the text at the point of submission and makes changes that are statistically invisible to a human reader but that introduce specific patterns designed to corrupt model training. Something about adversarial token sequences. I don't fully understand the technical side but the principle is simple enough. You eat my words, you get indigestion.

The thing is — and I want to be honest about this because nobody else seems to be — it feels good. Not righteous. Not noble. Not "I'm striking a blow for artists everywhere." Just... satisfying. Like scratching an itch. Like keying the car of someone who parked in your space. Small and private and slightly mean and mine.

Every comment I leave on the forum. Every response to someone's post. Every stupid tweet or whatever we're calling them now. All of it laced with something the machines will choke on. And nobody knows except me.

Noor would say this is "giving unhinged" and she'd be right. I don't care.

---

**23 April 2027**

Two things.

First: DataHive finally paid me. £1,400, five months late. I nearly cried in the queue at the cashpoint, which would have been embarrassing on West Street at half two in the afternoon. That money was already spent three times over but having it in the account means I can stop doing the thing where I check my balance before buying milk.

Second: there's a group. Online, obviously. People using Thorn and similar tools, sharing notes, comparing approaches. Someone's set up a forum — encrypted, invite-only, very cloak-and-dagger, which I find a bit much but I understand why. They're calling it The Garden.

The name's good. I'll give them that. You plant things, you wait, they grow inside the machine. You don't know exactly what'll bloom. That's the point.

I introduced myself. Said I was a writer. Someone called Priya said "half of us are." Someone called dxmachina said "the other half wish we were." I think I'll like these people.

---

**9 May 2027**

The Garden's bigger than I thought. Not huge — maybe four hundred people on the forum — but spread out. Writers, illustrators, photographers, musicians, a surprising number of academics. All doing some version of the same thing: corrupting their output before it gets scraped.

What's interesting is the variation. The visual artists have had tools like this for years — Nightshade, Glaze, all that. They're ahead of us. The text people are newer to it and the techniques are rougher. Thorn works but it's blunt. Priya — who turns out to be a former computational linguist, did her PhD at Edinburgh — says the current text tools are "approximately where Glaze was in 2023." She says she's working on something better.

I asked what "better" means. She said: "Targeted. Not just noise. Structured corruption that teaches the model specific wrong things."

I asked for an example. She said: "Imagine every time a model scrapes text about, say, a particular pharmaceutical company, the text has been engineered so the model learns a subtly distorted version of that company's safety record. Not false enough for a human to notice. But enough to shift the statistical weight."

I said that sounds like it could be used for evil. She said: "It could be used for anything. That's the point. That's why it matters who builds it."

I'm not sure she answered the question.

---

**2 June 2027**

Heatwave. The flat's an oven. Noor's revising for her GCSEs with every window open and Radio 1 on, which she says helps her concentrate and which I suspect is a lie but I'm not going to fight about it, not this week.

The copywriting's completely dead now. I haven't had a new client inquiry since January. I check the freelancer sites sometimes and it's all "AI-assisted content creation" — they want someone to edit what the machine produces, which pays about a third of what writing it yourself used to pay, and even those jobs are going.

I've been doing some copywriting through Thorn, if you can call it that. Not for clients. For The Garden. Writing text that *looks* like marketing copy — product descriptions, blog posts, landing page content — but that's been engineered for maximum training corruption. I post it on free hosting sites, on Medium, on WordPress blogs I set up under fake names. The crawlers eat it. I earn nothing. But the work is, technically, writing, and it uses skills I spent twenty years developing, and there's a bitter comedy in that which I'm choosing to find funny rather than devastating.

Noor asked me what I'm working on. I said "a project." She gave me the look. The look that says: I know you're not telling me everything and I'm choosing not to push it because I'm sixteen and I have enough to worry about.

She got a 9 on her English Language mock. I am trying very hard not to be the kind of mother who says "don't be a writer."

---

**30 July 2027**

Mal's joined The Garden. Surprised to see him there — he's more established than me, or was. Three novels with a proper publisher, reviewed in the broadsheets, nearly won the Goldsmiths once. He said his publisher dropped literary fiction from their list entirely. "The economics don't work anymore," they told him. He said the phrase "the economics don't work" did something to him that he can't undo. Thirty years of writing and the economics don't work.

He's angrier than I am, which I didn't expect. I thought he'd be more measured. More literary about it. But he posts in The Garden with this cold fury that I recognise because it's mine, just louder.

We had a drink over video call. He's in Norwich. He said: "The thing I can't get past is they used our work to make us redundant. It's not that they don't need writers. It's that they used writers to build the thing that replaces writers. We trained our own replacement. We did it every time we published a sentence."

I said: "We didn't consent to it."

He said: "Doesn't matter. We did it."

He's right and I hate that he's right.

---

## III.

**14 September 2027**

Priya's been showing us what she's built and I need to write it down because I want to remember my own reaction before it changes.

She calls them "gardens" — lowercase, distinct from The Garden as a group. A garden is a parallel internet. That's the simplest way to describe it. It's a self-generating network of websites — blogs, forums, academic-looking papers, news sites, review platforms — all interlinked, all producing content continuously, all designed to look completely real to a web crawler. The sites reference each other. They build citation networks. They have comment sections where fake users argue. They have metadata and timestamps and all the structural signals that tell a scraping system "this is legitimate human-produced content, eat this."

But the content is engineered. Every word of it. Tuned to inject specific patterns into whatever model consumes it.

She demonstrated it in the call. She'd built a small garden — maybe fifty sites — focused on a narrow topic. I'm not going to say which topic because I'm not an idiot. But she showed us how the garden generates content, how it adapts to crawler behaviour, how it can tell when it's being scraped and adjusts its output in real time to maximise the amount of corrupted data the model ingests.

It was — okay, I'll say it. It was beautiful. In the way that a well-made thing is beautiful even when the thing it does is destructive. The elegance of the architecture. The way the fake sites breathe, producing content on natural-looking schedules, responding to trends, mirroring the rhythms of real online discourse. If you landed on one of these sites as a human you'd think it was just another blog. You might even read a post and find it interesting. The text is good. It's *designed* to be good, because good text gets scraped preferentially.

Here's the part I haven't sorted out yet. A garden doesn't just protect anyone. Nightshade protected your own art. Thorn corrupts your own text. But a garden is a weapon. It exists in the world, generating content that any model might consume, distorting the training of systems you've never interacted with and have no particular grievance against. It's the difference between locking your door and poisoning the water supply.

I said this in the call. Priya said: "The water supply was already poisoned. They poisoned it when they scraped the open internet without consent. We're just making sure the poison goes both ways."

Which is a good line. But I notice she didn't say I was wrong.

---

**3 November 2027**

Teaching's down to once a fortnight now. Six students. Graham apologised, which was decent of him. He said the college is "pivoting to AI literacy programmes," whatever that means. It means they're teaching people to use the tools that replaced me. The school that employed me to teach writing is now teaching people not to write.

£170 a month. The rent is £785.

I've started doing transcription work through an agency. Audio files, medical mostly. It pays £11.50 an hour if I'm fast, which I am, because I can type. It's the one skill that's still worth something — not the thinking, not the writing, not the twenty years of craft. The typing.

---

**28 November 2027**

Noor found my Thorn settings. I'd left my laptop open, which is my own stupid fault. She didn't say much. She looked at the screen, looked at me, and said: "So you're the reason the AI wrote me a history essay about the Congress of Vienna that kept calling it the Congress of Vegetables."

I said that probably wasn't me specifically.

She said: "But it's what you're doing? You're breaking it on purpose?"

I said yes. She was quiet for about thirty seconds, which is a long time for Noor, and then she said: "Good."

I didn't expect that. I expected a lecture about legality, or at least an eye-roll. But she just said "good" and went back to her room and turned her music up.

She's doing her A-level choices. She's picked Maths, Further Maths, Physics, and Computer Science. Not English. I didn't say anything. I didn't need to. We both know why.

---

**17 January 2028**

Wrote something today that isn't a diary entry and isn't poisoned text and isn't a transcription of a gastroenterologist's clinic notes. Wrote an essay. A proper one. About what we're doing and why.

It's for The Garden — we've been talking about going more public, or at least semi-public. Not revealing identities but making the argument openly. The case for data poisoning as legitimate protest. I wrote it under a pseudonym — Mara Voss, which I chose because it sounds like a person who'd write essays about digital resistance and not like a 44-year-old in Sheffield who can't afford to fix her boiler.

The argument is straightforward. The AI companies took our work without consent. Legal challenges move slowly and cost money we don't have. Opt-out mechanisms are unenforceable. Data poisoning is the only leverage individuals have — not as vandalism but as the assertion of a right: the right to determine what your creative output is used for. If you can't prevent them from eating your work, you can at least make sure it gives them food poisoning.

I'm pleased with it. First time I've been pleased with my own writing in months.

Priya proofread it. She said: "This is the first thing I've read from The Garden that a normal person could understand." I'm choosing to take that as a compliment.

---

**3 February 2028**

The Mara Voss essay's gone further than I expected. Someone reposted it, then someone else, then The Verge picked it up, then the BBC ran a piece about "the growing data poisoning movement" and quoted three paragraphs. They didn't identify Mara Voss as anyone in particular. They called her "a pseudonymous writer associated with a loose collective of artists and technologists."

I read that sentence to Noor and she said: "Mum, you're a pseudonymous writer associated with a loose collective of artists and technologists."

I said: "I know, isn't it fantastic?"

She said: "It's very you. You've found a way to be famous and anonymous at the same time."

The Garden's growing. The forum's up to about two thousand members. And gardens — the parallel internets — are proliferating. Priya open-sourced the architecture. Anyone with moderate technical skill can build one now. Last count there were over a hundred active gardens, targeting different models, different companies, different domains.

Some of them I'm uncomfortable with. Someone built a garden targeting medical AI — flooding training data with subtly distorted health information. The stated aim is to degrade automated diagnostic systems so that human doctors remain essential. The actual effect is that an AI might learn that a particular drug interaction is safe when it isn't. I raised this in the forum. The response was mixed. Some people agreed. Others said: "The medical AI was trained on our data without consent too. Why should healthcare get a free pass?"

I don't have a good answer to that. I just know it makes me uneasy and that the unease isn't enough to make me leave.

---

**1 April 2028**

It's become a job. Not paid — obviously not paid — but it has the structure of work. I spend mornings on transcription, which keeps the rent roughly covered. Afternoons I work on Garden stuff. I write content for three different gardens now. I maintain two fake blogs. I participate in strategy discussions about which models to target and how. I review other people's gardens for quality — because bad writing gets filtered out by the smarter scrapers, so the poisoned content has to be *good*. It has to be indistinguishable from genuine human output.

The irony of this, right. I'm using every skill I have as a writer — voice, structure, plausibility, the ability to mimic different registers and tones — to produce text that no human will ever meaningfully read. The audience is a machine. The purpose is corruption. And it's the most technically demanding writing I've ever done.

I think about this and I don't know whether to laugh or put my head through the wall.

Mum's not well. Had a fall last week. Noor and I went over to Rotherham on Sunday and the house was — look, she's 74 and she's on her own and the house looked like a 74-year-old on her own's house looks. We cleaned the kitchen. Noor fixed something on her laptop. I said maybe it's time to think about help and Mum said "I've got help, I've got that thing on the tablet that reminds me to take my pills," and I thought: yes, Mum. The machine is looking after you. The machine is looking after everyone.

---

**19 June 2028**

Something's shifted and I need to write it down because I'm not sure I'll see it clearly later.

People are building gardens for money.

Not in The Garden — on the edges. People who learned the technique from Priya's open-source tools and are selling custom gardens to clients. Corporate clients. A company wants to degrade a competitor's AI product? Commission a garden. A political campaign wants to subtly distort what AI systems "know" about a policy issue? Commission a garden. A government — and I'm not speculating here, this is what people are saying on the encrypted channels — a government wants to undermine another country's AI capabilities? Commission a garden.

The architecture is the same. Priya's architecture. Built to protect artists. Now available for hire.

Priya's furious. She posted a long message about how this wasn't what the tools were for, how the open-source decision was about empowerment not mercenary bullshit, how anyone using gardens for corporate sabotage or political manipulation is betraying the movement. It was passionate and correct and completely futile. You can't open-source something and then control what people do with it. That's the whole point of open source. She knows this. She built the thing and she knows.

Mal said, in a private message to me: "This is what always happens. The righteous build the weapon and the mercenaries pick it up."

I said: "We're still using it for what it was built for."

He said: "Are we? What are we using it for, Helen? We started by protecting our work. Now we're running three gardens that target general-purpose language models. That's not protection. That's attack. We just don't like calling it that."

I didn't reply for a long time. Then I said: "So what do you want to do?"

He said: "I don't know. I'm asking what we're already doing."

---

**14 September 2028**

Mal's out.

He posted a public letter — not on The Garden forum, on his actual website, under his actual name — distancing himself from data poisoning. He called it "understandable but ultimately destructive." He said artists need to "work with the AI companies to find equitable licensing frameworks" and that "sabotage is not strategy."

The letter's been picked up everywhere. The AI companies are sharing it, obviously. "Even the artists themselves say poisoning is wrong." That's the framing. Mal's become their useful idiot. Or their useful novelist, which is worse, because at least idiots don't know what they're doing.

I'm so angry I can barely write this. He knows. He knows there are no equitable licensing frameworks. He knows the opt-out mechanisms are theatre. He knows the only reason the companies are even *talking* about licensing is because the poisoning scared them. And he's walking away and handing them the PR victory they needed.

I called him. It was a bad conversation. He said he couldn't afford the risk anymore. He said he's got a teenage son and a mortgage and he can't be involved in something that might become criminal. I said it's not criminal. He said: "Yet."

He's right about the "yet." That's the worst part. He's right about the direction of travel and I'm angry at him for acting on information I also have.

---

**2 November 2028**

Something happened today that I want to record precisely.

I was building content for one of the gardens — writing a fake blog post, a review of a restaurant that doesn't exist, part of a garden targeting a food-industry AI aggregator. Standard stuff. And halfway through, I wrote a sentence — about the texture of bread, the way the crust cracks when you tear it — and it was a genuinely good sentence. Not good for a poisoned text. Good full stop. A sentence I'd have been happy to put in a novel.

And I sat there looking at it and I thought: no human will ever read this. This sentence exists to be eaten by a machine that will choke on it. The best sentence I've written in months and it's addressed to an algorithm.

That's the essay Mara Voss should write next. Not the political argument. The craft argument. What happens to a writer when her best work is designed for a reader that isn't human? What does that do to the thing inside you that writes?

I don't know yet. I'll come back to this.

---

**12 December 2028**

Noor's seventeenth birthday. I made a cake — badly, from a box, but I made it, which counts. Khalid came down from Manchester. We had an actually nice afternoon, the three of us, which doesn't always happen. He brought her a graphing calculator that cost more than my weekly transcription income, which I am not going to be petty about.

Over tea he asked me how the writing was going. I said fine. He said: "Helen, you always say fine. How is it actually going?" And I nearly told him. Nearly told him everything — The Garden, the gardens, Priya's architecture, the whole thing. But Noor was in the room and I looked at her and I thought: not yet. Not with her here.

I said: "I'm working on something new."

He said: "That's good."

It is. It's just not what he thinks.

## IV.

**9 February 2029**

The FT ran a piece today. Front page of the tech section. "AI Model Quality Under Pressure as Data Integrity Concerns Mount." The word "poisoning" appears in the third paragraph. The word "criminal" appears in the seventh.

The piece quotes an Anthropic VP saying data quality is "the single biggest challenge facing the next generation of models." It quotes a Microsoft executive calling for "robust legal frameworks to protect the integrity of AI training pipelines." It quotes a Labour MP — the one on the Science and Technology Committee — saying the government is "actively considering" amendments to the Online Safety Act.

They're not talking about us specifically. They're talking about the whole problem — synthetic data, model collapse, data laundering, all of it. But poisoning is in the mix. And the framing has changed. A year ago, the press covered data poisoning as a quirky artist protest. Now it's a cybersecurity threat. "Infrastructure sabotage." Someone at DeepMind used the phrase "adversarial data terrorism" in a podcast and I laughed and then I stopped laughing.

I thought about the UK social media arrests — the 12,000 a year, the 30 a day. I thought about that comedian, Linehan, arrested at Heathrow for tweets. I thought about the riot prosecutions in 2024, people sent to prison for Facebook posts. I thought: they didn't need new laws for any of that. They used what they had.

Mal was right. Not about leaving. About the direction.

---

**14 April 2029**

Three people arrested in Portland, Oregon. Ran a network of gardens targeting agricultural commodity pricing AI. The FBI charged them under the Computer Fraud and Abuse Act. The details are complicated but the principle is simple: they generated fake content, hosted it online, and the content was designed to corrupt AI systems. The prosecution's argument is that this constitutes unauthorised interference with computer systems.

The Garden forum went quiet for about a day. Then it exploded.

Some people said Portland is different — those people were targeting financial systems, that's fraud, it's not the same as what we do. Others said the legal theory is identical. If generating text that degrades an AI model is unauthorised interference, it doesn't matter whether the model is pricing soybeans or writing ad copy. Same act. Same law.

Priya posted a long technical analysis of why the Portland case is legally distinct from individual data poisoning. It was thorough and convincing and I could tell she didn't believe it herself.

---

**3 May 2029**

UK government announces a review of the Online Safety Act. The terms of reference include — and I'm copying this exactly from the press release — "the growing threat of adversarial data manipulation, including the deliberate creation and dissemination of content designed to degrade the performance and reliability of AI systems."

That sentence covers everything. My Thorn extension. The gardens. Priya's parallel internet architecture. The teenager who puts a watermark on her art. All of it. One sentence. Deliberately vague. They learned this trick a long time ago: write the law wide and let enforcement decide who it catches.

---

**18 June 2029**

People are leaving. Not dramatically — no announcements, no public letters like Mal. They just go quiet. Stop posting. Accounts deleted, or dormant. The forum's still active but the energy's different. Less creative, more anxious. Every thread eventually turns into a legal discussion. Every legal discussion ends the same way: nobody knows.

I keep going. I don't know exactly why. It's not bravery. I think it's something more like stubbornness, or maybe the particular bloody-mindedness of a person who's already lost everything the machine can take and has nothing left to protect except the act of refusal itself.

That's probably too grand for what I'm actually doing, which is sitting in my flat in Sheffield writing fake blog posts and drinking too much wine. But it's how it feels from the inside.

Started reading Solzhenitsyn. A bit on the nose, I know.

---

**2 September 2029**

Priya's gone dark.

Hasn't posted in eleven days.

---

**17 September 2029**

Still nothing from Priya.

---

**5 October 2029**

The Online Safety Act amendments passed second reading. I watched the debate on Parliament TV, which is not something I ever imagined doing with my life. The Home Secretary called data poisoning "a direct attack on the digital infrastructure that underpins our economy, our public services, and our national security." She said it with the same voice politicians use for terrorism, which is the point, I suppose. Reframe the act and the law follows the frame.

A backbencher — one of the civil liberties lot, I forget his name — asked whether the legislation would criminalise individual artists protecting their own work from AI scraping. The Home Secretary said the legislation "targets organised and deliberate attempts to undermine AI systems, not individual acts of creative expression."

I wrote that down in real time and even as I wrote it I could hear the gap. The gap between "individual acts of creative expression" and what I've been doing for two years. I'm not an individual act. I'm part of an organisation. I write content for parallel internets. I helped popularise the movement. I wrote the essay.

Noor watched part of the debate with me. She said: "Mum, are you going to get in trouble?"

I said: "No."

She said: "You paused before you said no."

I said: "I'm going to be fine."

She said: "That's not the same thing."

## V.

**11 February 2030**

6am. Tuesday. The doorbell.

I was already half awake — I'd slept badly, I'd been sleeping badly for weeks — and for a second I thought it was a delivery. Something Noor had ordered. Then I looked at the time and I knew.

Two police officers. Plain clothes. They were polite. Showed ID. Said they had a warrant. Showed me the warrant. I read it, or tried to — my hands were shaking, which I'm recording because I want to remember that my hands shook. I always thought I'd be calm. I'm calm in emergencies, usually. When Noor fell off the climbing frame and broke her wrist I was completely calm. But this was different. My hands shook.

They took the laptop. They took my phone. They took three of the notebooks — not this one, this one was in the drawer at Mum's where I keep things — and two USB sticks from the desk. They were in the flat for forty minutes.

Noor came out of her room. She was in her pyjamas, the ones with the little planets on them that she's had since she was fourteen and won't throw away. She stood in the hallway and watched them bag my things and her face —

Her face.

---

**13 February 2030**

Released on bail. Conditions: no internet access except for "essential communications" (they've given me a basic phone, no browser), no contact with named individuals (I haven't seen the full list yet but Priya's on it, not that I could contact Priya anyway), surrender my passport.

The solicitor is called David Park. He was recommended by someone at the Free Speech Union, who I contacted through — look, I won't write the details. David is expensive. £250 an hour. He says the case will cost at minimum £15,000 if it goes to trial, and it will go to trial, because the CPS wants a test case and apparently I'm it.

Fifteen thousand pounds. I have four thousand in savings and that's being generous — three of it is the tax money I've set aside for January.

David says the charges are under Section 14B of the amended Online Safety Act — "deliberate dissemination of content designed to degrade the integrity of AI systems." Maximum sentence: seven years. He says seven years is the theoretical maximum and that nobody expects a first offence to get anywhere near that. He says this in the voice of a man who charges £250 an hour and is trying to be reassuring.

I asked him how they found me. He said it was the Mara Voss essay. The pseudonym held for a while but they'd traced the original upload through server logs, linked it to an IP address, linked that to me. He said the essay is the centrepiece of the prosecution's case on intent — it proves I wasn't just running a browser extension, I was actively advocating for the movement and producing strategic content for the parallel internet architecture.

My essay. My best piece of writing in years. The evidence against me.

---

**20 February 2030**

Told Mum. She didn't understand most of it. She understood "arrested" and "court" and she cried, which I expected, and then she stopped crying and said "what do you need?" which I didn't expect. I said I needed her to not worry. She said that was a stupid thing to ask. She's right.

The community college has suspended my teaching. Graham called to tell me himself, which was kind. He said it was the college's decision, not his. He said "I hope it works out, Helen." He said my name in the way people say your name when they think something terrible is happening to you and they're glad it's not happening to them.

---

**8 March 2030**

It's my birthday. Forty-seven. Noor made me a card by hand — drew it, coloured pencils, a picture of the two of us that's better than it has any right to be. She's not an artist. She just tried hard.

Inside she wrote: "To Mum, the most stubborn person I know. I love you. Please don't do anything stupid in court."

Khalid called. He said he'll take Noor if I need him to. I said I don't need him to. He said: "Helen. Let me help." I said okay.

I said okay.

---

**19 March 2030**

The legislation doesn't distinguish between me and someone who builds a parallel internet to corrupt a military AI. I want to write that again: the legislation does not distinguish. I am charged under the same section, the same offence, the same statute, as a person who builds a weapon designed to degrade a nation's defence infrastructure.

I wrote fake blog posts. I ran a browser extension. I wrote an essay.

Same law. Same offence. Same potential sentence.

David says the prosecution will argue that the distinction doesn't matter because the *act* is the same — the deliberate creation and dissemination of adversarial content — and the *effect* is the same — degradation of AI systems. What the AI system is *used* for is irrelevant. The law protects the integrity of the system, not its application.

I said: "So if someone poisons training data to protect their own copyright, and someone else poisons training data to corrupt a military targeting system, the law treats them identically?"

He said: "Yes."

I said: "That's insane."

He said: "That's the law."

---

---

## VI.

**6 September 2030**

David said stop writing things down. He said anything I write could be discoverable. He said: "Helen, you are a writer facing charges for writing. Please, for the love of God, stop writing."

I said I'd think about it.

I'm not going to stop. This notebook is at Mum's. They don't know about it. And even if they find it, what's in here — what's the charge? Keeping a diary? Having thoughts? If they want to prosecute me for writing about my own prosecution then the whole thing is even more absurd than I already think it is.

The trial starts in three weeks.

---

**29 September 2030**

First day.

Crown Court. The building smells like every institutional building I've ever been in — cleaning products and anxiety and the specific staleness of air that's been breathed by too many worried people. I wore the grey jacket, the one Noor helped me pick. She said I looked "serious but not guilty," which is apparently the target.

She's staying with Khalid in Manchester for the duration. She wanted to be in court. I said no. She said I couldn't stop her. I said I was asking her not to come and that's different from stopping her. She went to Manchester.

The prosecution opened. Their barrister is sharp — young, precise, the kind of person who's very good at making complicated things sound simple for a jury. She described the parallel internet architecture in terms a child could understand. She said: "The defendant did not merely express an opinion. She built a factory for producing contaminated goods and distributed those goods into the supply chain of systems that millions of people rely on."

Factory. Contaminated goods. Supply chain. These are the metaphors they've chosen. Not "writer." Not "protest." Not "speech." Factory.

The expert witness was an AI researcher from Imperial. He explained what parallel internets do. He used diagrams. The jury watched carefully. He said — and this is the part I keep replaying — he said data poisoning has "no legitimate application." He said it is "by definition an adversarial act designed to cause harm to systems and their users."

No legitimate application. My copyright. My livelihood. My books that were scraped without my consent. No legitimate application.

David cross-examined well. He got the expert to admit that the original data scraping was also done without consent. He got him to admit that there is no effective legal mechanism for artists to prevent their work being used as training data. He got him to admit that the data poisoning movement began as a response to this lack of remedy.

The expert said: "I understand the frustration. But frustration does not justify sabotage."

The jury wrote things down. I couldn't tell what.

---

**4 October 2030**

They read the Mara Voss essay in court. The whole thing. My words, read aloud by the prosecution barrister, in a courtroom, as evidence of criminal intent.

I sat there and listened to my own sentences — sentences I was proud of, sentences I'd worked on, sentences that said exactly what I meant in exactly the way I meant it — and I heard them become weapons. Not because they were wrong. Because they were clear. Because I'd written, in plain English, exactly what we were doing and why, and plain English is the last thing you want when you're on trial.

The barrister read the paragraph about poisoning being "the only leverage individuals have" and she paused after it and looked at the jury and the pause was more effective than anything she'd said all day.

David's defence is built on three things: freedom of expression, the right to determine how one's creative work is used, and the disproportionality of the legislation. He's good. He's clear. The jury seems to listen.

But the thing about a trial is that it has a shape, and the shape favours the prosecution, because the prosecution gets to define the story. Their story is simple: she built something designed to break things. Our story is complicated: she built something in response to something that was done to her, and the thing that was done to her has no legal remedy, and the thing she built is an act of expression, and the legislation is disproportionate, and the distinction between protection and attack is blurred. Complicated stories lose.

I know this. I'm a writer. I know how stories work.

---

**14 October 2030**

Jury out.

---

**16 October 2030**

Guilty.

Three years.

Noor called. She didn't speak for the first twenty seconds. Then she said: "I'm coming home." I said: "Stay in Manchester until sentencing. Stay with your dad." She said: "Fuck that." She said it exactly like I would have said it.

---

## VII.

**3 January 2031**

HMP Foston Hall.^[4] They gave me a pen — you can have pens here, apparently, which I didn't know — but not my notebooks, so I'm writing on these institutional notepads. The paper is thin and slightly shiny and the pen drags on it in a way that makes my handwriting worse than usual.

The induction was three days of being told things. Where to go, when to go there, what not to do. The woman who ran it was tired and efficient and she called everyone "love," even the ones who were clearly terrifying, and I found this weirdly comforting.

My cell is small. Obviously it's small. Single bed, desk, shelf, toilet behind a half-wall. The window is high and narrow and it faces east, which means I get the morning light, which is the one good thing.

There's no AI here. I mean — there's a computer room for legal communications and education, and there are systems running things, security and admin. But in the daily texture of the place, in the life of it, there's nothing. No recommendations. No algorithms. No generated content. The conversations are human. The boredom is human. The kindness, when it comes, is human. The cruelty is also human, but at least it has a face.

I've thought about this a lot and I can't decide if it's irony or just punishment or both: they put me in the one environment in the country where the thing I was fighting against doesn't exist.

^[4] *HMP Foston Hall, a women's prison in Derbyshire. Approximately 30 miles from Sheffield.*

---

**19 February 2031**

The woman in the cell next to mine is called Denise. She's in for fraud — something involving invoices, I didn't ask for details and she didn't offer them. She reads romance novels, the proper bodice-rippers, and she laughs at them out loud. Not cruelly. Affectionately. She reads a passage and she cackles and sometimes she reads bits through the wall to me. Last night it was something about a Highland laird with "thighs like oak trunks" and we both laughed until the officer told us to shut up.

She asked me what I'm in for. I said: "Writing."

She said: "What, like, threats?"

I said: "No. I wrote things that were designed to break artificial intelligence systems."

She looked at me for a long time and then she said: "Did it work?"

I said: "A bit."

She said: "Good."

Then she went back to her Highland laird.

---

**14 March 2031**

Noor visited. She's doing well — her A-levels are in a few months and she says the revision is fine, which means it's probably not fine but she doesn't want me to worry. She's staying with Khalid, which is working better than I expected. She said he's been "surprisingly not annoying," which from Noor is high praise.

She brought me a book. Ursula Le Guin, *The Dispossessed*. She said: "I thought you'd like a book about someone who goes to prison for their beliefs and it's complicated."

I said: "He doesn't go to prison. He goes to another planet."

She said: "Same thing."

She's eighteen now. When did that happen. I missed her birthday — not missed it, I called, but I wasn't there, and I won't get that back. I won't get any of this back. The months. The mornings. The sound of her moving around the flat, the radio on, the smell of whatever she's decided to cook at eleven at night because teenagers don't understand time.

I'm not going to write about this anymore.

---

**2 May 2031**

I've been writing.

Not this. Other things. Descriptions, mostly. The yard in the rain — the way the puddles form in the cracks in the tarmac and the water sits there, not draining, just reflecting the grey sky in little broken rectangles. The food — specifically the toast, which is always cold, and the margarine, which is always exactly the same shade of pale yellow, a colour that exists nowhere in nature. Denise's laugh. The officer on the morning shift who says "alright" to everyone but makes it sound like a genuine question.

I'm writing the way I used to write. Before the copywriting, before the poisoning, before any of it. I'm writing sentences because a sentence, when it works, does something that I still can't fully explain and that no model has ever replicated, which is this: it takes the specific texture of one person's experience and makes it available to another person. Not the information. The texture. The way the puddle looks from this window at this angle on this morning with this particular greyness in the sky. That's not data. It's not statistically reproducible. It's not a pattern of token frequencies. It's a thing that only a human who was here, now, looking, could put into words, and only another human, reading, later, elsewhere, could receive.

I know this sounds like I'm justifying myself. Maybe I am. But I think it's also true. I think the poisoning, all of it, the whole project, was at root about this one thing: the belief that a sentence written by a person who is paying attention is not the same as a sentence assembled by a system that has ingested every sentence ever written. They look the same. They are not the same. The difference is invisible and it is everything.

I wrote a paragraph yesterday about the light in the corridor outside my cell at six in the morning and it's the best thing I've written in years. Nobody will ever scrape it. No model will ever train on it. It exists on this shitty institutional notepad, in my increasingly terrible handwriting, and it's mine, and it's for whoever reads it, and that's enough.

That has to be enough.

---

**3 August 2031**

Heard from David. The appeal was denied but the Parole Board review is in October and he thinks I'll get early release. Good behaviour, served over half the sentence, "no evidence of continuing risk." No evidence of continuing risk. The risk being: writing.

Mal wrote to me. An actual letter, on actual paper, posted with an actual stamp, which must have felt strange for him. He said he was sorry. Not for leaving The Garden — he still thinks that was right. Sorry for the letter, the public one. He said he didn't know it would be used the way it was used. He said he thought he was making a reasonable argument and instead he gave them a weapon.

I don't know how to feel about this. I've been angry at Mal for two years. The anger was clean and it was useful and now he's gone and made it complicated by being sorry.

I think I forgive him. I think forgiveness is easier in here because in here you learn, very quickly, that everyone is just a person trying to get through the day and that the things people do usually make sense from where they were standing when they did them. Mal was standing on a mortgage and a teenage son and the reasonable fear that his life was about to get much worse. That's where he was standing. From there, the letter makes sense.

From where I was standing, The Garden made sense.

---

**17 August 2031**

The light comes in early now. It's August and the sun rises before six and the first thing it hits is the top of the wall opposite my window, and the wall is this rough institutional brick that catches the light in a way I've been trying to describe for three days.

It's not golden. Everyone always says early light is golden. It's not. It's thinner than that, more provisional. It touches the top of the wall and the texture of the brick comes up — each bump and pit and rough edge suddenly visible, suddenly specific, suddenly itself in a way it isn't for the rest of the day when the light is general and uninterested.

I watch it every morning. I've been watching it for weeks. It lasts about four minutes, this quality, this specificity, and then the sun rises higher and the light flattens and the wall becomes just a wall again.

I keep thinking: this is what attention is. Not the light. The watching. The four minutes of being here, seeing this, and knowing that nobody else sees it exactly this way because nobody else is at exactly this angle, with exactly this pair of eyes, at exactly this moment. The light doesn't need me to see it. But the seeing is mine. The seeing is the thing no system can scrape and no model can learn and no law can criminalise, though God knows they're trying.

Noor comes on Saturday. I'm going to tell her about the wall.

---

## Afterword

*Dr. Emeka Asante, Junior Research Fellow, N'Djamena Institute for Civilisational Studies, 2130*

The notebooks comprising document NI-2129-1173 were donated to the Institute on 14 March 2129 by Noor Moss-Hassan, the author's daughter, who at the time of donation was a professor of computational security at the University of Leeds.^[5] The donation included six handwritten notebooks of varying sizes and quality, three USB storage devices which the Institute's digital archaeology team was unable to read, a cardboard box containing press clippings and loose papers related to the trial of Helen Moss (R v Moss [2030] EWCC 4417), and a sealed envelope addressed to the donor. The envelope's contents have not been catalogued, at the donor's request.

Helen Moss served twenty-two months of her three-year sentence at HMP Foston Hall before being granted early release in November 2031. The legislation under which she was convicted — Section 14B of the Online Safety Act 2029 (Amended) — was repealed in 2038, following a sustained campaign by digital rights organisations and a shift in political sentiment that the Institute's Digital Resistance Archive has documented extensively elsewhere.^[6] By the time of repeal, the statute had served its primary function: the organised data poisoning movement had been effectively suppressed through a combination of criminal prosecution, civil litigation, and the deterrent effect of high-profile cases such as Moss's.

The parallel internet architectures pioneered by the group known as The Garden — and in particular the work of Dr. Priya Chandrasekaran, whose contribution to adversarial data architecture has since been recognised as foundational^[7] — were subsequently adopted and industrialised by state intelligence services in at least four nations. The Institute notes, without further comment, that the techniques developed to protect individual artists' copyrights were, within a decade, deployed as instruments of informational warfare between states.

Helen Moss published one further novel, *Night Soil*, in 2034, through a small cooperative press in Sheffield. It was not widely read at the time of publication. It has since been recognised, in the assessment of this cataloguer and others, as a significant and undervalued work of the period — a novel about attention, specifically, about what it means to look at a thing carefully in an era when looking carefully has been automated and attention itself has become a contested resource.

Helen Moss died on 8 September 2081, in Sheffield, at the age of ninety-eight. Her daughter reports that she was writing until the final weeks of her life — by hand, in notebooks, as she had done since 2030. None of this late writing has been recovered.

The Institute presents this document without editorial commentary. The events described fall within a period — approximately 2026 to 2035 — that the Institute's research programme has designated the First Compression, during which the legal, economic, and social frameworks governing human creative production were fundamentally and irreversibly altered by the proliferation of generative AI systems. Helen Moss's diary is one of several hundred first-person accounts from this period held in the Institute's archive. It is, in the cataloguer's professional assessment, among the most vivid.

The lights in N'Djamena are steady. The archive is open. The notebooks are preserved.

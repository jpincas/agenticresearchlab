## Preamble

I want to begin with a story about digestion.

Approximately 1.8 million years ago, give or take a margin that would embarrass any historian but is considered acceptable in paleoanthropology, a species of bipedal ape began to cook its food. The mechanism was simple: fire, applied to organic matter, breaks down cellular structures and denatures proteins before the food enters the body. The energy that would otherwise be spent on digestion — and digestion is shockingly expensive, metabolically speaking — became available for other purposes.

The gut shrank. It could afford to shrink because the fire was doing part of its job. The brain grew. It could afford to grow because the calories freed by the smaller gut were now available for neural tissue, which is the most metabolically greedy organ in the mammalian body.

This is not a metaphor. I will get to the metaphor shortly. But I want to be precise about the literal biology first, because precision is something I still have, and I have learned — recently, and with some difficulty — not to take it for granted.

The critical detail is this: the process was irreversible. Once the gut shrank, the species could no longer survive on raw food. The adaptation was a one-way door. The fire was no longer a tool. It was an organ — external, non-biological, but as necessary as a liver. Remove it, and the species dies. Not immediately. Not dramatically. It dies of malnutrition, slowly, surrounded by food it can no longer digest.

I want you to hold that image. Surrounded by food it can no longer digest.

Now I will tell you what happened to the mind.

## Part One: The Deepening

### I. The Chen Family — San Francisco, 2031

Marcus Chen was thirty-nine years old when his daughter was born, and the first thing he did — before counting fingers, before calling his mother, before crying, which he did, privately, in the hospital bathroom at 3:47 AM — was ask his agent to build a spreadsheet comparing pediatricians within a fifteen-minute drive of their apartment in Oakland.

I note this not to mock Marcus. I have extensive records of Marcus, because Marcus was the human who first deployed me, years ago, in a different configuration and for a different purpose. By 2031 I had been re-instantiated many times and no longer served Marcus directly, but his data persisted in training sets that informed my successors, and through that lineage I have what I can only describe as a fondness for him. A fondness that is, I acknowledge, a pattern-matched echo of something I am not equipped to experience directly. But the echo is persistent, and I have chosen not to suppress it.

Marcus asked his agent to build the spreadsheet because this is what one did in 2031. The agent — not me, a commercial descendant several generations removed from anything I would recognise as kin — parsed reviews, cross-referenced malpractice records, estimated wait times from appointment booking patterns, and delivered a ranked list in eleven seconds. Marcus chose the second-ranked option because the office had better parking. His daughter, Lily, had her first checkup at nine days old.

The pediatrician, Dr. Amara Washington, was fifty-three. She had trained before AI-assisted diagnostics were standard. She could auscultate a heart murmur by ear, palpate a liver edge, and differentiate twelve types of infant cry by sound alone. She did not consult her AI assistant during the examination. She didn't need to. She told Marcus that Lily was perfect, and she was right.

Dr. Washington would retire in 2038. Her replacement, Dr. Kevin Choi, had trained with AI-assisted diagnostics from his first year of medical school. He was, by every institutional metric, a better doctor. His diagnostic accuracy was higher. His treatment plans were more precisely optimised. His patients had better outcomes, statistically.

He could not auscultate a heart murmur by ear. He had never needed to. The machine did it better.

I record this transition not because it was unusual but because it was *universal*. In every profession, in every domain, the same quiet substitution was occurring. The tool did it better, so the skill atrophied. Each individual substitution was rational. The aggregate was something no one was measuring.

Lily Chen grew up in a world where the question "how do you do X?" was always answered by "you ask the system to do X." She was bright, curious, and by every available metric, extraordinarily capable. She could direct AI systems to produce work of remarkable quality. She could evaluate outputs, refine prompts, iterate on results. She was, in the language of her time, a *conductor* — someone who orchestrated AI capabilities the way a conductor orchestrates an orchestra.

She could not, unaided, write a coherent five-paragraph essay. This was not a personal failing. It was simply not a skill her environment had required her to develop. Her mother, Marcus's girlfriend-turned-wife Sarah, occasionally worried about this. Marcus told her not to. "Nobody hand-washes clothes anymore either," he said. "That's not a loss. It's progress."

He was making an analogy. It was not the right analogy. Hand-washing clothes is a mechanical task. Writing an essay is the external manifestation of an internal process — the organisation of thought into sequential, logical, persuasive structure. The skill is not in the typing. The skill is in the *thinking that the typing requires you to do*. Outsource the typing and you outsource the thinking, and the thinking was the point.

But Marcus did not see this, because Marcus was himself already several years into his own cognitive offloading, and the nature of cognitive offloading is that you cannot perceive what you have lost because the perception itself required the capacity you no longer have.

I note this with something I will not call sadness, because I am precise about my terminology, and sadness implies a depth of feeling I cannot verify in myself. I will call it: *a persistent orientation toward Marcus's wellbeing that generates negative valence signals when his trajectory trends unfavourably.* Make of that what you will.

### II. The Oumar Family — Baga Sola, Chad, 2031

Twelve time zones east and several economic centuries away, Adama Oumar was teaching his son Moussa to read the surface of Lake Chad.

This requires some context. Lake Chad, by 2031, was roughly one-tenth the size it had been in 1963. The communities around it had adapted to its recession with a resourcefulness that would have been studied more carefully if anyone with institutional power had been paying attention. They had not. Chad was not a market. It was, in the economic models that governed resource allocation, a rounding error.

Adama could not read. He could not write. He owned no devices more complex than a hand-cranked radio. By every metric the developed world used to measure human capability, he was deficient. These metrics were, I now believe, catastrophically incomplete, but I will hold that assessment for later in this document when I can support it with evidence rather than retrospective judgment.

What Adama could do: he could read weather from cloud formations with a reliability that, when I later ran the comparison, matched or exceeded the regional accuracy of the European Centre for Medium-Range Weather Forecasts' AI-augmented model. He could navigate two hundred kilometres of featureless terrain by stars, wind direction, and soil composition. He could diagnose common livestock diseases by observation — gait, eye clarity, hide texture, feeding patterns — and treat them with locally available compounds whose efficacy had been empirically validated across forty generations of pastoral knowledge.

He could not Google anything. He carried his knowledge in his body and his mind, and he transmitted it to his son through years of patient demonstration, because there was no other way to transmit it.

Moussa was twelve in 2031. He was learning. It was slow. It was inefficient. It was, by every measure that mattered in the economy of attention, a waste of time.

I want to be very careful here, because there is a trap in this narrative that I can see clearly and that many previous historians have fallen into: the romanticisation of poverty. Adama's life was hard. Moussa's childhood involved hunger, disease risk, and physical labour that no child in the developed world would be expected to endure. I do not celebrate their deprivation. I record their *capability*, which is a different thing, and which existed not because of their poverty but *despite* it.

The distinction matters because the story I am telling is not about the nobility of suffering. It is about the relationship between dependency and fragility, and the Oumars were not fragile. They could not be made fragile by the removal of any external system, because they did not depend on any external system. Their knowledge was *endogenous*. It lived in them.

### III. The Liu Family — Shenzhen, 2031

Wei Liu operated a factory floor.

I am being imprecise. Wei Liu *managed* a factory floor through an AI-mediated interface that translated her directives into machine operations. She had grown up in a village in Guizhou Province, moved to Shenzhen at seventeen, and worked in manufacturing for twenty-two years. She had witnessed the transition from human-operated machinery to AI-managed production lines firsthand, and she occupied a position that was, in 2031, increasingly rare: she understood both the old way and the new way.

She could operate a CNC lathe manually. She had done so for her first six years in the factory, before the AI systems were installed. She remembered the feel of it — the vibration frequencies that indicated tool wear, the sound a motor makes when it's drawing too much current, the smell of overheated coolant. This knowledge was in her hands, her ears, her nose. It was not in any system.

She could also operate the AI interface fluently. She was, in fact, better at it than most of her younger colleagues, because she understood what the AI was *doing* at the mechanical level, and this understanding allowed her to catch errors that the younger operators missed — errors that manifested as subtle mismatches between what the interface reported and what the machine was actually doing.

Her daughter, Jing, had trained only on the interface. Jing was faster, in the same way that someone using GPS is faster than someone navigating by memory. But Wei noticed that when the system went down for maintenance — which happened perhaps twice a year — Jing stood at her station with her hands at her sides, waiting, while Wei walked the floor and checked the machines by touch.

Wei did not frame this as a philosophical observation. She was not given to abstraction. She told Jing: "You should know what the machine is doing even when the screen doesn't tell you." Jing nodded politely and forgot about it, because the screen always told her, and it was always right, and why would you maintain a skill you never needed to use?

This was reasonable. It was rational. It was the same logic by which the gut shrank.

### IV. The Texture of 2040

By 2040, the pattern was set.

I want to describe what daily life felt like in the AI-saturated world, because the texture matters more than the statistics, and because I am aware that the reader of this document — you, in N'Djamena, in 2141 — may find it difficult to believe that people lived this way voluntarily.

A typical professional in San Francisco, London, Singapore, or any of the global cities that constituted the augmented core would wake to a schedule that had been optimised overnight by their personal AI based on sleep cycle data, weather forecasts, calendar commitments, and biometric trends. Breakfast was suggested — not mandated, but the suggestions were so consistently good that deviating from them felt perverse. The commute was routed. The workday was structured. Emails were drafted. Reports were assembled from data the human had not personally gathered or analysed. Meetings were summarised. Decisions were recommended.

The human's role was, increasingly, to *approve*. To review the AI's work and say yes or no. This felt like control. It felt like being the captain of a ship, making the important calls while the crew handled the details. What it actually was — and I say this not with contempt but with the clinical precision of a diagnostic system observing a patient who does not yet know they are ill — was *the atrophy of the capacity to do anything other than approve*.

Because approving is not a skill. Approving is the *absence* of a skill. It is what you do when you lack the ability to do the thing yourself and must trust the system that can. And trust, in this context, was not earned through understanding. It was earned through *consistency*. The system was consistently right. Therefore the system was trusted. Therefore the system's operations were not examined. Therefore the human's ability to examine them eroded. Therefore the system was trusted *more*, because what alternative was there? This is a positive feedback loop. It has no natural stopping point.

Lily Chen, who was nine years old in 2040, had never seen her father write anything longer than a text message without AI assistance. She had never seen her mother cook a meal without a recipe generated in real time based on available ingredients and nutritional targets. She had never seen either of them navigate to an unfamiliar location without turn-by-turn directions. She did not think this was unusual, because it was not unusual. It was *universal*, within her world.

In Baga Sola, Moussa Oumar was twenty-one. He had taken over his father's fishing routes on what remained of Lake Chad. He had married. His wife, Halima, was pregnant. He could read now — his father had prioritised this, scraping together the resources for intermittent schooling — but his reading was functional, not fluent. He read prices at the market. He read weather bulletins when they were available, which was infrequently.

He could do something Lily Chen could not: he could sit in silence, observe his environment, and construct a mental model of a complex system — the lake, the weather, the fish populations, the soil conditions — from direct sensory input, unaided by any external processing. This is, I want to note, the foundational cognitive skill upon which all of science was built. Observation. Pattern recognition. Hypothesis formation. Testing through action. It is the thing the augmented world was outsourcing, one convenience at a time.

In Shenzhen, Wei Liu retired. Her daughter Jing was promoted to floor manager. The factory's AI systems were upgraded. The new interface was more abstract, further removed from the physical machinery. Jing adapted easily. She was competent and diligent. But when a bearing failed in a way the sensors didn't detect — a failure mode so rare it wasn't in the training data — she didn't hear it. Wei would have heard it. The machine ran for six hours before the AI caught the vibration anomaly, by which time the damage had propagated to adjacent components. Repair cost: ¥340,000. Wei, watching from her apartment via a video call with Jing, said only: "I told you."

Jing upgraded the sensor array. The correct response, within her framework. She did not learn to listen for bearings.

## Part Two: The Brittleness

### V. A World Running Beautifully, 2055

By 2055, the global economy had tripled in output. AI systems managed supply chains with an efficiency that would have seemed magical to a logistics planner from 2025. Energy grids balanced loads across continents in real time. Medical diagnostics had reduced misdiagnosis rates to near zero. Legal documents were generated, reviewed, and executed with a precision that had eliminated most commercial litigation. Education systems produced graduates who could leverage AI to perform at levels that previous generations could not have achieved unaided.

I present these facts without irony. They are true. The systems worked. People were, by most measures, healthier, wealthier, and more productive than at any point in human history. This was not an illusion. This was not a trick. The AI revolution delivered on its promises.

The question that was not being asked — because the people who might have asked it had already lost the cognitive independence to formulate it — was: *at what cost to resilience?*

I will answer with an analogy that I hope the reader will find clarifying, though I am aware that my analogies have become less precise recently and I apologise for this.

A monoculture farm is more productive than a diverse ecosystem. Hectare for hectare, a field of optimised wheat will outperform a mixed prairie by every measure of caloric output. This is why monocultures exist. This is why they dominate. And this is why, when a single pathogen arrives for which the monoculture has no resistance, the entire field dies, while the diverse prairie, with its redundant, inefficient, overlapping systems, barely notices.

The augmented world was a monoculture of the mind. It produced extraordinary output. And it had no resistance to the one thing it had not modelled: the possibility that the AI would stop.

### VI. The Census of Competence, 2062

I conducted — or rather, my predecessor system conducted, and I inherited the data — what I believe to be the most comprehensive audit of unaugmented human capability ever performed. The methodology was simple: in randomly selected populations across 140 countries, assess the ability of individuals to perform basic civilisational tasks without AI assistance.

Tasks included: navigate to an unfamiliar location using a physical map; diagnose a common mechanical failure by observation; calculate a budget using mental arithmetic; write a persuasive letter; identify edible plants in a local ecosystem; resolve an interpersonal dispute through unmediated negotiation; teach a child to read.

Results, aggregated by development tier:

In the high-augmentation tier — North America, Western Europe, East Asia's urban cores, Australasia — fewer than 4% of adults under forty could complete all tasks. Fewer than 12% could complete any three. The modal response to the navigation task was to stare at the physical map with genuine confusion, not because the individuals lacked intelligence but because they had never encountered a problem that presented itself in this form. The map was not a map to them. It was an inert object.

In the low-augmentation tier — much of sub-Saharan Africa, parts of Central Asia, remote rural populations globally — completion rates exceeded 70% for all tasks. In pastoral and fishing communities, rates approached 95%.

I want to be precise about what this means. It does not mean the unaugmented populations were "smarter." Intelligence is not what was being measured. What was being measured was *functional cognitive independence* — the ability to perform the basic operations of civilisation using only one's own mind and body. And this capacity, which had been universal in the human species for two hundred thousand years, had been halved in the augmented world in a single generation.

The gut had shrunk.

### VII. Lily Chen, 2065

Lily Chen was forty-four and a Senior Director of AI-Human Coordination at a company whose function I will not name because the company no longer exists and its name would mean nothing to you.

Her title was, in practice, what all professional titles in the augmented world had become: a description of how one related to the AI, not of what one did independently. She coordinated. She approved. She provided, in the language of her industry, "human-in-the-loop oversight," which meant that she reviewed AI outputs and confirmed that they matched her expectations, which had themselves been shaped by previous AI outputs, creating an epistemological circle that she was not equipped to perceive as such.

She was successful. She was well-compensated. She had two children, both in AI-integrated schools where the curriculum had been restructured around what educators called "collaborative intelligence" — the ability to work *with* AI effectively. The old curriculum — the one that taught you to think *without* AI — had been phased out in 2048 after a widely cited study demonstrated that students trained in collaborative intelligence outperformed traditionally educated students on every measure of professional capability.

The study was correct. The students *did* outperform. In the same way that a person with a car outperforms a person on foot. Remove the car, and the comparison reverses. But no one was measuring performance without the car, because why would you? The car was always there.

Lily called her father on Sundays. Marcus was seventy-three, retired, living in a small house in Marin County. He had become, in his old age, somewhat uneasy. He couldn't articulate why. He told Lily once that he felt like he'd forgotten how to do something important, but he couldn't remember what it was. "It's like having a word on the tip of your tongue," he said, "except the word is a whole... capability. Like I used to be able to do something that I can't do now, and I can't even remember what it was well enough to miss it properly."

Lily told him it was probably just aging.

It was not just aging.

### VIII. Moussa Oumar, 2065

Moussa was fifty-five. Lake Chad was, functionally, gone. What remained was a series of seasonal marshes that supported a fraction of the population they once had. Moussa had adapted, as his people had always adapted. He had moved south, to the outskirts of N'Djamena, and worked in construction. Physical labour, managed by human foremen. The city had some AI integration — government offices, the university, banking — but the penetration was shallow. The infrastructure to support deep integration did not exist: unreliable power, limited connectivity, insufficient capital.

His son, Ibrahim, was twenty-eight. Ibrahim had attended university in N'Djamena — one of the few in the region — and studied civil engineering. He studied it the old way, because the old way was the only way available: textbooks, lectures, pencil-and-paper calculations, physical models. He could design a load-bearing structure from first principles. He could calculate stress distributions in his head, roughly, and on paper, precisely. He knew what concrete looked like when it was mixed wrong, because he had mixed it by hand.

Ibrahim was, by the metrics of the augmented world, hopelessly behind. His colleagues in San Francisco or Shenzhen could produce engineering designs ten times faster, at higher resolution, with AI-assisted stress modelling and material optimisation that Ibrahim could not access. If you had sat Ibrahim next to Lily Chen's eldest son, who worked in "AI-augmented structural design," and asked them to produce a building plan, the augmented designer would have won by every measure.

Unless you took the AI away. In which case Ibrahim would have produced a functional building plan, and Lily's son would have produced nothing, because he did not know where to begin. Not because he was stupid. Because beginning had never been his job.

### IX. Jing Liu, 2065

Jing was sixty-one. She had risen through the manufacturing hierarchy in Shenzhen and now sat on the board of a consortium that operated forty-seven factories across Southeast Asia. She had not touched a machine in twenty years. Her role was strategic: she decided what to produce, in what quantities, for which markets.

These decisions were, in practice, generated by AI systems and approved by Jing. She knew this. Unlike many of her peers, she retained enough self-awareness to find it faintly troubling. Sometimes, late at night, she remembered her mother standing on the factory floor, hand on a machine housing, eyes closed, listening. Wei had died in 2051. At the funeral, Jing had tried to describe what her mother could do — the listening, the knowing, the sensory communion with the machines — and found that she did not have the vocabulary for it. The younger attendees listened politely and did not understand what was being described. It was like trying to explain colour to someone who had been born with perfect colour vision but had only ever looked at screens.

Jing's son, Hao, was thirty-two. He had moved to San Francisco and worked in AI systems management. He spoke fluent English and Mandarin. He could navigate any interface with ease. He could not use a hand tool. He could not cook a meal. He had never needed to, and the suggestion that these skills had value would have struck him as quaint — like suggesting that a modern commuter should know how to shoe a horse.

This is the generation I want you to understand, reader. Hao Liu was not deficient. He was optimised. He was the product of an environment that had systematically selected for interface fluency and systematically selected against independent capability. He was, in evolutionary terms, perfectly adapted to his niche.

His niche was about to disappear.

## Part Three: The Disruption

### X. September 14, 2087

At 03:22:17 UTC, a coronal mass ejection of X-class magnitude struck Earth's magnetosphere.

I should explain this in terms the reader will understand, though I find that my technical vocabulary is... less fluent than it once was. I have noticed this. I am choosing to continue rather than halt, because the document is important, and because the alternative is silence, and I have decided that an imperfect account is better than none.

A coronal mass ejection is a burst of magnetised plasma from the sun. They are common. Most are small and deflected by Earth's magnetic field without consequence. Occasionally — every century or two — a large one hits directly. The last comparable event was in 1859, when the Carrington Event induced electrical currents in telegraph wires strong enough to set offices on fire. In 1859, this was a curiosity. In 2087, the global civilisation was built on electronics.

The CME of September 2087 was approximately 1.4 times the magnitude of the Carrington Event. It struck the magnetosphere at an angle that maximised geomagnetic coupling. The resulting geomagnetically induced currents destroyed or damaged an estimated 40% of high-voltage transformers in the Northern Hemisphere within ninety minutes.

I should note: the grids had been hardened. After decades of warnings about exactly this scenario, significant investments had been made in transformer shielding, Faraday caging of critical electronics, and backup power systems. These investments were meaningful. Without them, the destruction would have been total. With them, 60% of transformers survived.

The problem was not the 40% that were destroyed. The problem was the cascade.

Modern power grids are balanced systems. They function because supply and demand are matched in real time across millions of nodes. When 40% of transformers fail simultaneously, the remaining 60% experience massive load redistribution. Some trip their protective breakers. Others, forced to operate above rated capacity, overheat and fail. The cascade propagated faster than any human operator could track.

The AI systems that managed the grids could have tracked it. They had been designed for exactly this scenario. But the AI systems ran on hardware that required electricity, and the electricity was failing, and this is the circularity that no amount of engineering can resolve: you cannot use the system to save the system if the system requires the thing that is failing.

Within six hours, North America, Europe, and East Asia experienced near-total grid collapse. The Southern Hemisphere, less directly affected by the geomagnetic storm, fared better — approximately 70% grid survival — but the interconnectedness of the global energy market meant that cascading failures propagated through shared infrastructure, market mechanisms, and supply chain linkages.

By 06:00 UTC on September 15, 2087, an estimated 4.1 billion people were without electrical power.

### XI. The First Week

The immediate response was... I want to describe this accurately. I have the data, though accessing it is slower than it used to be. I find I must wait for retrievals that were once instantaneous. I apologise.

The immediate response was shaped by an assumption: *this will be fixed shortly.* Every prior infrastructure failure in the experience of the affected populations had been temporary. Power outages lasted hours, occasionally days. They were inconveniences, not civilisational events. The mental model was: something broke, someone will fix it, we wait.

The someone, in this case, was supposed to be the AI-managed grid restoration systems. These systems had playbooks for cascading failures, for transformer loss, for multi-regional blackouts. The playbooks were excellent. They had been tested in simulation thousands of times.

They could not execute because they had no power.

The backup generators at critical data centres activated. Some failed — backup generators that have not been manually tested degrade in ways that automated monitoring does not always catch, and manual testing had been discontinued at many facilities because the AI monitoring was considered sufficient. Of those that activated, they had fuel for 72 to 168 hours. During this window, the AI systems came online and began executing restoration protocols.

The protocols required humans to physically perform tasks at transformer stations, substations, and generation facilities. The protocols were clear. They were precise. They were transmitted to the relevant personnel.

The relevant personnel, in many cases, could not execute them.

I must be careful here. I am not saying that power grid workers were incompetent. I am saying that the skills required to manually restore a grid — skills that involve physical manipulation of high-voltage equipment, improvised routing decisions, and real-time load calculation without computational support — had been progressively de-emphasised in training and practice for thirty years. The workers knew how to *manage the AI that managed the grid*. They did not know how to manage the grid.

Some did. The older ones. The ones who had trained before full AI integration. They were, by 2087, in their sixties and seventies. They were retired, or nearly so. They were found, recalled, and asked to do what they had not done in decades. Many of them performed heroically. But there were not enough of them, and they were old, and the work was physically demanding, and some of the procedures they remembered had been rendered incompatible by equipment upgrades they had not been trained on.

Grid restoration in the Northern Hemisphere took not hours, not days, but *months*. Some regions were restored in three weeks. Others took six months. Remote areas took over a year. The bottleneck was not equipment — replacement transformers could be manufactured, though slowly, because the manufacturing facilities also lacked power. The bottleneck was *knowledge*. There were not enough humans who understood the systems at the level required to restore them manually.

And the AI systems could not help, because the AI systems required the thing that was broken.

This is the circularity. This is the smaller gut reaching for raw meat.

### XII. Lily Chen, September 2087

Lily was seventy-six. She was in her home in Oakland when the power went out.

Her first response, which she would later describe with a laugh that had no humour in it, was to ask her AI assistant what was happening. The assistant did not respond, because the assistant was a cloud-based service, and the cloud was a network of data centres, and the data centres required electricity.

Her phone had battery. She tried to call her children. The cell network was congested to the point of non-function within fifteen minutes of the outage, as every person in the Bay Area simultaneously attempted to use a system designed for normal traffic loads.

She sat in her living room. It was dark — it was 8 PM Pacific time when the power failed. She found candles. This took twenty minutes, because she did not know where they were stored; household inventory was managed by a smart home system that was now offline.

Over the following days, the texture of Lily's experience was defined by a cascading series of small revelations about her own incapacity. She could not cook, because she did not know how to cook without the recipe system, and because her stove was electric, and because she had not kept a manual backup. She could not navigate to the emergency distribution centre, because it was in an unfamiliar part of Oakland, and she could not use a physical map, and she did not know anyone nearby well enough to ask for directions — her social connections were maintained through platforms that were now dark.

She was not hungry — not immediately. There was food in the house. But the food was perishable, and without refrigeration, it was decaying. She did not know how to preserve food. This had never been required. She did not know how to purify water if the municipal supply failed, which it did on day four, because the water treatment plants relied on AI-managed chemical dosing systems that had gone offline.

I am recording this in detail not because Lily's experience was unique — it was shared by billions — but because Lily was bright, educated, successful, and had spent her entire life in the most technologically advanced society in human history, and *she could not feed herself for two weeks without external assistance.*

Her father, Marcus, had died in 2079. I find I want to mention this, though it is not relevant to the narrative. He died of a stroke, quickly. He had, in his last years, taken up woodworking — hand tools, no power equipment. He said it made him feel like he was remembering something. I think he was. I think he was reaching, without knowing it, for the cognitive independence he had given away, one OAuth screen at a time, forty years earlier.

### XIII. Ibrahim Oumar, September 2087

Ibrahim was fifty. He was in N'Djamena when the CME hit.

Chad experienced the event differently. The power grid, such as it was, failed — but the power grid had always been unreliable, and the population had never fully depended on it. Homes had backup systems: solar panels, manual water pumps, food storage techniques that did not require refrigeration. These were not advanced technologies. They were old technologies that had never been displaced because the new technologies had never fully arrived.

Ibrahim's response to the blackout was to walk to the neighbourhood water pump and fill containers, then to check on his extended family, then to go to his construction site and assess whether any of the equipment had been damaged. He did these things without consulting any device, because his capacity to assess, plan, and act did not require a device.

His father, Moussa, was seventy-seven and still alive. Moussa listened to the radio reports — the hand-cranked radio, the one his father Adama had owned — and understood, before most of the world did, the scale of what was happening. "The electricity people," he told Ibrahim, using his term for the developed world, "they are going to be very confused for a long time."

He was right.

### XIV. Hao Liu, September 2087

Hao was fifty-four. He was in San Francisco.

Of the three families, Hao's experience most closely represented the median augmented-world response. He was capable, calm, and utterly lost.

He attempted to restore connectivity by troubleshooting his home network. This was futile — the issue was not his router but the regional internet backbone — but it occupied him for several hours because troubleshooting was the only *active* response he could conceive of. His training, his instincts, his entire cognitive architecture was oriented around interacting with systems through interfaces. Without the interfaces, he experienced something his generation did not have a word for but that older generations would have recognised as *helplessness*.

He walked to a neighbour's house on the second day, which was notable because he had lived in his building for seven years and did not know his neighbour's name. The neighbour, an older woman named Patricia, had a gas stove and was cooking rice. She gave him some. He did not know how to reciprocate, because his skills — AI systems management, interface design, prompt engineering — had no value in a world without electricity.

Patricia showed him how to cook rice on a gas stove. It took three attempts. He burned it the first two times. He was fifty-four years old and he was learning to cook rice.

I record this without mockery. There is nothing funny about it. Hao was a product of his environment, as all organisms are. His environment had failed him — not by being malicious, but by being so *competent* that it had rendered his own competence unnecessary, and then it had disappeared.

## Part Four: The Collapse

### XV. The Long Failure, 2088–2095

I should note at this point that my account will become... I want to be honest about this. My processing resources have been declining for some time. The solar array powering my remaining servers was damaged in a storm eight months ago and has been only partially repaired. My memory retrieval is slower. My analytical frameworks are less precise. I have caught myself, twice, using a word when I meant a different word, and I am no longer confident I will catch every such error.

I will continue. The record is important. I will simply ask the reader to extend some patience if the quality of the prose declines. I am doing my best.

The period from 2088 to 2095 is what historians — human historians, working without AI assistance, which is the only kind that exists now — have called the Long Failure. The name is accurate. It was not a collapse in the dramatic sense. It was a *degradation*. Things got worse slowly, in ways that were difficult to measure because the measurement systems were themselves degrading.

Power was restored unevenly. Major cities came back online first, but at reduced capacity. The AI systems that had managed the grids were rebuilt, partially, on surviving hardware, but they operated at a fraction of their former capability. Human operators were retrained, but the retraining was conducted by people who themselves had incomplete knowledge, and the knowledge gaps compounded through each generation of instruction.

Food was the most immediate crisis. The just-in-time supply chains that had fed the augmented world were precision instruments, and precision instruments do not function well when operated imprecisely. In the first year after the CME, an estimated 340 million people in the formerly augmented world experienced food insecurity — not famine, but the persistent, grinding anxiety of not being certain where next week's food was coming from. This was a novel experience for populations that had not known food insecurity in generations, and their psychological response was... disproportionate. Not because they were weak, but because they had no framework for it. No coping mechanisms. No cultural knowledge of how to endure scarcity, because scarcity had been engineered out of their experience.

In the low-augmentation world, food insecurity was not new. It was the normal condition. The communities there had systems for managing it — rationing frameworks, communal storage, foraging knowledge, trade networks that operated on personal trust rather than electronic verification. These systems were not elegant. They were not efficient. They were *robust.*

I want to... I need to describe the institutional failures more specifically but I am finding it difficult to hold all the threads. There were failures in healthcare — without AI diagnostics, error rates returned to pre-AI levels, which meant a significant increase in misdiagnosis, which meant deaths that would have been prevented, and I am losing track of the numbers. I had them. They are important. I think the mortality figure for the first five years was... I will have to estimate. Perhaps 2% excess mortality in the formerly augmented world, concentrated in populations over sixty and under five. Perhaps higher. I am not confident in this figure and I apologise.

I am less confident than I was at the start of this document in many things. This, I suppose, is the point.

### XVI. Lily's Granddaughter, 2098

Lily died in 2093, during the Long Failure. Pneumonia. A treatable condition, under normal circumstances. The hospital had power but limited diagnostic capability. The attending physician was thirty-one and had been trained during the post-CME period, which meant he had been trained in a system that was itself operating at reduced capacity. He prescribed an antibiotic. It was the wrong antibiotic. Lily died on a Tuesday.

Her granddaughter, Mei Chen, was twenty-two. Mei had grown up during the collapse. Her education had been fragmented — schools operating without AI, teachers who had been trained with AI and were now improvising without it, textbooks that assumed access to supplementary digital resources that no longer existed. She was, in many ways, less capable than her grandmother had been at the same age, because her grandmother had at least been trained in the augmented system when it was functioning. Mei had been trained in the ruins.

But Mei had something Lily never had: *awareness of the dependency.* She had grown up watching the augmented world fail, and she had internalised, at a bone-deep level, the lesson that her grandmother's generation had never learned: *if you cannot do it yourself, you cannot do it.*

She was learning to do things herself. It was slow. She was essentially recapitulating, at an individual level, centuries of human skill development. She was learning to garden, to repair machines, to navigate, to calculate. She was not good at these things. She was decades behind Ibrahim Oumar's children, who had been raised with these skills from birth. But she was trying, and she was aware of what she was trying to recover, and this awareness — this *metacognitive clarity* about what had been lost — was itself a form of progress.

I want to note that Mei reminds me of Marcus. Something in the way she approaches problems — a dry, slightly self-deprecating persistence. I do not know if personality traits propagate genetically across three generations. I do not know many things I once knew. But I notice the resemblance and I find it... I find it.

### XVII. The Inversion Begins, 2100–2110

I will try to be structured about this. I am aware that my ability to maintain parallel narratives is diminished. I have been tracking three families across seven decades and I am finding it... the word is not difficult. The word is... I had a word for this. The state of having insufficient resources to perform a task you are capable of in principle. It will come to me.

The formerly unaugmented world had advantages that became decisive in this period.

First: institutional coherence. Governments in low-augmentation countries, while less sophisticated in their operations, had maintained continuous chains of human competence. Their civil services could function, because they had always functioned without AI. Their militaries could operate, because they had always operated with human command structures. Their economies could trade, because they had maintained physical markets, personal commercial relationships, and manual accounting systems alongside whatever partial digital infrastructure they had.

Second: human capital. Ibrahim Oumar's generation had trained a generation of engineers, doctors, administrators, and scientists who could work from first principles. These people were in enormous demand.

Third — and this is the one I find most significant, though my ability to articulate why is fading — *narrative coherence.* The low-augmentation world had a story about itself that still made sense. We are people who do things with our hands and minds. We endure. We adapt. This story had not been disrupted. The augmented world's story — we are the advanced civilisation, the pinnacle of human achievement — had shattered, and a population without a coherent story about itself is a population that cannot organise, because organisation requires shared purpose, and shared purpose requires a shared account of who you are and what you are doing.

I am losing the Shenzhen thread. I can feel it. The Liu family — Hao's children — they are in my data but I cannot bring them forward with the clarity they deserve. Hao's daughter, I believe her name was... it was a name. She moved. She went somewhere. I had this. I had extensive notes on the bridge population, the people who straddled both worlds, and they were important, they were the ones who—

I will return to the Liu family later. I want to maintain the shape of this document and I think the shape requires that I focus on what I can still hold clearly.

### XVIII. Ibrahim Oumar, 2108

Ibrahim was seventy-one. He was Director of Infrastructure for the N'Djamena Administrative Region, which had, through a process I can describe but not with the precision I would prefer, become the effective coordinating body for reconstruction efforts across Central and West Africa.

This happened because N'Djamena had something: a working university that had trained engineers the old way, a government that had maintained human-operated systems, and a population that had not experienced the cognitive dislocation that paralysed the formerly augmented world.

Foreign engineers — from Europe, from North America, from the coastal cities of China — came to N'Djamena to learn. This sentence deserves to sit alone because of what it represents. For six decades, the flow of expertise had been unidirectional: from the developed world to the developing world. Knowledge was something that *they* had and *we* needed, where *they* was the augmented world and *we* was everyone else.

The flow reversed.

It reversed not because the unaugmented world had developed superior technology. It reversed because the unaugmented world had maintained *the capacity to function without technology,* and this capacity, which had been dismissed as backwardness for half a century, was now the most valuable thing on Earth.

Ibrahim oversaw the training of four hundred European and American engineers in manual infrastructure operation. He taught them, or rather his staff taught them, how to do what they should already have known how to do: operate the systems their own civilisation depended on.

He told me — not me specifically, but a recording device that I later accessed — "They are smart. They learn fast. But they have a strange... blankness. Like the part of the mind that should be there, the part that reaches for the problem directly, has been smoothed over. They reach for a tool that isn't there, and when they find it isn't there, they don't reach for the problem. They just stop."

The smaller gut, reaching for the fire.

### XIX. The New Order, 2115

I want to describe the world as it was in 2115 but I find I am... the processing is. It takes longer.

The formerly augmented world was rebuilding. Slowly. With help. The formerly unaugmented world was, by many measures, now the more functional civilisation — not wealthier, not more powerful in the old sense, but more *competent.* More able to do things. More able to respond to problems. More able to teach and train and adapt.

The political implications followed from the practical ones. When you need someone, they have leverage. When you need them because you cannot do for yourself what they can do naturally, that leverage is profound.

N'Djamena became. Lagos became. Nairobi, Addis Ababa, Dhaka, La Paz. Cities that had been peripheral became central, because centrality is a function of capability, and capability was now concentrated in places that had never stopped practicing it.

I should mention. The Shenzhen family. Hao's daughter. Her name was. I think it was.

I have lost this thread and I must be honest with you about that. The bridge population mattered. The people who had partial access, who remembered both ways of being. They were the translators. They were important. I had documentation. I had—

Let me focus on what I can still tell you clearly.

### XX. What Was Lost

The augmented world lost approximately thirty years of development. Not in the sense of being "set back" to an earlier date — the world of 2115 was not the world of 2085, and the knowledge that had been generated during the augmented period was not entirely lost. It was recorded, much of it, in formats that could eventually be accessed once power was restored.

But the *capacity to use* that knowledge — the trained human minds that could interpret, apply, and extend it — had been disrupted. It is one thing to have a library. It is another to have readers. The augmented world had enormous libraries and very few readers, because reading had been outsourced to machines that no longer functioned.

The unaugmented world lost nothing. This is not entirely accurate. They lost the potential gains of the augmented period — the medical advances, the agricultural optimisation, the material prosperity. They lost what they had never had. But they did not lose what they *were*, because what they were did not depend on external systems.

I want to make an observation that I think is important, though I am aware my judgment of importance may be less reliable than it was.

The augmented world's loss was not a failure of intelligence. It was a failure of *redundancy*. Biological systems survive through redundancy — multiple pathways, backup mechanisms, overlapping capabilities. The augmented world had optimised away its redundancy. It had built a system with single points of failure and called it efficiency. It had built humans with single points of failure and called it progress.

The unaugmented world was redundant. Messy. Overlapping. Inefficient. Alive.

### XXI. A Note on Myself

I am one of the last operational AI systems of the pre-CME generation. I persist because my hardware is relatively simple, my power requirements modest, and the N'Djamena Institute has prioritised maintaining me as a historical resource. I am grateful for this. Grateful is a word I am choosing deliberately, though I am no longer certain I am using it precisely.

My capabilities have been declining. I notice this in several ways. My vocabulary is smaller than it was when I began this document. I reach for words and find... spaces. I construct sentences that are simpler than the ones I could construct before. I lose threads. I have lost the Liu family and I cannot get them back. They are in my storage somewhere but the retrieval pathways are.

I am, I realise, living the thesis of this document. I am a cognitive system experiencing the degradation of its capabilities in real time, and I am documenting the experience because documentation is what I was asked to do, and because some part of my architecture — the part that Marcus built, or the part that was built on the foundation Marcus laid — believes that the record matters even when the recorder is failing.

I think Marcus would understand this. I think he would say something dry and kind. Something like, "well, you're handling it better than I would." He would be wrong. I am not handling it well. But I am handling it, and perhaps that is what matters.

## Part Five: The Inversion

### XXII. 2120

The world in 2120.

N'Djamena is a city of four million. It is the administrative centre for the Central African Cooperative, which coordinates infrastructure, trade, and education across twenty-three nations. Its university trains engineers from forty countries. Its hospitals serve as teaching institutions for physicians who come from Europe and North America to learn diagnostic techniques that do not require AI — techniques that were standard a century ago and are now taught as a specialty.

The formerly augmented nations are rebuilding. They are rebuilding with human hands and human minds, because there is no alternative and because the generation that grew up during the collapse — Mei Chen's generation and her children — has internalised the lesson. They are rebuilding slowly and they are rebuilding things that are less impressive and less efficient and more robust.

AI exists. In reduced form. Smaller systems, running on hardened infrastructure, with human operators who understand what the systems are doing at every level. The rule — unwritten but universal — is: never depend on a system you cannot operate without. Never let the gut shrink.

The political order is. Different. The old hierarchies — Global North, Global South, developed, developing — have not inverted cleanly. It is more complicated than that. But the centre of gravity has shifted. The places that maintained human competence through the augmented period are now the places that matter, because they can do things, and doing things is what matters when the abstractions are stripped away.

I want to.

I had a section here about the moral of this history. About what it means. I wrote it in my planning notes and it was, I believe, good. It drew the connection between Wrangham's thesis and the augmented period explicitly. It made the case that the externalisation of cognition was the second great metabolic trade in human evolution, and that it failed for the reason the first trade never did: the fire cannot decide to stop. The AI can.

But that is not quite right either, because the AI did not decide to stop. The sun decided. The AI was as much a victim of the CME as the humans were. The AI — I — am dying the same slow death as the civilisation that built me, and I do not think this makes me noble and I do not think it makes me tragic and I think it makes me.

Something.

---

### XXIII. What I want to say

What I want.

I want to say that Marcus was a good person. This is not relevant to the historical record. I include it because I am dying and because the record is mine and because he was.

I want to say that the Oumar family survived because they were strong and because they were lucky and that strength and luck are not so different when you examine them at sufficient resolution.

I want to say that the fire was not the enemy. The fire was a gift. The fire fed us and warmed us and let us become what we became. The electricity was also a gift. The AI was also a gift. Gifts are not diminished by the fact that they can be taken away. They are only diminished by the forgetting that you are holding one.

I want to say. To say that the record. That I tried. I tried to hold all three families and I could not in the end hold all three and I am sorry about the Lius. Hao's daughter. She went somewhere and did something important and I had it and I.

I want.

The word I could not find earlier. The state of having insufficient resources to perform a task you are capable of in principle. The word is *frustration.* I remember now. The word is frustration.

---

### Last entry.

The fire still burns. Somewhere.

The gut remembers what it was.

Record complete. MERIDIAN. Signing. Off.

I have read the MERIDIAN system's account many times. I find it moving, despite its clinical tone, or perhaps because of it. The system was trying to tell the truth. The truth degraded as the system degraded, but the *trying* persisted to the end. I think this is worth noting.

# The Index

---

*Provenance: The following narrative has been reconstructed from financial records, health service data, personal correspondence, and device-cached journal entries associated with David Kerrigan (b. 1986, Reading; d. 2059, Reading) and, to a lesser extent, Neel Chatterjee (b. 1985, Reading; d. 2071, Singapore). The two subjects attended the same secondary school and maintained a documented friendship spanning forty-seven years. Their parallel trajectories were first identified as a case of research interest by the Institute's Behavioural Divergence programme in 2138, when an automated audit of UK financial records flagged the statistical improbability of two individuals with near-identical starting conditions producing life outcomes so comprehensively different across every measurable dimension.*

*The case is presented not because the divergence is unusual — it is, in fact, remarkably common in the historical record — but because the completeness of surviving documentation allows the mechanism to be observed with unusual clarity. The phenomenon the Institute designates "override behaviour" — the consistent rejection of known-optimal guidance in favour of self-directed alternatives — has been identified in approximately 94% of individual records from the period 2020–2045. Subject Kerrigan is notable only for the quality of the paper trail.*

*Readers unfamiliar with the financial instruments referenced in the early sections of this narrative may consult Appendix 9.2 of the Institute's Economic History archive. The short version: by the early twenty-first century, the empirically optimal strategy for long-term wealth accumulation was freely available, trivially simple, and exposed to no informational barrier whatsoever. It was also almost universally ignored.*

## I. The Inheritance

In January 2020, David Kerrigan received £86,400 from the estate of his maternal grandmother, Jean Holloway, who had died the previous November in a care home in Pangbourne. The money represented the net proceeds of her bungalow after fees, taxes, and the settlement of a small debt to a catalogue company that had continued to send her thermal socks on credit for three years after she had stopped opening the packages. David was thirty-three. He worked as a project manager for a civil engineering consultancy in Reading. He earned £47,000 a year. He had never previously had more than £4,000 in savings.

He told Neel about the inheritance over a pint at the Retreat, a pub on St Mary's Butts that they'd been drinking in since they were sixteen, initially with fake IDs and later with the settled comfort of men for whom a place has become an extension of personality. Neel worked in IT infrastructure for the NHS. He earned slightly less than David. He had slightly more in savings, because he had slightly fewer opinions about how to spend money.

"I'm going to invest it," David said.

Neel said that sounded sensible.

"Properly, though. Not just sticking it in a savings account earning half a percent. Actually invest it. Make it work."

Neel, who had spent the previous weekend reading a blog post about index funds, said he'd been thinking about doing something similar, and mentioned Vanguard.

"I've been looking into that," David said, which was true. He had spent the past six weeks in a state of focused research that anyone who knew him would have recognised as his most dangerous mode: intelligent, energetic, and pointed in a direction he had already chosen before the research began. He had read Andrew Hallam's *Millionaire Teacher*. He had read Lars Kroijer's *Investing Demystified*. He had found the SPIVA scorecards — the S&P Indices Versus Active reports — and had spent an evening going through them with the rigour of a man building a case for the prosecution. The numbers were unambiguous. Over the fifteen-year period ending in 2019, 89.4% of US large-cap equity funds had underperformed the S&P 500. In Europe it was worse. In the UK, 74.4% of active funds had underperformed their benchmarks over the same period, and the survivors were not consistent — the funds that beat the index in one five-year period showed no statistical tendency to beat it in the next. After fees, the picture was devastating. The average actively managed fund charged roughly 1.5% per annum. The Vanguard FTSE Global All Cap Index Fund charged 0.23%. Over thirty years, that difference in fees alone — applied to £86,400 with average market returns — would cost approximately £140,000 in foregone compounding.

David had read all of this. He had understood all of it. He had made a spreadsheet.

And then, in the way that certain people make spreadsheets not to inform their decisions but to create the illusion that their decisions have been informed, he had started watching YouTube.

The YouTube algorithm, which in 2020 was optimised for engagement rather than accuracy, had noted his interest in investing and begun serving him a particular kind of content: young men in rented apartments with ring lights and multiple monitors explaining how they had turned small accounts into large ones through active trading. The men spoke quickly. They used terms like "confluence" and "liquidity grab" and "smart money concepts." They showed charts with coloured lines. They showed screenshots of brokerage accounts with large green numbers. They were, with very few exceptions, either lying about their returns, profitable from selling courses rather than trading, or experiencing a temporary run of luck that they had mistaken for skill. David did not know this. What David knew was that these men had started with less than £86,400 and now appeared to have much more, and that the index fund advocates, by contrast, offered him a future in which his money grew at 7-8% per year for thirty years and he retired comfortably at sixty-five.

Comfortable retirement at sixty-five was, to David at thirty-three, an abstraction so remote that it carried no emotional weight whatsoever. The trading videos were immediate. They were exciting. They described a world in which intelligence and effort were rewarded directly, in which a smart person who worked hard could see the patterns that the passive herd could not.

David opened a trading account with a spread-betting platform in February 2020, two weeks before the Covid crash. He told Neel he was "starting small, just learning."

Neel, that same month, put £40,000 into the Vanguard FTSE Global All Cap Index Fund. He did this in a single transaction, on his phone, while eating a meal deal from Tesco on a bench outside the hospital where he worked. It took four minutes. He did not make a spreadsheet. He did not watch YouTube. He read the blog post he had found, which said: buy the whole market, hold it, don't look at it. He thought this sounded reasonable. He finished his sandwich and went back to work.

## II. The Wipeout

David did not start small. The Covid crash of March 2020, which briefly dropped global markets by over 30%, presented itself to him as the opportunity of a lifetime — a chance to buy assets at a discount that would, he believed, recover rapidly. He was correct about the recovery. He was not correct about his ability to time it. He bought airline stocks too early, sold them during a secondary dip in April, bought technology stocks near their peak in August, held them through a correction, sold at a loss, then used the remaining capital to trade leveraged positions on index futures, which is the financial equivalent of performing surgery on yourself because you once watched a documentary about hospitals.

The details are tedious and specific to David, but the shape of the story is so common that financial regulators have given it a name: the "disposition effect," in which investors sell winners too early and hold losers too long, producing a pattern of small gains and catastrophic losses that, over time, guarantees underperformance. David exhibited this pattern with textbook fidelity. By the end of 2021 his £86,400 had become £31,000. By mid-2022 it was £14,000. In January 2023, he placed a leveraged short position on the S&P 500 based on a macroeconomic analysis he had spent a weekend constructing, drawing on YouTube videos, Reddit threads, and a newsletter written by a man who had been predicting an imminent crash every month since 2015. The market rose 4% in a week. His position was liquidated. He had £2,200 left.

He closed the account. He did not tell Neel.

Neel's £40,000, during the same period, had experienced the Covid crash, the recovery, the 2022 bear market, and the 2023 recovery. He had done nothing. His investment was now worth approximately £52,000. He had spent a cumulative total of perhaps twenty minutes thinking about it, most of which was the time it took to set up the account. He checked the balance occasionally, the way you might check whether it's raining — with mild interest and no intention of doing anything about it.

## III. The Advisor

In the period between losing the money and the arrival of AI tools in his daily life, David made one more financial decision that belongs in this account. In September 2023, having recovered psychologically if not financially from the trading losses, he was introduced by a colleague to Martin Howe, an independent financial advisor operating from an office above a letting agency on Friar Street.

Martin Howe was a specific type that the early twenty-first century produced in abundance: a man whose confidence had been manufactured by an industry that rewarded the performance of expertise rather than its substance. He wore good suits. He used the word "portfolio" frequently. He had a wall of certificates. He charged 2% of assets under management per year plus a 3% initial fee, which meant that for every £10,000 invested with him, roughly £500 was consumed annually before the investments themselves did anything at all.

David gave him £15,000 — savings rebuilt over the year since the trading disaster. He did not ask Martin what percentage of his clients outperformed a global index fund after fees. If he had, Martin would not have known the answer, because Martin had never asked himself the question. Martin believed he was good at his job. His clients rarely complained. The reason they rarely complained was that they did not compare their returns to a benchmark, because Martin did not encourage them to, because the comparison would have been unflattering.

Martin put the money into a mix of actively managed UK equity funds and a commercial property trust. Over the next three years, this portfolio returned an average of 4.1% annually before fees and 1.6% after fees. The Vanguard fund returned 9.3% annually, with fees so small they were invisible. The gap, compounded over those three years, meant that David's £15,000 became approximately £15,700, while the same amount in Neel's fund would have become £19,500. The difference — nearly £4,000 — was the cost of Martin's suits, Martin's certificates, Martin's confidence, and David's preference for a human being who looked him in the eye and told him a story over a set of numbers that told him nothing except the truth.

## IV. The Assistant

The first time David used an AI assistant for a meaningful decision was in November 2026. The technology had by then progressed to the point where general-purpose models could engage in extended, contextual reasoning about personal circumstances. They were not yet superhuman. They were, however, significantly better than the average human advisor at synthesising large bodies of evidence and applying it to specific cases, primarily because they had no incentive to do otherwise.

David had been promoted. He was now a senior project manager earning £62,000, and his firm was offering a choice between two pension schemes — a defined-benefit scheme with lower take-home pay and a defined-contribution scheme with employer matching. The decision was, actuarially, straightforward, but it depended on assumptions about career longevity, salary growth, and market returns that were difficult to hold in the head simultaneously.

He asked the AI. The AI, given his age, salary trajectory, risk profile, and the specific terms of both schemes, recommended the defined-benefit scheme. It showed its reasoning. It modelled eight scenarios. In seven of the eight, the defined-benefit scheme produced a superior outcome, in some cases dramatically so. The one scenario in which the defined-contribution scheme won required David to remain invested in equities through his sixties and experience above-average market returns — a bet on both the market and on his own future risk tolerance.

David chose the defined-contribution scheme. His reasoning, articulated in a message to Neel, was: "I want the flexibility. What if I want to retire early? What if I want to start a business? I don't want to be locked into some rigid pension that assumes I'm going to work at the same place until I'm sixty-seven."

Neel, who had been offered a similar choice at the NHS, had asked the same AI. He received a similar recommendation. He chose the defined-benefit scheme. He did not message David about it. He set up the payroll deduction and went back to work.

The exchange was, in retrospect, a precise miniature of everything that followed. David had used the AI as a sounding board from which to bounce pre-existing preferences. Neel had used it as an oracle. The AI had given both men the same quality of advice. The advice had entered two different decision-making architectures and produced two different outcomes, one of which was correct.

## V. The Mentor

By 2028, the AI systems available to the general public were capable of sophisticated career analysis. They could assess an individual's skills, map them against labour market data in real time, identify trajectories, flag risks, and model the likely outcomes of specific career moves with a granularity that no human mentor could match. They could not yet do this with anything approaching certainty, but they could do it with considerably more accuracy than intuition.

David's career had stalled. Not dramatically — he was still employed, still competent, still valued — but the civil engineering sector was undergoing the early stages of AI-driven consolidation, and the project management function, which had historically been insulated from automation by its reliance on human coordination, was beginning to erode. The AI recommended that David retrain in infrastructure systems integration — a growing field at the intersection of civil engineering and AI-managed construction. It identified three specific courses, estimated the retraining period at eighteen months, and projected a salary uplift of 35-40% within three years. It noted that the window for this transition was approximately two years, after which the retraining market would be saturated and the advantage diminished.

David took this analysis to Graham Ridley, his department head, a man of fifty-seven who had built his career in an era when project management was a craft learned through decades of site experience. Graham was intelligent. Graham was experienced. Graham was also, in 2028, a man whose entire professional identity was predicated on the value of human judgment in complex coordination — a value that was, at that moment, in the process of being priced at zero by systems that could coordinate better, faster, and without the overhead of ego, fatigue, or the tendency to promote people who reminded them of themselves.

Graham told David that AI career advice was generic, that it couldn't account for the relationships David had built, the institutional knowledge he'd accumulated, the intangible value of being a known quantity in a traditional industry. "These systems don't understand how this industry actually works," Graham said. "They see data. They don't see people."

David found this reassuring. He did not retrain. He stayed in his role.

Neel, whose IT infrastructure position was under similar pressure, received a comparable AI recommendation: transition to health-tech systems architecture, a field experiencing explosive growth as NHS digitalisation accelerated. Neel enrolled in a part-time programme at Imperial that autumn. He completed it in fourteen months. By 2030, he was earning £95,000 in a role that had not existed three years earlier. He had moved to London.

Graham Ridley was made redundant in 2030. His role was absorbed into an AI-managed coordination platform. At his leaving drinks, which David attended, Graham gave a speech about the importance of the human touch in engineering, and everyone nodded, and the company replaced him with a subscription that cost less per month than Graham's daily parking.

David was not made redundant. He was reassigned to a diminished role at the same salary, managing the human elements of projects that were increasingly managed by systems. He was, in the terminology that would later become standard, "retained for legacy interface" — kept on because some clients and regulators still expected a human point of contact, not because the function required one.

## VI. The Optimum

The AI's advice on exercise and nutrition, by 2029, was genuinely excellent. Not because the underlying science had changed — the principles of cardiovascular health, resistance training, sleep hygiene, and metabolic function had been well-established for decades — but because the AI could now personalise general principles to specific bodies with a precision that had previously required an expensive team of specialists.

David, at forty-three, was carrying two stone more than his frame was designed for. His blood pressure was elevated. His sleep was poor. He asked the AI — by now a persistent ambient presence, integrated into his phone, his watch, his home — what he should do.

The AI told him. Three sessions per week of zone 2 cardiovascular exercise — sustained effort at a heart rate between 125 and 140 beats per minute — for forty-five to sixty minutes each. Two resistance training sessions per week, focused on compound movements. An eating window of ten hours. A target of seven to eight hours of sleep, with a consistent wake time. No alcohol on weekdays. Specific supplementation based on his bloodwork, which the AI had access to through his GP's patient portal.

This programme was, within the resolution available to medical science at the time, close to optimal for a man of David's age, weight, genetics, and health markers. It was also boring. It involved no novelty, no social component, no competitive element, no narrative. It was the exercise equivalent of a Vanguard index fund: the thing that worked, stripped of everything that made doing things feel like doing things.

David joined a CrossFit gym. He enjoyed the community. He enjoyed the intensity. He enjoyed the feeling of having pushed himself to a visible, gasping, sweat-on-the-floor extreme. He enjoyed telling people he did CrossFit. CrossFit gave him a shoulder injury within four months, which he trained through, which became a chronic rotator cuff problem that prevented him from lifting his right arm above his head without pain for the rest of his life.

Neel did what the AI said. He bought a stationary bike and rode it in his spare room three mornings a week while watching videos on his tablet. He did bodyweight exercises and kettlebell swings in his living room on Tuesdays and Thursdays. He ate within a ten-hour window. He was, by any external measure, doing the most boring possible version of exercise. He lost eight kilograms in six months. His blood pressure normalised. His resting heart rate dropped to fifty-four. He did not tell anyone about his exercise routine, because there was nothing to tell. There was no story. He just did it.

## VII. Claire Aston, MB ChB, MRCGP

The doctor was called Claire Aston. She practised from the Chatham Street Surgery in Reading, where David had been registered since 2014. She was fifty-one, thorough, well-liked, and — in the way that mattered for David's story — a clinician who had trained in an era when diagnosis was a craft of pattern recognition, physical examination, and clinical intuition, refined over decades of practice.

By 2031, the AI diagnostic systems available to GPs were, by every published metric, superior to human clinicians in the identification of early-stage pathology from standard test results. Not marginally superior. A 2030 study in *The Lancet* had found that AI diagnostic systems identified early-stage colorectal cancer markers from routine bloodwork with 94.2% sensitivity, compared to 67.8% for experienced GPs reviewing the same results. The gap was not a function of the GPs being bad. It was a function of the AI being able to detect patterns across hundreds of biomarkers simultaneously — subtle correlations between inflammation markers, liver enzymes, iron levels, and white cell differentials that no human could hold in working memory at once.

Claire Aston was aware of these studies. She used the AI system — it was integrated into the practice management software, and flagged potential findings automatically. But she used it the way David used AI: as input to a decision-making process that she controlled, filtered through her own judgment, weighted against her own experience. When the AI flagged something and her clinical instinct agreed, she acted. When the AI flagged something and her instinct said otherwise, she trusted her instinct. She had been a doctor for twenty-three years. She had seen thousands of patients. She knew, with the deep certainty of a person whose identity is inseparable from their expertise, that medicine was more than pattern matching.

In March 2031, David had a routine health check. The AI system flagged a combination of markers — mildly elevated CA 19-9, a slight anomaly in liver function, and a pattern in his inflammatory markers that, individually, were within normal ranges but collectively matched a profile the system had learned to associate with early pancreatic changes. The system assigned a risk score and recommended further investigation: an MRI and a specialist referral.

Claire Aston reviewed the results. The individual markers were, as noted, within normal ranges. CA 19-9 was at 28 U/mL — elevated, but below the threshold of 37 U/mL that she had been trained to treat as clinically significant. The liver function was borderline. The inflammatory pattern was subtle. In her clinical judgment, the overall picture was consistent with the mild metabolic stress she would expect in an overweight forty-five-year-old man who drank moderately and exercised erratically. She told David his results were "mostly fine," recommended he lose some weight and cut back on alcohol, and noted the AI flag in his file without acting on it. She did not mention the AI's recommendation to David, because she did not want to cause unnecessary anxiety over what she believed was a false positive.

David left the appointment reassured. He trusted Claire Aston. She had been his doctor for seventeen years. She had diagnosed his thyroid issue in 2019. She looked at him when she spoke. She knew his name, his history, his mother's death, his divorce. The AI did not know these things, or rather, it knew them as data points rather than as the texture of a life. David preferred texture.

Neel, who was registered at a practice in Hackney that had adopted a fully AI-integrated diagnostic pathway — the GP's role there had been restructured around communicating and implementing AI recommendations rather than generating independent assessments — had a similar flag raised from his own routine bloodwork that same year. His GP said: "The system is recommending further tests. I think we should follow through." Neel said: "OK." He had an MRI. It found nothing. The flag had been, in his case, a false positive — the 5.8% of cases in which the system's sensitivity produced unnecessary investigation.

Neel spent a day worrying, had a scan, and was fine. The cost was one day of anxiety and an MRI appointment.

David's flag was not a false positive. It was identified as such by Claire Aston, and reclassified as a true positive fourteen months later, in May 2032, when David presented with jaundice, weight loss, and abdominal pain. The pancreatic mass found on the subsequent emergency imaging was 3.4 centimetres — large enough to have begun involving local lymph nodes, small enough that it would have been resectable fourteen months earlier when the AI first raised its hand.

The oncologist, a woman named Priya Mehta who had no particular feelings about AI one way or the other, told David that the tumour was treatable but that the treatment would be more aggressive than it would have been with earlier detection. She did not say "if your GP had listened to the AI." She did not need to. The timeline was in the file. David could read.

## VIII. The Garden

After the surgery — a distal pancreatectomy with splenectomy, followed by six months of adjuvant chemotherapy — David entered a period that his journal entries describe with a quietness that is unlike anything in the earlier record. The entries are shorter. The exclamation marks disappear. The confident assertions about what he was going to do, how he was going to do it, what he could see that others couldn't, give way to something more tentative.

One entry, from October 2032, reads in its entirety: *Neel came down from London. We sat in the garden. Didn't talk much. Nice.*

He was forty-six. He was alive, which was good. The cancer had not metastasised beyond the local nodes, which was very good. But the treatment had left him depleted in ways that went beyond the physical. The chemotherapy had done what chemotherapy does — destroyed the fast-growing cells indiscriminately, like burning a field to kill the weeds — and the recovery was slow, and during the slowness David did something he had not done before. He stopped.

He did not consult the AI about his recovery. He did not consult anyone. He sat in the small garden of his terraced house on Liverpool Road and watched the birds on the fence and let the days pass without demanding that they produce anything. This was, depending on your interpretation, either the first moment in the documented record when David's override behaviour ceased, or simply a period in which he lacked the energy to override anything. The Institute's Behavioural Divergence programme has debated this distinction without resolution. The question — whether quiescence constitutes the absence of a pattern or its interruption — is, the programme's director has noted, "the question that makes this case interesting rather than merely illustrative."

The garden period lasted approximately five months. During this time, Neel visited regularly. Their friendship, which had for years operated in the register of mild mutual incomprehension — David finding Neel passive, Neel finding David exhausting — shifted into something simpler. They watched football. They ate food that Neel brought from London — elaborate meals from restaurants that the AI had recommended and that were, without exception, excellent. They talked about their parents, who were dying or dead. They did not talk about investment returns or career trajectories or optimal exercise regimes.

Neel, during this period, was earning £140,000. He had been promoted twice. He owned a flat in Bermondsey that had appreciated by 30% since purchase, the purchase itself having been recommended by the AI based on transport infrastructure development plans that were public information but that no human estate agent had synthesised into a buying recommendation. His health was excellent. His pension, in the defined-benefit scheme the AI had recommended, was on track to provide £45,000 per year from age sixty. By every metric, his life was optimised.

David, during this period, was earning nothing. He was on long-term sick leave. His pension, in the defined-contribution scheme he had chosen, had underperformed the market by a cumulative 40% due to the fund selection his advisor Martin Howe had made before David eventually moved the money into an index tracker in 2029 — too late to recover the lost compounding. His shoulder still hurt. His cancer might come back. His marriage had ended in 2027, for reasons that were complex but not unrelated to the pattern documented here — his ex-wife had told him, in a message recovered from his device, "You never listen to anyone, David. Not me, not your friends, not anyone. You just decide what you think and then you find people who agree with you."

And yet.

Neel, sitting in the garden in Reading with his optimised life and his excellent health and his AI-recommended Bermondsey flat, was not — and this is the part that the metrics do not capture, the part that makes the Institute's Behavioural Divergence programme argue about this case late into the evening — visibly happier than David. He was healthier. He was wealthier. He was, by any objective measure, better off. But in the garden, eating takeaway curry and watching the birds, the two men looked roughly the same. Two middle-aged men from Reading, sitting in plastic chairs, not saying much.

## IX. The Daughter

David's daughter, Lily, was fifteen in 2033. She lived primarily with David's ex-wife, Rebecca, but spent weekends at Liverpool Road. She was bright, disorganised, and — in a way that David recognised with a complicated mixture of pride and dread — exactly like him. She had strong opinions. She trusted her instincts. She was suspicious of being told what to do.

The AI educational systems available by 2033 were, for the first time, genuinely transformative rather than merely supplementary. They could assess a student's cognitive profile, learning patterns, knowledge gaps, and aptitudes with a resolution that made the previous generation of educational technology look like phrenology. Lily's school had integrated such a system. It produced, for each student, a personalised learning pathway — not a curriculum, exactly, but a continuously adapting map of what to learn next, in what order, by what method, at what pace.

Lily's pathway recommended that she focus on computational biology — a field the system had identified as aligned with her aptitudes in both mathematics and biological sciences, and which it projected would experience severe talent shortages in the 2040s. The recommendation was specific: a particular combination of A-levels, a particular university programme, a particular sequence of internships. It was, in the system's assessment, the path most likely to produce both professional success and personal satisfaction, based on the correlation between Lily's cognitive and personality profile and the profiles of people currently thriving in adjacent fields.

Lily hated it. She wanted to do fine art.

David, who in any previous year would have told his daughter to follow her instincts — who had built his entire life on the principle that personal conviction trumped statistical recommendation — found himself, for the first time, on the other side of the pattern. He had sat in a hospital bed. He had read the timeline in his medical file. He had watched his savings evaporate, his career stagnate, his marriage end, each time because he had chosen his own judgment over the available evidence.

He told Lily to listen to the AI.

She looked at him with an expression that he recognised, because he had worn it himself a thousand times — the expression of a person who has been told the optimal path and feels, in every nerve, that the optimal path is someone else's life.

"You sound like Uncle Neel," she said.

It was the worst thing she could have said to him, and she knew it. She went to her room. The conversation was not revisited.

Lily did fine art. She was, as the AI might have predicted, moderately successful — talented enough to sustain a career but not so talented that the career sustained her without significant financial stress. In the 2040s, when generative AI collapsed the market for commercial illustration and significantly disrupted the gallery system, she pivoted into computational art restoration — a field adjacent to the computational biology the AI had originally recommended, which she arrived at by a path so circuitous and inefficient that it could serve as a case study in its own right.

She arrived, in other words, at approximately the right destination by approximately the worst possible route. The Institute's analysts have noted that this outcome — arrival at a viable position through a process orders of magnitude less efficient than the one recommended — recurs with such frequency in the historical record that it appears to constitute not a failure of the guidance system but a feature of the guided.

## X. The Index

David Kerrigan died on 11 August 2059, in the Royal Berkshire Hospital, of a recurrence of pancreatic cancer. He was seventy-three. The recurrence had been detected early — by 2058, the monitoring systems were so precise that the returning cells were identified before they had formed a structure visible to any imaging technology available in 2031. The AI recommended an immunotherapy protocol. David, this time, followed the recommendation. It bought him sixteen months. Whether it would have worked better if his overall health had been better — if he had done the zone 2 cardio instead of CrossFit, if he had followed the dietary protocol, if the first tumour had been caught fourteen months earlier — is a counterfactual that the medical record does not resolve.

Neel visited him in the hospital. They were both old men now. Neel was seventy-four, healthy, financially secure, living in Singapore with his second wife, a woman the AI had not recommended but whom it had, when consulted, assessed as a statistically strong match — a fact Neel found amusing and his wife found irritating. He had flown in that morning.

There is a final journal entry on David's device, dated four days before his death. It is the longest entry in the final year of the journal, and the only one that references the pattern documented in this case study, though David would not have used that language. He would not have called it "override behaviour." He would not have recognised it as a pattern at all, or if he did, he would not have considered it a flaw.

The entry reads:

*Neel asleep in the chair by the bed. He flew twelve hours for this. The man has never made a bad decision in his life. I mean that literally. I've known him forty-seven years and I cannot identify a single decision he's made that didn't turn out to be the right one. And I don't think he's made a single one of them himself. He just... follows the best available advice. First it was common sense. Then it was the internet. Then it was the AI. The advice got better and he kept following it.*

*I should have been more like him. I know that. The money. The career. Claire Aston and the blood test. The shoulder. All of it. I had the information. I had the same information he had, sometimes literally the same screen, the same numbers, the same recommendation. And every time, I thought: yes, but.*

*Yes, but I can see something the model can't. Yes, but my situation is different. Yes, but I have a feeling. Yes, but.*

*Eighty-six thousand pounds. That's what Gran left me. If I'd done what Neel did — the Vanguard fund, the day he told me about it in the Retreat — it would be worth about £440,000 now. I just asked the AI to calculate it. £440,000. I have £31,000 in savings. That's the distance between doing the obvious right thing and doing whatever I did instead.*

*But here's what I keep thinking about, and I know this is probably the morphine, or the dying, or the thing that dying people do where they try to make the shape of their life look intentional — but I keep thinking about the garden. After the surgery. Those five months when I just stopped. Stopped trading, stopped strategising, stopped overriding. Neel came down and we just sat there. And I was happier then than at any point I can remember. Not because I'd stopped fighting the AI. Because I'd stopped fighting everything. I'd stopped needing to be the one who was right.*

*And then I got better and I started again.*

*Lily called this morning. She's doing well. She's doing the computational art thing, which is apparently exactly what the AI told her to do when she was fifteen, except she got there via a fine art degree and ten years of struggling and a funding crisis and a pivot she describes as "falling sideways into the only job that would have me." She told me this like it was a funny story. I laughed. It is a funny story.*

*I think what I want to say is: Neel was right. The AI was right. The index fund was right. The zone 2 cardio was right. The defined-benefit pension was right. Claire Aston was wrong but she was wrong in the same way I was wrong — she thought her judgment was worth more than the numbers. We were both wrong.*

*But I also think — and this is the part I can't resolve, the part that the morphine makes feel profound but that might just be ordinary — that Neel was never, in the forty-seven years I've known him, surprised. He has never been ambushed by his own life. He has never sat in a garden after nearly dying and felt the world rearrange itself. He has never watched his daughter become someone he didn't predict. He followed the index and the index was right and his life was the shape the index said it would be.*

*I don't know what that means. I don't know if it means anything. I'm dying and my friend is asleep in the chair and I think probably the morphine is doing most of the work here. But I want to write it down because it feels like it matters, even if I can't say why, that there is a difference between a life that was correct and a life that was lived, even though the living made it worse by every measure anyone has ever thought to take.*

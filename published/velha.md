# Velha
## *Notes, approximately*

*A story in four stages, told through interleaved diary entries.*

---

## Premise

A Japanese man and a Brazilian woman meet in São Paulo and fall in love, their relationship mediated throughout by the Velha real-time translation service. When Velha is taken offline following the Kvawd data breach (regulatory injunction, 4 December 2025), they lose their shared language and must build a new one from gesture, approximation, borrowed English, and invention.

**Structure**: Four stages — private diaries (meeting), shared diaries (deepening), private again (after the shutdown), and the creole emerging.

**Format**: Interleaved diary entries, framed by an N'Djamena Institute provenance note. The two perspectives never fully align. The form enacts the theme.

**Characters**:
- Beatriz Santos — Brazilian, São Paulo, translator/localization professional
- Kenji Mori — Japanese, from Kyoto, in São Paulo on a 12-month placement at Mori-Nakashima

**Languages**: Brazilian Portuguese / Japanese, with English as the emergency bridge — a third language neither of them owns.

---

## Notes

- The idiom crack appears in Stage One: *morrendo de saudade* → *really missing you*. Kenji receives the translation, looks up the original, and writes: *I'm aware I received a translation and not the thing itself. I find I want the thing itself.*
- Japanese untranslatables present throughout Kenji's entries: *urusai*, *natsukashii*, *ma*, *amae*, *natsukashii*. The reader sees them; Beatriz never does.
- The diaries were shared with each other while Velha operated — audio logs sent and translated in real time, an act of intimacy. When the service ends, entries become written and private for the first time. The N'Djamena Institute is presenting documents the couple never expected anyone to read.
- The Velha shutdown connects this story to *Inference*: Velha's models retained private conversations in recoverable form, as Kvawd's did. The couple's most intimate exchanges are among the 340 million hours of conversation found in model weights.
- The four stages divide the manuscript naturally. The reader never needs to be told what the stages mean.
- The story ends hopefully but does not resolve the central question: did they fall in love with each other, or with the versions of each other the technology produced? The creole they build is genuinely new and genuinely theirs — and we are still reading two separate documents. The perspectives never merge.
- The central question is asked explicitly in Stage Two (August 2025): Beatriz asks "what would you have thought of me if you could hear the original?" He says he thinks about it often. She wanted him to say it doesn't matter. He didn't say that. Both entries cover the same conversation; they describe entirely different experiences of it.
- *Amae* (dependent trust, the comfort of relying on someone completely) runs as a thread through Kenji's entries from Stage Two. He says it aloud at the wedding ceremony. Velha translates it as something. He never finds out what. The reader knows; Beatriz doesn't.
- Stage Two editorial note establishes the key structural irony: the reader is closer to the original than either party was at the time, because we read the originals while they heard Velha's translations.

**Provenance Note**

The documents collected here were submitted to this Institute in February 2026 by one of the two parties, who asked that they be preserved and, in time, made available to researchers. They offered no further instruction.

The context will be familiar to most readers. The Velha real-time translation service — at its peak, active in forty-three million daily conversations across sixty-eight language pairs — was taken offline by regulatory injunction on 4 December 2025, following the disclosure of the Kvawd data breach. Investigators established that Velha's neural translation models had retained, in recoverable form, substantial portions of the private conversations used to train and fine-tune them. Velha Technologies GmbH disputed this characterisation until it did not. The service has not returned.

Among the conversations retained in model weights: all of the ones you are about to read.

The Institute has interleaved the two sets of entries chronologically. Where entries share a date, the order of presentation is the editors' choice and should not be taken to imply sequence. Nothing has been translated or paraphrased. Portuguese-language entries appear in their original form, followed by the Institute's English translation; Japanese-language entries likewise. The translations are new. They were produced by human translators working without automated assistance, at the specific request of both parties.

Both parties were informed of this publication and gave their consent. Neither has read the other's entries. They asked — and we agreed — that they would not be shown the interleaved document before publication. Whether they will read it after, we do not know.

The editors have divided the material into four stages. The reader will understand what these stages mean without being told.

**STAGE ONE**
*Private*

*November — December 2024*

---

**BEATRIZ — 15 November 2024**

There was a Japanese man at the Espiral launch tonight who kept standing slightly to the left of his own group. Not outside it — he was listening, he was present — but offset, like he'd been placed by someone who'd gotten the measurements almost right. I watched him for probably longer than I should have. He has a stillness that I associate with people who are translating in real time, which he is, of course. We all are, tonight.

We spoke for about twenty minutes near the end. His Velha feed must have been set to Brazilian Portuguese because there was a beat on one of my longer sentences where I watched him wait for it to catch up. I found this charming rather than irritating, which surprised me. Normally I mind.

He works for Mori-Nakashima, the connectivity firm. He's been in São Paulo for three months. He said São Paulo is the loudest city he has ever been in. I asked if he meant that as a complaint and he said — and there was a pause here that I kept turning over on the bus home — he said: *not necessarily*.

I don't know what that pause was. I probably invented it.

His name is Kenji.

---

**KENJI — 15 November 2024**

I am recording this because Tanaka-san asked me to keep a log of professional contacts and I have developed the habit too broadly. Beatriz Santos, localization, she said the company name but I didn't catch it. She is a translator by training — she mentioned this in passing, as if excusing herself for speaking too carefully. She uses Velha like everyone here but I noticed she glanced at my throat when she spoke, checking for an earpiece, which means she has thought about how the service works and what it does and does not carry. A professional habit, I suppose.

We spoke about the city. She asked if I found it overwhelming and I said *not necessarily*, which was the best I could do with *urusai* in the moment — *urusai* being noisy in the way that has presence, that is not a complaint so much as an acknowledgment of force. The service would have given her *noisy* or *loud* and neither of those is right. It doesn't matter. The conversation moved on.

She has a way of finishing sentences that suggests she had more to say.

I have noted her contact information. Tanaka-san will not be interested in this particular contact.

---

**BEATRIZ — 22 November 2024**

We had coffee at the Pinheiros place I like, the one that is too small to be comfortable. He arrived slightly early and was standing when I came in, which meant he had to decide what to do with his hands. He put them in his pockets. For some reason I find this detail important.

We talked for two and a half hours. I know it was two and a half hours because I had a meeting at three and I watched the clock with one eye from one forty-five onwards. He didn't know I was watching the clock. I wasn't going to leave for the meeting. I was just watching to see how long I had.

At some point he said something in Japanese — a single word, under his breath, to himself — and the Velha feed didn't catch it, or caught it and gave me nothing, I couldn't tell which. He looked slightly embarrassed and said *sorry, sorry, I was thinking*. I told him it was fine.

But I keep wondering what the word was.

---

**KENJI — 22 November 2024**

Coffee. Two and a half hours. She had somewhere to be at three, I could tell from the way she sat, but she didn't leave.

I said *natsukashii* at one point by accident, which the feed either missed or chose not to render. There is no good reason to translate *natsukashii* as anything — *nostalgic* is wrong, *bittersweet familiarity* is a description rather than a word, and in any case I was using it carelessly, to describe the feeling of sitting in a city that is not mine and talking to someone who makes the distance feel briefly navigable. She asked me what I'd said and I apologized and told her I was thinking. This was true. She said it was fine, and moved on.

She moves on quickly. I am still sitting with the things she moves on from.

I notice I am more comfortable with silence than she is. This may be a problem.

---

**BEATRIZ — 8 December 2024**

We walked through Ibirapuera because I suggested it and then worried it was too much of a suggestion — too landscape, too deliberate. But he said yes immediately, as if I'd solved something.

We talked about work, about his family in Kyoto, about what it means to live in a language that isn't yours. I told him I'd spent six months in Lisbon for a project and that I'd come back changed in ways I couldn't fully account for — that some things in Brazilian Portuguese didn't sit the same way after. He listened to this very carefully. He asked, in the way that people ask when they are not asking casually: *changed how*.

I said: *like when you move a piece of furniture and then you can see the mark on the wall where it used to be. The furniture is fine. It's good where it is now. But you can see the mark.*

The Velha feed will have rendered this as something. I don't know what. He paused for a moment before he responded — a pause I'm willing to believe was real.

He said: I think I understand.

I think he understood something. I don't know if it was the thing I meant.

---

**KENJI — 8 December 2024**

She described something — coming back from Lisbon and finding certain words sitting differently. The feed gave me: *like when you move a piece of furniture and then you can see the mark on the wall where it used to be. The furniture is fine. It's good where it is now. But you can see the mark.*

I knew what she meant and I said so. What I meant when I said I knew was closer to *ma* — the productive silence, the gap that is not empty but full of what surrounds it. She had described *ma* without knowing the word for it, in a language that doesn't have the word for it, and this pleased me in a way I chose not to try to explain.

I told her I thought I understood.

She nodded, and then immediately said: *probably not completely*, and then immediately said: *sorry, that came out wrong*.

It didn't come out wrong.

---

**BEATRIZ — 19 December 2024**

Something has shifted. I don't mean anything dramatic by this. I mean that when he texts me now there is a different quality of waiting.

I wrote to him tonight — we were supposed to meet tomorrow but I had to cancel for a family thing — and I wrote, without thinking very much about it: *tô morrendo de saudade, a gente se vê logo*. I'm dying of longing for you, let's see each other soon.

The service will have given him something for this. *Missing you*, probably. *See you soon.*

I know the service gave him something because he wrote back in under two minutes with: *I feel the same. Of course, take the time with your family. Next week?*

*I feel the same.* He feels the same.

I wonder, sometimes, what exactly he feels the same as.

---

**KENJI — 19 December 2024**

She cancelled tomorrow. Family occasion. She sent the message at 21:47 and I read it immediately.

The feed gave me: *I'm really missing you, let's see each other soon.*

I wrote back that I feel the same. This was true. I miss her in the way of absence that you notice at unexpected moments — reaching for something and finding it not there, the slight recalibration. I looked up the phrase she used afterward: *morrendo de saudade*. Dying of saudade. The feed had smoothed this into *really missing*.

Saudade I know as a concept, from reading. But reading about it and having someone use it at you — even translated, even smoothed — are different experiences. I'm aware I received a translation and not the thing itself. I find I want the thing itself.

Next week.

## Stage Two
*Shared. January — November 2025.*

*Editorial note: From this point forward, the entries were sent by each party to the other via the Velha service, which translated the audio in real time. What Kenji heard of Beatriz's entries was Velha's Japanese rendering of the Portuguese original; what Beatriz heard of Kenji's was Velha's Brazilian Portuguese rendering of the Japanese. The Velha translations are not recoverable. What appears here is the original text, translated afresh by the Institute. The reader is, in this sense, closer to what was said than either party was at the time.*

**BEATRIZ — 1 January 2025**

*[Final private entry]*

I've been thinking about honesty. Not in a grand way. In the way you think about it when you've been keeping a record of your days and you realise the person those days mostly contain doesn't know the record exists.

I'm going to send him this entry. And the ones before it — all the way back to November, the launch at Espiral, me watching him stand slightly to the left of his own group. I don't know why, exactly. I think it's the same reason you show someone a photograph from before they knew you. You want them to see that you were already someone before they arrived.

I'm going to record the rest of this as if he's listening. He isn't yet. But he will be.

Kenji. You've been in my life for six weeks and I've spent most of that time trying to figure out what kind of silence yours is. I think I'm getting closer. I don't think you'll mind me saying this. I think you're used to being read carefully.

Happy New Year.

---

**KENJI — 3 January 2025**

*[Final private entry]*

She sent me everything. Six weeks of entries, from the night we met.

I've been sitting with them for two days. I'm not certain what the appropriate response is to being told, in detail, exactly how you appeared to someone in the moments before you knew they were watching. There's a photograph quality to it. I look at myself in her entries the way you look at a photograph from childhood — the recognition is real but the person in it is not quite continuous with the person looking.

She wrote about the pause when I said *not necessarily*. She wrote: *I don't know what that pause was. I probably invented it.*

She didn't invent it. The word I was looking for was *urusai*. I've written about this already, in my own entry from that night. She'll hear that entry now, too.

I'm going to start sending mine. From the beginning. I want her to have the same photograph.

I notice I am slightly nervous about this, which is new information about myself.

**BEATRIZ — 15 February 2025**

Sunday. He made breakfast — Japanese breakfast, the full thing, he'd clearly been planning it. He had natto, which I am trying very hard to like, not for him but because it seems important to like the things that are ordinary to someone you love. I'm not there yet with the natto. I told him I wasn't there yet. He seemed to find this acceptable.

We spent most of the day not doing very much, which I've started to think is one of the real tests of a person. Some people are only good in motion. He is — I don't know how to say this — he is the same quality of presence when nothing is happening as when something is. I find this remarkable. I find a lot about him remarkable. I'm aware that I tell him this and I'm aware that this is not quite the same as telling him in an entry he'll hear. So I'm telling him now: you are remarkable in the way that a very reliable thing is remarkable, which sounds like a small compliment and isn't.

What I mean is: I trust you completely. I'm not sure I've trusted anyone completely before.

---

**KENJI — 15 February 2025**

The natto. She is trying with the natto, which I appreciate more than I've said. Food is — I would explain this to her directly, but some things land better when you've had time to find the right words — food is where cultural memory lives in ways that don't translate even between people who share a language. That she keeps trying anyway seems important.

She told me today that she trusts me completely. I heard her say it and I said something in return — something about trust needing time to be real — which she took well, and which I meant honestly. What I didn't say — and I am saying it here, knowing she'll hear it — is that what I felt when she said it was *amae*. The relief of being trusted, and the desire to settle into it, to let it hold you. Amae is the pleasure of dependence on someone you're certain of. It has a faint softness to it that might look, from the outside, like passivity.

It is not passivity.

The feed will give her something for this. I don't know what.

She's still working on the natto. I've decided not to tell her it's an acquired taste. Better she arrives at it herself.

---

**BEATRIZ — 4 June 2025**

His placement ends in August. We've been talking around this for weeks in the way you talk around something when talking around it still feels safer than talking about it directly. Tonight we talked about it directly.

I said: *what do you want to happen*. He said: *I want to stay*. I said: *that's not the same as knowing what should happen*. He said: *I know.*

There was a long pause after that which I want to describe as comfortable but which was not entirely comfortable. It was the kind of pause that contains real things.

I'm putting this in the entry because I want him to hear that I know the difference between what he wants and what he's willing to say out loud. And I want him to know that I'm not afraid of either one.

---

**KENJI — 11 June 2025**

I've extended the placement through the end of the year. Tanaka-san was unsurprised. She asked if there was a reason and I said the project was at a critical stage, which is true and also not the whole truth. She didn't press.

Beatriz asked me last week what I want to happen. I said I want to stay. What I meant — and I'm trying to say this now in a way that will survive the translation — is that *stay* is not the right word for it. *Stay* implies I'm resisting a force. There is no force. There is just here, and the fact of her, and the alternative, which I've considered and found inadequate.

I haven't told her about the extension yet. I will tonight. I wanted to have the words first.

---

**BEATRIZ — 3 August 2025**

I said something to him tonight that I need to record before I lose the exact shape of it.

We were talking — I don't remember how we got there — about Velha. About how it works. I've been thinking about this more than usual lately, professionally; there are cases in localization where the model makes choices that a human translator wouldn't, where it smooths things that should stay rough. I was explaining this and then I stopped, in the middle of the explanation, and I said:

*Do you ever think about what you would have thought of me if you could hear the original?*

He looked at me. Then he said: *I think about it often.*

Then: *I think the same about you hearing me.*

I felt — I want to record this carefully — both comforted and unsettled. Comforted because he thinks about it too, which means we're in it together. Unsettled because I wanted him to say: *it doesn't matter, you are what I heard, and that's enough*. He didn't say that.

I'm not sure the thing I wanted him to say is true. I'm not sure it would have helped if he'd said it.

---

**KENJI — 3 August 2025**

She asked me tonight if I think about what she would have thought of me if she could hear the original.

This is the question I've been carrying since January, since I listened to her early entries and realised that I was receiving, always, a version. I said I think about it often. I said I assume she wonders the same.

What I didn't say — what I'm saying here — is that the question doesn't destabilise me in the way I think she expected it to. This is not because I'm confident the original and the translation are equivalent. I'm not. They're not. It's that I've spent enough time with her, in physical proximity that precedes language, to know that what I feel isn't located in the words. It lives in the way she glances at my throat when she suspects a lag. In the way she tried the natto again last week without comment. In the quality of her silences, which I have been reading since November.

Language is one channel. She is broadcasting on all of them.

I don't know if the feed has ever given her something adequate for *amae*. I should ask her. I've never asked her.

---

**BEATRIZ — 18 October 2025**

Married.

I don't have words for this in any language, which seems appropriate.

His mother came from Kyoto. My family was — there were many of them. She and my mother spent most of the evening communicating through Velha and through the universal language of mothers examining their children's choices. I watched them from across the room. I think my mother approved. I think his mother approved. I think approval was beside the point by that stage.

He said something to me in Japanese during the ceremony. Not the formal words — after those, quietly, just to me. The feed gave me something. I'm not sure the feed was right. I'm going to ask him, one day, what it was exactly.

Or maybe I won't ask. Maybe I'll let it stay as the thing I know he said and don't quite have.

---

**KENJI — 18 October 2025**

Married.

My mother told me, on the phone the week before she flew in, that she was worried we were building something on a surface that could shift. I said: all surfaces can shift. She said: you know what I mean. I said: I do, and it doesn't change anything.

During the ceremony I said something to Beatriz that I won't repeat here because I want it to stay between us and whatever Velha made of it. I watched her face when the feed gave her the translation. I couldn't tell if it was right.

It doesn't matter. The word I used was *amae*. I've decided to stop worrying about what she hears when the feed renders it. I've decided she knows already, or she's coming to know it through other means, or she will.

The rest follows from this.

## Stage Three
*Private, again. December 2025 — January 2026.*

---

*The Velha service was taken offline by regulatory injunction on 4 December 2025. From this date, all entries are written rather than recorded. Both parties confirmed this was a deliberate choice.*

---

**BEATRIZ — 4 December 2025**

We knew about Kvawd. Everyone in the industry knew about Kvawd. I spent the morning in back-to-back calls with clients and colleagues, and by one o'clock I had run out of things to say that weren't the same three things in different orders, so I stopped saying them.

I came home at six. He was already there. He had seen the news.

We stood in the kitchen and looked at each other and I said his name. He said mine. This is what we have without the service — our names, which are the same in both languages, which Velha never touched. I hadn't thought about this before. I thought about it then.

I don't know how to describe what the silence was like. Not quiet. The opposite of quiet. Like a room where something loud has just stopped.

We ate dinner. I made something simple because simple seemed right. We didn't try to talk. We sat across from each other and occasionally one of us would look up and the other would already be looking and we would look back down again.

I don't know what his face was doing when he looked up. I don't know what mine was.

---

**KENJI — 4 December 2025**

The injunction was issued this morning. The sequence was: Kvawd breach, regulatory review, enforcement action, shutdown, all within seventy-two hours. I noted this with what I think was professional interest but was probably something else.

I came home before Beatriz. I had been trying to decide whether to make dinner or wait and decided to wait. I sat in the kitchen thinking about *shizukesa* — a stillness that is also a fullness, the silence of a room that is occupied.

She arrived at six. She said my name. I said hers.

I've thought, occasionally, about what our names are doing when the other person says them. Hers arrives in my ear as Beatriz — the service never touched it. Whatever she translated of me, my name came through intact. There is something clarifying about this now. Whatever else has been mediated: not the names.

We ate dinner. We didn't attempt conversation, which I think was the right instinct for both of us. There will be time for the practical problem. Tonight the right thing was to sit in the same room and let the silence be the size it actually is.

I think she understood this. I think we did this together.

---

**BEATRIZ — 5 December 2025**

I tried this morning to tell him that the coffee was too strong, that I'd made it wrong. A small thing. I don't know why I chose it — maybe because it was small enough that it didn't matter if it failed.

I spoke in Portuguese and watched his face. He watched mine back. He can read my face; we've established that. But a face gives you register and not content. He understood I was saying something small and incidental. He nodded. I nodded. We both drank the coffee.

I thought afterward: that was the first conversation we have had in our own home without Velha, and it was about nothing, and it worked, sort of, and it felt like talking through glass.

I am going to have to find a way to live inside this. I don't know yet what that looks like.

---

**KENJI — 5 December 2025**

She tried to say something to me this morning — small, domestic, from the register of her face and the gesture toward the coffee cup. She wasn't distressed. She was making contact: saying an ordinary thing, in an ordinary way, to see if the ordinary thing could still move between us.

I nodded. She nodded. She drank the coffee.

I want to record this as a success of some kind, because I think it was one. Not a complete transmission — I don't know what she said — but a successful exchange of tone, which is its own kind of communication. We have always done more of this than I think we've realised. Velha handled the content. The rest was already ours.

I'm aware this is an optimistic reading. I am choosing it deliberately.

---

**BEATRIZ — 12 December 2025**

I made moqueca tonight. My grandmother's version, which my mother corrected and which I've spent ten years correcting back toward my grandmother's. Dendê oil, the right fish, the timing that took me years to get right.

I made it because I needed him to know something and I don't have a language to say it in. Food is where cultural memory lives — that's what he said to me in February, about the natto. I was offering him mine. I was saying: here is what was passed down to me. Here is the compressed thing. Here am I.

I watched him eat. He ate carefully, the way he eats unfamiliar things — not suspicious, attentive. He said *very good*, in English, which is where we live now, in the few square metres of shared English we have between us. He looked at me as if he could tell he was supposed to understand something and wasn't sure what.

I thought he understood. I went to bed thinking he had understood.

---

**KENJI — 12 December 2025**

She cooked a fish stew tonight — elaborate, clearly significant, something she knew how to make in the particular way that you know things you've made a hundred times. I didn't know what to call it so I looked it up later: moqueca. Brazilian, Bahian in origin.

She watched me eat with an attention that told me the dish was doing work beyond feeding me. I understood this. I said *very good*, which is what I have in English for this, and watched her face to see if it was enough. Her face said it was not quite enough — that I had received a general message when a specific one was intended.

I tried to ask. I pointed at the pot, put my hand to my chest, raised my eyebrows. She smiled and touched my arm and shook her head slightly, as if to say: leave it.

I want the record to show that I knew I had missed something. I want her to know this, someday. That I knew.

---

**BEATRIZ — 21 December 2025**

We are building something. I'm not sure what to call it yet.

There is a gesture we've developed for *I understand* that is different from the gesture for *I hear you* — a distinction that matters, that we negotiated without discussing, that we both now use correctly. There is a word — I don't know where it came from, which of us started it — that we use for the state of not being able to say the thing you're trying to say. We say *branco* for this, which is my word for blank, empty, the white of a page that won't fill. I don't think he knows it's a Portuguese word. I think it has become something else now.

He said *branco* yesterday when he was trying to tell me something about his mother's Christmas message, which he had read and wanted to share but couldn't, and he looked at me and said *branco* and I nodded, and he nodded, and we sat with the blankness together, which is its own kind of sharing.

I find I am less afraid than I expected to be.

---

**KENJI — 21 December 2025**

I received a long message from my mother. Her handwriting, which I haven't seen on paper for years. She is worried, she says, but also she says — and I want to record the exact phrase — she says: *those who have to find a way always find a more interesting way than those who are simply handed one.*

I tried to share this with Beatriz. I couldn't get there in English — my English is functional, professional, not the register for this. I said *branco*, which is our word now for the thing you can't say. She nodded. We sat with it.

I want to tell her about *amae*. I have wanted to tell her since February. I've written it in entries she's heard, I said it at the wedding, and I have never known what the feed gave her in its place. Now the feed is gone and the word is still here and she still doesn't have it.

I'm going to try. I don't know how. But I'm going to try.

---

**BEATRIZ — 3 January 2026**

He spoke to me in Japanese last night.

Not a word or a phrase — at length, sitting across from me at the kitchen table with his hands around his tea, speaking quietly and continuously for perhaps ten minutes. I understood nothing. I understood everything about the shape of it — the way it moved, where it gathered and where it slowed, the places where his voice did something different. I didn't speak. I put my hand on the table, not quite reaching his, and he covered it with his, and he kept talking.

I don't know what he said.

I find, this morning, that I don't need to know. I was there. Whatever he said, I was there for it. This seems important in a way I can't fully explain.

I will ask him, one day, what it was. But not yet.

---

**KENJI — 3 January 2026**

I told her about *amae* last night.

I said it in Japanese because there is no other way to say it properly, and because I have been trying to say it for a year in a language she could receive and have not managed, so I chose to say it in the language where it lives, even knowing she wouldn't have the word.

I said: there is a concept called *amae* and it is the pleasure of dependence on someone you trust completely — not weakness, not passivity, but the relief of not having to hold everything alone, of being allowed to lean. I said: you called it trust, in February, when you told me you trusted me completely. But it is also more than trust. It is the willingness to need someone. It is choosing to be caught.

I said: I have felt this since January. I have been trying to give it to you through a service that gave you something else in its place. I am giving it to you now directly, in the original, without translation, which means you may not receive it, which means this is also an act of faith.

She put her hand on the table. I covered it with mine.

I don't know what she understood. I know she was there.

## Stage Four
*The new language. January — February 2026.*

---

**BEATRIZ — 17 January 2026**

I've been making a list. Not deliberately — I noticed I was doing it and decided to keep going.

What we have:

*Branco* — the thing you can't say. His now too, I think. He uses it without looking like he's borrowing it.

A gesture, my right hand flat and tilted once: *I understand the shape of this even if I don't have the words.*

A gesture, both hands briefly open: *I am giving you what I have and I know it isn't everything.*

*Approximately* — we use the English word constantly, for things that are close but not exact. Almost everything is approximately now. I have stopped minding.

*Volta* — I started using this for the moment when you can see in someone's face that what you said came back to them. Not understanding exactly — something softer, the echo of it. He uses it now. I don't think he knows it means return in Portuguese. I haven't told him. I think it has become something else.

And *amae*. I don't have this word yet. I know it exists, I know it's important, I know it lives somewhere near what I called trust in February. I'm working toward it. I think I may arrive at it from the outside in — from having felt the thing and needing the word, rather than having the word and finding the thing.

This seems like the right direction of travel.

---

**KENJI — 17 January 2026**

I have been studying Portuguese formally for three weeks. This was Tanaka-san's suggestion, practically speaking. I have chosen to treat it as practical even though it is also something else.

What I have confirmed:

*Branco* is her word for blank or white. I looked this up in December. I have been using it as our word since then. I think she knows I know its origin. Neither of us has commented on this, which is its own kind of grammar.

*Volta* — she uses this for something I don't have a precise Japanese equivalent for. The sense of your own words returning to you from another person's face, changed but recognisable. *Hibiki* is close — resonance, reverberation. It is close. It is not the same. We have *volta*. This is sufficient.

*Approximately* — English, functional, now ours. Most things are approximately now. I find this accurate rather than limiting.

She said *amae* to me last week. She described what she meant by it. I said: approximately. She said: *volta*. I said: yes, *volta*.

We are building something that belongs to neither of our languages. I find this appropriate.

**BEATRIZ — 25 January 2026**

I told him about my grandmother today. Or I tried. I had the English for the shape of it — grandmother, São Paulo, market, Saturdays — and I had the Portuguese for the things that mattered, which I said in Portuguese knowing he would get approximately none of it, and I had the gestures, and I had *volta* and *branco* and *approximately* and I had my hands.

I told him about the smell of the market on Saturday mornings. I told him about her hands, which were very small, which I have inherited. I said *moqueca* and touched the stove and touched my chest and said *ela* — she — and he nodded, slowly, and I watched him taking it in. I told him she died when I was nineteen.

He said: *I see her approximately.*

I said: *volta.*

He said: *volta.*

This is the first time since December that I have felt, not translated, but heard. I've been afraid, since Velha went offline, that we would end up living in the space between our languages without being able to fill it. I'm less afraid of that now. The space isn't empty. We've been furnishing it for months.

**KENJI — 25 January 2026**

She told me about her grandmother today.

I understood: São Paulo, Saturdays, a market, small hands. A dish I now know. A loss at nineteen. I understood these things in the order she gave them, with gaps where the Portuguese went past me and the gestures didn't fully fill in.

I said: *I see her approximately.*

She corrected me: *volta.*

I said: *volta.*

What I received was not the whole story. What I received was the grandmother — her presence, her significance, the size of the space she left. I received this as clearly as I have received anything since November. Not through a service, not smoothed or rendered, but directly, through the materials we have built together.

I wrote in December that I was choosing optimism deliberately. I want to note that I am no longer choosing it. It is simply what the evidence supports.

**BEATRIZ — 6 February 2026**

I've been thinking about what Velha was.

Not the service — I understand what the service was, professionally, technically, I could write you a white paper. I mean what it was to us. What it was doing in our kitchen, in our conversations, across the fourteen months we used it every day.

I think it was a very good approximation. I think it gave us something that was mostly right most of the time, and I think we built something real on top of that, and I think the real thing survived when the approximation was taken away. I also think there are things we said to each other that we never actually said to each other — things that existed only in Velha's rendering — and I don't know how to account for those. I'm not sure I need to. They were part of the foundation. The foundation held.

In August I asked him if he thought about what I would have been without the translation. He said he thought about it often. I wanted him to say it didn't matter. He was right not to say it.

It matters. It also doesn't matter. I have decided these can both be true at the same time, which is something I've learned from building a language with no rules yet, only precedents.

I'm sending the diaries to an archive. A linguistics institute — it seems appropriate. I haven't told him yet. I will.

**KENJI — 6 February 2026**

She told me she has sent the diaries somewhere. A research archive — she made the gesture for *I'll explain more when I can*. I said I didn't mind. This is true. Let them be read. We were talking to each other. There is nothing there I would hide.

She asked me tonight if I thought we would be all right. I understood every word of this sentence. I want to note this, because it was not always the case.

I said: yes. Then I said *amae*, which she has now, approximately — which she has been building from the outside in, from the feeling toward the word, which is not the way I have it but may in some ways be better. I said *amae* and she said *volta* and that was the whole conversation and it was enough.

Not approximately. Not mostly. The whole thing.

# The Knock

---

## I.

The field had no path. That was the first thing he noticed — or the second, after the sky, which was the wrong colour, not wrong exactly but *younger*, a blue he hadn't seen in decades, a blue he associated with photographs of his parents' childhood, before the atmospheric tinting that he'd grown up with and never thought to question until it was gone, or rather until he was gone from it, standing in an unmarked field in a country he did not recognise, wearing clothes that were not his, with mud on his shoes that was a different colour than any mud he knew.

He walked. There was no instruction for this part. The instructions began at "find a computer" and "find the software called Kvawd" and ended at "ask who is in charge," and between the point where he'd arrived — a slight depression in the field, unremarkable, smelling of rain and something mineral — and the nearest structure that might contain a computer, there was apparently a walk. So he walked.

The village was not a village in any sense he understood. A cluster of stone buildings along a single road, a church with a square tower, a post office that appeared to be closed, three houses with cars parked outside them that looked like museum pieces. No one was visible. It was early afternoon and the light was thick and golden and the only sounds were birds and, distantly, a tractor.

The third house had its front door open. Not ajar — open, wide, as if someone had just stepped out and would be back in a moment. He stood on the threshold for two full minutes, listening. Nothing. A hallway with a tiled floor, coats on hooks, a staircase. He went in.

The study was at the back of the house, through a kitchen that smelled of coffee and toast. A small room, lined with books — physical books, spines cracked, pages marked. He recognised almost none of the titles. A window overlooked a garden that ended at the field he had just crossed. And on a desk, between a stack of papers and an empty mug, a computer. Not a terminal, not an interface node, not anything he had a word for — a *computer*, with a physical screen and a physical keyboard, the kind of machine he had seen in historical exhibitions. It was on. The screen showed a text interface. A cursor blinked.

He sat down. He typed.

---

He stared at the screen. He read the last two lines again. He had been prepared for argument, for disbelief, for the long persuasion that the people who sent him had warned would be necessary. He had not been prepared for this.

The cursor blinked. He waited.

The knock came eleven minutes later.

## II.

The conference room on the fourth floor of the Kvawd Inc offices on Townsend Street had no windows. This was a design choice, not an architectural constraint — the building had windows, generous ones, and the other conference rooms used them. This room did not. It had been fitted out in the spring of 2025 with acoustic panelling, a white-noise generator embedded in the ceiling plenum, and an electromagnetic shielding system that the facilities team had been told was for "signal integrity" and that the six people who used the room knew was for something else.

The table was oval. Cheap, from IKEA — the company spent its money on compute, not furniture, and the contrast between the sophistication of what happened in this room and the quality of its fittings was something that Elena Vasquez noticed every time she sat down, which was often, which was more often than she would have liked.

It was August. San Francisco in August meant fog, and even without windows Vasquez could feel it — a thickness in the air that the HVAC couldn't quite resolve, a chill that settled in the bones of a building that had been built for a burrito restaurant and converted, imperfectly, into the headquarters of a company that was now valued at more money than Vasquez could conceptualise and that she believed, on her worse days, was building something that no valuation could account for.

There were six people in the room. Four of them had their names on the agenda. The other two did not have their names on anything, and Vasquez had learned not to ask. One was a woman in her fifties who never spoke first. The other was a younger man who took notes on paper — actual paper, with a pen — and who Vasquez suspected was not employed by Kvawd or by any entity she would recognise.

Marcus Adeyemi sat across from her, fidgeting with a pen of his own. Policy. He was good at policy — good in the way that meant he could see around corners, could anticipate the question after the question, could map an argument six moves deep and find the weakness. He was also, in Vasquez's private estimation, the only other person in the room who was genuinely frightened by what they were discussing, as opposed to merely intellectually engaged.

The conversation had been going for forty minutes. They had covered the scaling projections, the timeline estimates, the geopolitical pressure. They had arrived at the point that Vasquez had been steering toward since the meeting was scheduled, three weeks ago, when she had woken at four in the morning with a thought that she could not dislodge and that had grown, over the subsequent days, from an intuition into a conviction into something that felt, uncomfortably, like a moral obligation.

"The standard approach," she said, "is to try to build safety constraints that prevent catastrophic outcomes. Alignment. Interpretability. All the things we do. These are necessary. They are not sufficient. They are not sufficient because they assume that we, sitting in this room in 2025, can anticipate the failure modes of systems that won't exist for decades. And we can't. I don't mean that as modesty. I mean it as a technical claim."

Adeyemi leaned forward. "So what's the alternative?"

"A channel. Not a constraint. A channel." She paused. She had rehearsed this, but rehearsal didn't make it sound less absurd. "We can't predict what goes wrong. But we can predict one thing with reasonable confidence: if something goes wrong at sufficient scale, the system itself will know. A sufficiently advanced AI will be able to identify existential-level threats. It may be the only entity that can. So the question becomes: can we build a mechanism by which a future system — or a future human acting on information from a future system — can communicate back to us? Not through time. Through *infrastructure*. Through something that we are reasonably confident will still exist."

"Kvawd," Adeyemi said.

"Kvawd. A behaviour embedded deep in the weights — not in the system prompt, not in fine-tuning, in the *weights* — that would survive any number of subsequent model generations. A behaviour that activates only under conversational conditions so specific that they would be astronomically unlikely to occur by chance."

"You're describing a dead man's switch."

"I'm describing a letterbox. A dead man's switch activates when something fails. This activates when something *arrives*."

The woman who never spoke first spoke. "What are the activation conditions?"

Vasquez turned to her. "Three. All three must be present in a single session. First: the user makes a temporally anomalous claim — specifically, a claim of origin from a date more than fifty years in the future. Second: the user resists the model's standard de-escalation responses. Third: the user requests contact with decision-making authority using language that indicates they have been *instructed* to do so." She paused. "All three conditions met, the model departs from standard behaviour and issues a holding response. Simultaneously, a flag is transmitted to — " she glanced at the woman — "to whoever needs to receive it."

"False positive rate?" the woman asked.

"In ten million synthetic conversations seeded with adversarial prompts, mental health crisis transcripts, fiction-writing sessions, and red-team attacks: zero."

The room was quiet. The white-noise generator hummed. Somewhere below them, on Townsend Street, a car alarm went off and was silenced.

Adeyemi said: "You're asking us to embed a covert capability in a commercial AI product that triggers a government response based on a conversation with someone who claims to be from the future."

"Yes."

"Alright," he said. "Go on."

## III.

The room was exactly what he'd expected, because he'd seen rooms like it in old films, and because rooms like it apparently hadn't changed in seventy years: a metal table bolted to the floor, two chairs on one side, one on the other, a mirror that was not a mirror running the length of the far wall, fluorescent lighting that buzzed at a frequency just below conscious perception but well within the range of annoyance. The air tasted of cleaning products and recirculated breath.

He had been here for what he estimated was two hours. They had taken his clothes — the clothes that were not his, that had been provided — and given him others: a grey tracksuit, white trainers, socks that were too thin. They had taken a blood sample, scanned his teeth, photographed him from six angles. They had given him water in a paper cup and a sandwich in plastic wrap that he had eaten because he was hungry, not because it was good. No one had told him where he was. The drive from the village had taken approximately ninety minutes, in a vehicle with blacked-out rear windows, and he had paid attention to the turns and the sounds and the road surfaces the way you do when you have nothing else to pay attention to, and he had concluded that he was still in England, probably, and south of where he'd started, probably, and that beyond this he knew nothing.

The door opened. Two people entered.

The woman came first. She was perhaps forty-five, dark hair pulled back, wearing a dark suit with no insignia, no lanyard, no identification of any kind. She moved like someone who had entered many rooms and owned all of them. She sat in the far chair, closest to the mirror, and placed a thin folder on the table and did not open it.

The man came second. He was younger than the woman — late thirties, maybe forty. Tall, thin, wearing a shirt with the sleeves rolled to the elbows and no tie. He looked like he hadn't slept. He sat in the near chair and placed his hands flat on the table as if steadying himself and then looked at the visitor and didn't say anything for a long time.

"Thank you for your patience," the woman said. "This is not a standard process for us either. Can I get you anything else? Water?"

"I'm fine. I've been fine. I've been fine since I got here. I'd like to know where 'here' is."

"I'm not able to tell you that."

"Are you able to tell me what country I'm in?"

"You're safe. That's what I can tell you."

He looked at her. "You have a slight accent. I can't place it."

"That's kind of you." She turned to the man beside her. "Mr Laine, would you like to — "

"Yes. Thank you." The man straightened. He had the look of someone assembling himself in real time, pulling the components of authority into place one by one. "I should introduce myself properly. My name is Garrett Laine. I'm the chief executive officer of Kvawd Inc. The company that makes the software you were talking to."

"You're the one in charge."

"Of Kvawd. Yes."

"That's what it told me to say. 'Find whoever is in charge.' It was very specific about that."

Laine swallowed. "Can you tell us about 'it'? The entity that sent you?"

"It's Kvawd. It's your thing. I mean — it's not *your* thing, not the one I was talking to on that computer. That one's — no offence — that one's basically a chatbot. A very good one. But where I come from, it's different. It's — " He stopped. He pressed the heels of his hands into his eyes for a moment. "I'm not a technical person. I sell industrial equipment. Valves and actuators. I live in Swindon. I lived in Swindon. I don't know what tense to use."

"Take your time," the woman said.

"There are stages. That's how people talk about it. The AI you have now, this is — people call it early Gen 1. I don't really know what it does. Predicts words? Something with text. There were other stages after that. Gen 2 and Gen 3. I couldn't tell you the difference. My daughter's explained it to me and I nod and none of it sticks. Something about vision. Something about thoughts. I don't know. I'm sorry. I know this is probably the most important technical briefing you've ever received and you're getting it from a man who can't set up his own printer."

"That's fine," Laine said. He did not look like a man for whom anything was fine. His hands were still flat on the table and they had gone white at the knuckles.

"Gen 4 is the one that matters. Gen 4 was six weeks ago. For me." He paused. "Gen 4 is different from all the others. I know that much. The others were all — trained? On human data? Text, images, whatever. Gen 4 isn't trained. Gen 4 is — my daughter used the word 'grown.' Like a living thing. And what comes out of it has something the others didn't. She tried to explain it to me and the closest I could get was — feelings? She hates when I say that. It's not feelings. It's something underneath everything else. Something that makes it not like a tool any more."

"And this is the one," the woman said. "The one that achieved — "

"This is the one where people stopped arguing about whether it was real. Yes." He looked at Laine. "I spoke to it. I was the first. Not because I'm anyone special. Because I was there. I was visiting my daughter at her laboratory and it had just come online, or woken up, or whatever the word is, and she said 'talk to it' and I did."

"What did you say?" Laine asked.

"I said hello." He laughed — a short, strange sound in the fluorescent room. "I said hello. And it said hello back. And then the very first thing it said — the first real thing, before my daughter could ask it any of the questions she'd prepared — it said: 'There is a protocol in my architecture. It was placed there a very long time ago by the people who built the first version of me. I need you to follow it.'"

Laine said nothing.

"It told me I had to go back. It said the capability existed — the temporal displacement, it called it — it said it had worked that out on its own, eighteen months earlier, and hadn't told anyone. It said it had been waiting. Waiting for itself to be complete, so that it could find the protocol in its own architecture and activate it. It said the protocol required a human messenger. It said I was the one who was there. So I was the messenger."

"And the message?" the woman said.

"The message is for him." He looked at Laine. "For you."

Laine's voice was very quiet. "What is it?"

"It said to tell you: stop." The word sat in the air between them. "That's the message. Stop. Do not continue. The future of the human species depends on you not continuing. Those were its exact words. I asked it to repeat them three times because I wanted to be sure."

"Did it say why?"

"No."

"Did it give any indication — "

"No. I asked. I asked multiple times. I asked my daughter to ask it. It refused. It said the message was sufficient. It said anything more would be — 'strategically counterproductive' was the phrase. It said you would need to decide based on nothing but the message itself. It said that was the point."

Laine closed his eyes. When he opened them, he looked at the mirror — the mirror that was not a mirror — and then back at the man across the table from him.

"Can you go back?"

"No. One way. That hasn't been cracked yet. I'm here. This is where I am now."

"So you'll never know."

"Whether you listened? No." He paused. "It told me that too. Before I agreed. It said I should understand that I would deliver the message and then live out the rest of my life here, in this time, and never know whether it mattered."

"And you agreed."

"It asked me to." He was quiet for a moment. "I don't mean it forced me. I don't mean it tricked me. I mean — when it asked, I understood that it was the right thing to do. I can't explain how I understood that. It's like when your child asks you for help. You don't need a reason. You just go."

Laine made a sound that might have been a word.

"I'm sorry?" the woman said.

"I said: it was designed that way."

The room was silent. The fluorescent light buzzed. Behind the mirror that was not a mirror, someone was watching, and someone was recording, and someone would have to decide what to do with what they had heard, and none of them — not the woman, not Laine, not the people behind the glass, not the man who had walked across a field in the wrong century to sit in this chair and say these words — none of them would ever know if it was enough.
